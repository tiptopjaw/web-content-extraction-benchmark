{"title":"Something Big Happened in 1998","author":null,"date":null,"main_content":"Something Big Happened in 1998\n\nBy Chad Whitacre ❧ Published on December 19, 2025\n\ntl;dr It is reasonable to accept that OSI founded Open Source with the Open Source Definition.\n\nDisclosure / advertisement: I work for\nSentry.\n\n37signals recently launched a source-available software product that they choose\nto call open source, in open\ndefiance of the Open Source\nInitiative ’s Open Source\nDefinition (OSI’s OSD). Paul Sawers has a good\nroundup\nof responses (here’s my\ncontribution ).\n\nMoving a conversation from X to GitHub is almost always a good idea, so I\nopened a PR inviting 37signals\nto join Fair Source, the initiative we launched a year ago to\ncarve out a niche for the best of non-compete, source-available, eventually\nOpen Source licensing. DHH graciously hosted some healthy back and forth (most\nof it after closing the PR). Fair Source collaborator Zeke Gabrielse since\nfurthered the case on his blog. I want to\nzoom in on one particular claim that came up.\n\nThe Etymological Ploy\n\nDetractors of OSI and the Open Source Definition sometimes attempt to\nundermine them by contesting the history of the term “open source.” David,\nfor\nexample\n(emphasis mine):\n\nOSI failed to gain the open source trademark for a reason. It was already in\nwide circulation by the time they sought to institutionalize a specific\ndefinition that was different from the one derived from the words.\n\nThis is false.\n\nYes, OSI abandoned its\napplication to\ntrademark “open source.” Yes, this is an inconvenient truth for an organization\nthat understands itself to be the “ steward of the Open Source\nDefinition.” Yes, representatives of OSI have at\ntimes been clumsy (to put it mildly) in handling this truth, and that has eroded\ntrust.\n\nYes, Christine Peterson’s\nstatement,\n“I am the originator of the term ‘open source software’,” needs some\nqualification to fully square with anecdotal evidence of the term’s appearance\nprior to OSI’s founding, such as Dieter Plaetinck has\ncollected.\nThat said, while there were a handful of outlier references to “open source”\nprior to OSI, “wide circulation” there was not.\n\nA Clearer Picture\n\nLet’s look at four charts that paint a more complete picture of the history of\nthe term “open source,” and its prevalence before and after the founding of OSI\nin February of 1998. We’ll move from broad to specific datasets.\n\nBooks\n\nGoogle provides a tool to search and compare\nterms across essentially all English\nbooks. Comparing the frequency of “free software” and “open\nsource”\nshows a clear explosion in usage of the latter, coincident with the founding of\nthe Open Source Initiative. Prior to that, “open source” does not appear to\nhave been “in wide circulation.”\n\n(Both “open source” and “free software” have additional meanings unrelated to\nour subject, such as open source intelligence, and freeware. I take it that the\nnoise for both is roughly equal, so their relative frequency is still\nindicative.)\n\nMagazines\n\nIn the 1980s and 1990s, print magazines were an important venue for\ncommunication. PC Magazine, PC World, and Byte are the three most\nrelevant for us with accessible archives. Internet Archive has a partial\narchive for the first. Vintage\nApple has full archives for the\nother\ntwo.\n\nThe pattern is similar in magazines as in books: effectively nobody was\ntalking about Open Source until OSI introduced it with the Open Source\nDefinition.\n\nUsenet\n\nUsenet was an online forum that lasted\nfrom 1979 into the\n’20s.\nIt was an important venue of early Internet activity, including collaborative\nsoftware development. Internet Archive has a\nhistorical collection.\n\nI analyzed the ~53 million posts to the comp.* newsgroups from 1986 to 2013.\nI found a clear explosion in usage of\nthe term “open source” at the founding of OSI. 0.004% of posts mentioned the\nterm pre-OSI, compared with 0.686% post-OSI. That’s a 19,282% increase.\n\nThe conversation in comp.* would have been much more technical than in the\ngeneral computing magazines, let alone the Google Books corpus. If there\nis one place to expect “open source” to have been “in wide circulation,” it\nwould be here. It wasn’t, until OSI came along.\n\nThe Hecker Memo\n\nPerhaps the starkest illustration of the watershed in the etymology of “open source”\nthat occurred at OSI’s founding is Frank Hecker’s\nmemo,\nwhich we have in two versions:\n\n“ Netscape Source Code as Netscape\nProduct,” published\ninternally at Netscape on November 11, 1997\n\n“ Setting Up Shop: The Business of Open-Source\nSoftware,” reworked for\nan external audience in May, 1998\n\nWhat happened in between? The founding of OSI. In fact, it was precisely\nFrank’s memo that led Netscape to publish their source code. Frank was a sales\nengineer, offering up a (failed) hail mary against the 800 lb. gorilla,\nMicrosoft. Free software advocates seized the moment to accelerate their own\nagenda, and the Open Source movement was born.\n\n(Incidentally, Frank decided to publish the original memo for the first time\njust last year, during an email exchange he and I had while I was writing “ The\nHistorical Case for Fair\nSource.”\nKinda fun.:)\n\nIn the post-OSI version of Frank’s memo, I count 305 usages of the term “open\nsource,” where of course its meaning is bound to the then-freshly minted Open Source\nDefinition. In the pre-OSI original? Exactly one.\n\nForest and Trees\n\nYes, there’s a couple trees back there. Let’s not lose sight of the forest.\nSomething big happened in 1998. Whatever your opinion of events since, it’s\nreasonable to accept that the Open Source Initiative\nfounded the Open Source movement\nwith the Open Source Definition.\n\nWhat Now?\n\nI for one happily acknowledge a debt of awe and gratitude to the (imperfect)\nfounders of OSI and the Open Source movement. The best way I know to pay this\ndebt is forward: to contribute constructively to the future of both.\n\nI appreciate Dieter\nbrainstorming\nbetter “ways the OSI could introduce itself and its mission.” Wikipedia’s\nframing\nseems balanced: “ The Open Source Definition (OSD) is the most widely accepted\nstandard for open-source software.”\n\nI also appreciate Kyle Mitchell’s\nconclusion\n(emphasis mine):\n\n[N]o person and no organization [legally] owns the phrase “open source” as we\nknow it. […] Our right to speak the term freely, and to argue for our\nown meanings, understandings, and aspirations, isn’t impinged by anyone’s\nprivate property.\n\nThat right extends to debate about how we ought or ought not use the term,\nfor OSI partisans as well as those annoyed, bemused, and abused by them. But\nthat debate must be won by reason and suasion, not harassment and naked\nclaims to authority.\n\nThere’s a real beauty in the (unintended) placement of the term “open source”\noutside the realm of legal recourse. It fits the broader political economy of\nOpen Source: in the words of RFC\n2119, we are in the realm not\nof MUST or MAY but of SHOULD. There are deep conversations to be had here about\nOpen Source, society at large, and the role of OSI. Let’s do more of that, and\nless bad etymology."}

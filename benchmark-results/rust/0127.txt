{"title":"2020 News Stories","author":"University of South Florida","date":null,"main_content":"Skip Over Breadcrumbs and Secondary Navigation\n\n10 Tips for Success in Online Classes\n\nby Jessenia Rivera\n\nIt’s no secret that a sudden shift to online instruction may leave many students feeling\noverwhelmed about their academic success.\n\nLearning from home has its challenges, but here are some tips and strategies recommended\nby USF Instructional Technology faculty members James Hatten, PhD, and Sanghoon Park,\nPhD, to help you successfully navigate the transition to online learning. \n\nSet Yourself Up for Success from the Beginning\n\n1. Establish a productive learning environment\n\nWorking on assignments from a slouched position and streaming Netflix at the same\ntime is not ideal if you aim to be productive. Dr. Hatten, who specializes in online\nteaching and learning, recommends that students choose an area in their homes that’s\nfree from common distractions.\n\n“The couch is probably not the best place to be at,” Dr. Hatten says. “Get up and\ngo to a place in your house that you can make your workplace.” \n\n2. Set a schedule for completing and reviewing assignments\n\nWorking on three courses at the same time can cause for an individual to experience\na high level of stress, but this can be avoided if you allocate specific times to\nwork on each class. One example Dr. Hatten shared is working on one class between\nthe hours of 11 a.m. to 3 p.m. This schedule allows students to create a type of structure\noften seen with traditional, in-person classes.\n\n“My belief is that most people end up either procrastinating or getting too involved\nthat they won’t shut (their computers) off,” Dr. Hatten says. “So have a set time\nof some sort.”\n\nAlong with the time you set aside to complete assignments, it’s also suggested to\nhave a set time to look over the tasks for every one of your classes so that you can\nset up an agenda for each week. In doing this, you won’t mistakenly forget to turn\nsomething in.\n\n3. Seek virtual interactions with your peers\n\nStudying with a group of peers in the library or simply receiving on-the-spot clarifications\nfrom classmates is obviously not possible during this time. However, forming virtual\ninteractions through platforms such as GroupMe or Microsoft Teams is a feasible action\nyou could take in order to maintain that sense of collaboration and community. \n\n4. Use the ‘chunking’ strategy to section out tasks\n\nThe term “chunking” refers to taking a large task or a large amount of information\nand dividing it into smaller units. Instead of staring at a computer screen for three\nhours at a time, Dr. Hatten suggests that students “chunk” their time by following\na specific pattern.\n\n“Work on one class, figure out a task and then reward yourself at the end,” Dr. Hatten\nsays. “So, what I mean by that is: Get up, get some coffee, get a snack, go for a\nrun or just get away for a half hour. Then come back and do the next chunk.” \n\nFind Ways to Stay Motivated\n\nWhile there are various steps you can take to create a routine and maintain productivity,\nevery now and then a lack of motivation may set in and you could find it difficult\nto accomplish the task at hand. Dr. Park, whose research is focused on developing\nmotivational interventions for online learners, explains why exactly students may\nexperience this. \n\n“Online courses basically mean that you are learning separate from others,” he says.\n“The feeling that you're isolated from your peers and even your instructors—that physical\nand emotional distance, causes many motivational issues.” \n\nDr. Park encourages individuals to firstly recognize when their motivation is low\nand then determine the reason why. Below are a few strategies he advises students\nto use.\n\n5. Try to increase your interest in the work\n\nThere may be times where you go over an assignment or task that seems rather tedious.\nInstead of waving it off as the days go by, think about how you can turn the assignment\nor task into an engaging one. Using this strategy means using your imagination to\ncreatively modify the work you’ll turn in. \n\n6. Make the work you're doing online more personally significant\n\nYou may experience a lack of motivation when you feel unconnected to an assignment\nor task being given. Dr. Park encourages students who are confronted by this feeling\nto think critically about how that assignment could be useful in helping them in the\nfuture. \n\n“You have to find a way to connect the task to what you’re interested in already,”\nDr. Park said. “If you’re in the graduate level, you may want to consider using those\ncompleted assignments or tasks for the conference presentations.”\n\n7. Imagine yourself on a path toward mastering the subject \n\nThis kind of talk is executed when you think out loud about the goals you’re trying\nto accomplish. The conversation with yourself can usually begin like this: What will\nI achieve by completing this assignment? \n\nDr. Park shares an example on how answering this question can produce a continuous\nresponse that starts with the credit you earn for the assignment, then the grade your\nreceive from taking the course and finally ending with landing the dream job you’ll\nhave after graduation. \n\n“Thinking like this is what leads you to say: This is not something I must do, this\nis something I need to do to achieve my goals,” Dr. Park says.\n\nKeep a Positive Frame of Mind\n\n8. Problem-Solve on Your Own\n\nAs you adjust to working online, it’s important to remember that most questions can\nbe answered when you read instructions carefully and when you go over each module\nproperly. Though professors are prepared to answer your questions, instead of sending\nmultiple emails throughout the day for each problem you’re facing, it may be more\nproductive to first try to resolve the issue yourself through a Google search.\n\n9. Focus on Your Self-Care\n\nIt’s completely understandable if you need to step away from the computer for a few\nhours or if you need to take a day to sleep-in. Taking the time to recover is necessary\nand you shouldn’t feel bad about it.\n\n10. Have Compassion for Others\n\nRemember that individuals everywhere are going through many of the same experiences\nyou are right now. Have patience towards those who might not know how to set up a\nvideo chat or who may be taking a longer time to adjust to this \"new normal.\"\n\nTo assist USF students with the academic and wellness support needed during this unprecedented\ntime, the university has prepared a toolkit of information and links to helpful resources\non all campuses.

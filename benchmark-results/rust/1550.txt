And it can be expensive. As a matter of fact, in our post on video production, we estimated that it costs between $1,000 and $100,000 to create a video.  \n\nTo avoid overspending, it’s important to have clear terms outlined before, during, or after production.\n\nThat’s where a video review strategy comes into play. \n\nIt’ll help identify problems the client might have so they’re dealt with on time.\n\nYou can think of a video review strategy as a fail-safe system that comes into play if something goes wrong or even to prevent it from happening. \n\nHaving one will help you get \n\nA clear overview of the project: You’ll understand what matters most to the client and how to approach it without compromising quality. \n\nA comprehensive video review strategy gets every stakeholder involved, so you’ll have fresh pairs of eyes providing different perspectives on the video. \n\nAll of these ensure you have a professional end product. \n\nConsolidated feedback: A review system ensures video production teams don’t have to deal with conflicting feedback from multiple stakeholders. \n\nInstead, one person, preferably the project manager, collates and filters every piece of feedback, so the production team has a streamlined workflow. \n\nThe secret to a good video? Reviews and feedback. Let’s discuss the different stages of video reviewing in the next section.\n\nVideo review process: 5 stages of video reviewing\n\nYou know how video production is split into different phases? Each of those phases typically includes its own review process.\n\nThere are various stages during production where you need to pause and request revisions before you proceed. \n\nLet’s look at them in detail.\n\nStage #1: Scripting\n\nThe video review process begins here. \n\nThat’s because scripts define the outcome of the end product.\n\nThe script is the blueprint for a video, so you want to align what’s written with your client’s vision.\n\nYou also need to ensure that the style suits the client’s brand and can grab their audience’s attention instantly.\n\nCan’t I just use the creative brief as a guide instead?\n\nWell, you can, but it’s always better to have the client review the script since they’re probably more familiar with their audience than you are. \n\nStage #2: Audio editing\n\nAudio is another vital aspect of video production, so it’s important you get clients and stakeholders involved at this stage. \n\nAccording to Audacy’s State of Audio report, audio has been known to trigger emotions, trust, and connection.\n\nSo you want to find out from reviewers if there’s a sound used in the video  that can be replaced by silence. Or if the audio evokes the emotions your clients want their audience to feel.\n\nThis kind of feedback is better experienced than described. \n\nStage #3: Rough cut\n\nYou can also get stakeholders involved after the first version of the video is ready. Here you want them to check the scenes for continuity and ensure what you’ve done so far is in sync with the initial storyline. \n\nUpon reviewing it, they can let you know if clips need to be trimmed or cut entirely or if the video communicates the right idea to the audience. \n\nInvolving them at this stage and when new versions are created is another opportunity to get their thoughts on the music, sound, rhythm, and editing. \n\nStage #4: Final cut and editing\n\nEven after you’ve implemented all of the client’s feedback on the rough cut, you still need to ask them to review it again. \n\nIt’s better to spend some more time video editing and making iterations than to begin the process again. \n\nYou might have overlooked something when implementing their feedback, or they forgot to make important suggestions. So, get stakeholders involved before you publish the video. \n\nStage #5: Launching\n\nWhen you’re ready to hit the launch button, carry everyone along to dot the i’s and cross the t’s. \n\nIt’s tempting just to go ahead and publish after the possibly lengthy back and forth on review. But it’s always great to be on the safe side. \n\nAs stated earlier, fresh pairs of eyes can provide new insights you might otherwise have overlooked.  \n\n***\n\nIt’s important to keep in mind that implementing client feedback is an ongoing process. You should request reviews throughout the entire video production process.\n\nAt the same time, there are some best practices to remember.\n\nLet’s take a look at four of them next.\n\n4 video review best practices\n\nHow you respond to reviews from clients and stakeholders matters a lot in video production. \n\nEspecially when you have to deal with multiple reviewers. 😱\n\nHere are some pointers to help you stay on track:\n\n1. Watch closely and focus on essential elements\n\nTo make sure you don’t get overwhelmed by a flood of revisions when implementing a client’s feedback, focus on these key elements: \n\nGraphics: Does the overall design or video outlook reflect the brand’s feel?\n\nMessaging: Is the script successfully delivering the intended message to the client’s audience?\n\nMood: What kind of feelings do the video and audio elements evoke?\n\nShots: Do the visuals you’ve shot match what’s been said or the messaging you’re trying to get across?\n\nBranding: Is the video project aligned with the client’s branding guidelines?\n\nPay attention to these elements during the different rounds of video reviews, so you’re clear on which feedback to prioritize.\n\n2. Consolidate feedback for editors\n\nThis applies to team leads or project managers who are intermediaries between clients and video editors. \n\nWhen you receive feedback from stakeholders, it’s easy to forward everything as it comes and let the editors figure it out. \n\nThey are then left trying to understand which comments should be prioritized.\n\nHere’s what you can do for them instead: \n\nGo through the reviews \n\nCategorize them in a sequential order \n\nIdentify    contradictory feedback and reach out to stakeholders to figure out a way forward \n\nEliminate vague comments \n\nThis way, you can present consolidated feedback to editors with clear instructions they can act on. \n\n3. Have everyone on board from the start\n\nAfter getting the creative brief from clients, video production teams must wait until the rough cut is ready before including stakeholders, right?\n\nWrong! \n\nWhile that might seem like the logical thing to do, it’s reasonable to involve them in the production process early on. \n\nThis way, you’ll avoid starting all over again if the stakeholders have different ideas for the project. \n\nIt’ll also help you stick to the budget if everyone involved shares their ideas before, during, and after the production. \n\n4. Set a realistic timeline and schedule\n\nWhen working on the video project or reviews, don’t underestimate how long it’ll take to complete it. Set realistic due dates and inform clients about them. \n\nPreferably, you want to set a longer timeline to have enough time to implement the feedback.

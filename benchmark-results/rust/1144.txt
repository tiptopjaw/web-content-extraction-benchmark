While not as costly as enterprise-class providers, their focus on high-quality development and specialized expertise still requires significant investment.\n\nMid-Market Custom Software Development Companies\n\nMid-market consultancies provide the best balance between cost and quality, making them a strong choice for small-to-medium businesses (SMBs) and startups looking for scalable, high-performance software solutions.\nProject Size: $75,000 to $2 million\n2025 Software Development Hourly Rates: $120 to $250 per hour\nStrengths: They balance cost efficiency with technical expertise, making them a great fit for mid-sized businesses. Many mid-market firms also provide custom AI implementations, mobile applications, and SaaS solutions, making them a flexible option for businesses in growth mode.\n\nSmall-Class Custom Software Development Companies\n\nSmall-class firms operate lean, often consisting of boutique agencies or specialized development teams that cater to startups, local businesses, and mid-sized regional firms.\nProject Size: $20,000 to $500,000\n2025 Software Development Hourly Rates: $90 to $160 per hour\nExpertise: Many specialize in niche areas such as mobile development, UX/UI design, or specific programming languages. However, due to their smaller size, they may have limited bandwidth, so businesses should verify availability before engaging with a Small-Class provider.\n\nFreelance Software Developers\n\nIf you have a small project that can be handled by an individual and doesn’t require a team effort to complete, hiring a freelance software developer can be an acceptable choice.\n\nFreelancers tend to come in two flavors: the new developer that recently graduated from a development boot camp and the seasoned pro who used to work a 9-to-5 but got tired of the rat race and decided to strike out on their own. ‍\n\nNewbies\n\nNewbie software development freelancers are very excited and passionate about their newly acquired skill and are willing to accept any job at nearly any hourly rate.\n\nThe best of them can sometimes be quite skilled for their experience level. However, they often don’t know what they don’t know and will overestimate their abilities, causing them to over-promise and under-deliver.\n\nYou can expect to pay $50 to $75 an hour for an inexperienced software freelancer for projects ranging in size from $1,000 to $50,000. ‍\n\nSeasoned Pros\n\nThe seasoned pro has been developing software for a long time, knows half a dozen programming languages, and has worked on dozens or even hundreds of custom software projects.\n\nThey might be willing to work on your project (if you’re lucky) and will usually charge a premium for their time, with hourly rates typically between $100 to $300 for projects ranging in size from $5,000 to $100,000.‍\n\nPros of hiring a freelance software engineer\nYou can sometimes save money.\nThere are thousands of freelancers to choose from on websites like Upwork.\n\nCons of hiring a freelance software engineer\nThey are usually only really good at one thing — front-end development, back-end development, design, and so on. But for your project to be a success, you’ll likely need them to be good at everything.\nThey have a tendency to get bored and drop projects. Finding someone to finish their half-built project will be challenging.\nIt’s risky since there’s no recourse. Sure, you signed a contract, and you could sue them if things go south. It is unlikely that they are insured, and they may or may not have the ability to pay if you win.\nThey can disappear. Nearshore software development tends to be slightly more expensive, with rates ranging from $44 to $82 an hour. Project size tends to range from $10,000 to more than $1 million for both.\n\nOffshore development can be cost effective but also very risky if not properly managed. Many offshore software development projects fail, often for the following reasons:‍\n\nCommunication\n\nThe most important aspect of software development projects isn’t programming, it's communication.\n\nClients almost always underestimate how hard it is to properly communicate their vision to the development team.\n\nEven with clients in the same time zone, or in the same city, communication can be challenging.\n\nSo you can imagine how difficult it is when the designer and developers are on the other side of the world, in opposite time zones, and speak different languages.‍\n\nCode Quality\n\nMany offshore shops have hundreds of developers and focus on cranking out a high volume of low-cost projects, using older technologies that produce subpar software applications.\n\nThe unfortunate result is that many offshore projects never launch — and if they do, they are often buggy, outdated, and don’t function properly.‍\n\nTeam Size\n\nA common tactic employed by many offshore software development companies is to quote very low hourly rates, but over-staff the project with redundant, unnecessary resources.\n\nMost projects require only a part-time project manager and a part-time QA professional, and no business consultant. But many offshore companies will staff five full time employees regardless of what’s really required, just to get the total project cost up. For example, a common staffing plan for a medium-sized project will often include:\nFront-end developer\nBack-end developer\nProject manager\nBusiness consultant\nQuality assurance professional\n\nThe result is that the true hourly rate, in terms of productive hours actually worked, is much higher than quoted.‍\n\nTime Zones & Schedules\n\nOffshore development projects often go over budget and miss deadlines, in part due to the inefficiency of communicating across time zones. Day-to-day problems that could be resolved in a matter of minutes by a team in the same time zone, can take days when working across time zones.\n\nFor instance, let’s say a problem pops up, you notify your offshore developers, and go home for the night. The offshore team needs clarification of the problem, responds while you’re asleep, and they head home while you head into the office. You respond to their message and wait 12 hours for their response. This process continues, and a problem that should have taken a few minutes to define and fix has now taken days. Multiply this dynamic across the hundreds of problems that arise on most development projects, and it’s easy to see how delays are common when working with offshore teams.\n\nAt FullStack, we regularly have clients approach us looking for help fixing their app that was developed offshore.\n\nAs a courtesy, we’ll usually review the codebase and offer our opinion.

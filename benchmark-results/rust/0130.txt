{"title":"Tips for Online Success","author":null,"date":"2023-10-17T21:24:34.612134874+00:00","main_content":"The big difference in an online course is that students can do the work via the internet\nany time of day and, typically, any day of the week. The instructor can usually be\nreached by Canvas discussion, e-mail, and/or instant messaging.\n\nLearning online requires new strategies:\n\nPlan your time.\nYou should plan to spend at least the same amount of time you would spend for a face-to-face\nclass. For a 3 unit class, that would be about 9 hours per week (3 in-class hours\nplus 6 homework hours).\n\nFamiliarize yourself with the course’s online delivery system (e.g., Canvas).\nClick on the links in your course and learn where everything is. Find the course syllabus,\nrequirements, methods of communication, where you go for help, etc.\n\nKeep your contact information current.\nMake sure you have your current email address in Self Service so you can be contacted\nwhen necessary.\n\nParticipate!\nWhether you are working alone, or in a group, contribute your ideas, perspective and\ncomments on the subject you are studying, and read about those of your classmates.\n\nGet the support of your colleagues, family and friends before you start your online\ncourse. This built-in support system will help you when you have to sit at your computer\nwhile others around you are ready to relax.\n\nMake sure you have a private space where you can study.\nIt helps to have a quiet space of your own where you can shut the door and have books\nor papers easily available.\n\nLog on to your course often.\n….or a minimum of 3-4 days a week. If you let too many days go by without logging\non to your course discussion group, you will get behind and find it very difficult\nto catch up.\n\nTake time to be thorough.\nTake the time to think your ideas through and compose a response before posting your\ncomments to your class. It is important to adhere to correct spelling and grammar\nrules. Avoid typing in all capital letters (considered to be shouting), and avoid\nabbreviations and informal language (I'll C U L8R).\n\nBe polite and respectful.\nRemember, you are communicating with real people who have feelings, sensitivities,\nand opinions. Exhibit the same professionalism as you would in the workplace to create\na productive and supportive environment in your online class. Before hitting the Send\nor Submit button, ask yourself, \"Would I say this to the person face-to-face?\"\n\nSpeak up if you are having problems.\nWhen you enrolled in an online course you indicated that you could work in a self-directed\nenvironment. That doesn’t mean you are alone. First, look around the course to try\nand find the answers to your questions. You can also post or send questions to your\nclassmates or help them out. Finally, you may contact your instructor with any unanswered\nquestions.\n\nApply what you learn.\nIf it is possible, take the things you learn in your online course today and use them\nin your workplace or in other classes tomorrow. Also, try to make connections between\nwhat you are learning and what you do or will do in your job.\n\n(Copyright: Parts of this paper are reproduced from the Illinois Online Network (ION).\nCopyrights are owned by the ION and the Board of Trustees of the University of Illinois\nexcept in cases where the original creator retains copyright of the material.)"}

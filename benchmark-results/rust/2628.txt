Unlike the\nFPGA, the final clock speed wouldn’t be known until run time. It might be\nthat the design started at a slower clock speed, only to later speed up to\nthe full rate at run time. Unlike an FPGA which can be fixed later, there’s\nreally no room for failure in ASIC\nwork. At least\nwith an FPGA, if my board didn’t support a particular frequency, I could just\nrebuild the design for the clock frequency it did support. This particular\ndesign was built for\nASIC environments, and so it depended upon an analog component generating all\nthe clocks I needed. This worked great, up until someone wanted to purchase\nthe design to work on an FPGA, then another wanted it to work on an FPGA, and\nanother and so on.\n\nSDR\nDDR\n\nJust to add another twist to the problem, many protocols require data\ntransitions on both edges of the clock, a protocol often known as\n“Dual Data Rate” (DDR). Unlike the other designs above, these often require a\nclock that is 90 degrees offset from the data–so that each clock transition\ntakes place in the middle of each data valid window, rather than on the edges\nof the window. This sort of “offset” clock is necessary to guarantee setup and\nhold times within the slave peripheral. However, you can turn the clock off for better power performance.\nYes, there are rules … but we won’t get into those here today.\n\nI2S\n\nWe discussed generating an I2S clock at a totally arbitrary\nfrequency some time ago.\n\nI2C\n\nIn general, I2C is too slow to be the focus of this article. The techniques we discuss today\nmight work well for I3C masters, but I’m not nearly as familiar with those.\n\nSPI – not just NOR flash\n\nWhile SPI slaves have a device clock as well, handling these clocks is\nfundamentally different from what I’m describing today. My focus today\nwill be on generating clock signals for the purpose of controlling\nexternal devices–such as an SPI master might need to do.\n\nSpecifically, today I want to look at and discuss generating a clock with one\nor more of the following characteristics:\n\nOutput Signal: We’re talking about interface clocks–those generated by\nthe “master” of the interface. These are digital signals, output from\neither an FPGA (or ASIC) device.\n\nThe output may be accomplished via a component like an\nODDR or an OSERDES,\nwith or without an additional analog delay following.\n\nDiscontinuous: The clock may be discontinuous. Many protocols\n( flash,\nSDIO/eMMC, etc) allow or even require,\nthe clock to be stopped, or otherwise only toggled when there’s something to\nsend or receive. As mentioned above, stopping the clock may also be useful\nfor pausing a transmission in progress before a source buffer runs dry, or an\nincoming buffer overflows.\n\nDynamic Frequency: Often, the outgoing clock needs to change frequency\nduring operation as part of the protocol. For example, the SDIO protocol\nneeds to start at 400kHz, and then increase to 25MHz (or more). Therefore,\na good clock generator will need to be able to naturally generate multiple\nclock frequencies as the protocol requires.\n\nMinimum pulse width: Switching between frequencies must be done by rule:\nclock glitches must be fully disallowed and guaranteed against. Too-short\nclock pulses cannot be allowed. Clock high and low durations must always be\nat least a half period of the fastest allowable clock.\n\n90 Degree Offset for DDR Signaling: As shown in Fig 3, many modern\nprotocols require both positive and negative edge signaling (DDR). This\ndrops the required clock frequency by 2x, reducing the bandwidth that must\nbe carried over the PCB for the same data rate. However, the clock signal\nrequired to support such DDR signaling often needs to be delayed 90 degrees\nfrom the data, so that it transitions in the middle of the data valid period.\n\nFaster than the controller’s clock: Just to make matters worse, in my\neMMC design, I needed to generate a 200MHz\nDDR device clock from a 100MHz system clock.\n\nAll this is to say that our goal today will be to create a divided clock using\ndigital, rather than analog, logic. We’ll need to transition on the negative edge to do this.\n\nThis approach leaves us with two problems. The first is that we’re using our\nclock as a logic signal when we assign dev_clk to possible be the same as\nour source clock. The second problem is that we are transitioning user logic\non this clock. Worse, though, we’re now transitioning our user logic on both\nedges of the clock. Worse, this new clock is not the same\nas the original src_clk –even when they are at the same frequency. This can create additional delays through what otherwise\nmight be high speed logic.\n\nLikewise, the positive and negative edges of the same clock are also\n(technically) separate clock domains. Moving between them is “possible, but\nnot recommended.”\n\nGating\n\nYou may have noticed we haven’t properly gated our clock above. Sure, we\nused an active_clock signal to provide gating, but this signal does not\nguarantee the maximum frequency of the output clock. This, however, is a\nminor problem that most engineers reading this blog would be able to easily\nfix with a little bit of additional logic.\n\nTwo problems in particular, though, become deal breakers when it comes to this\ntype of design. The first is that DDR interfaces often require a clock delayed\nby 90 degrees from the data, as shown in Fig. 3 above. The simple approach\nwill not generate such a 90 degree delay. While one might use an analog delay\nelement, such as a Xilinx ODELAY element, to delay the clock signal by an\nappropriate amount, this will only work for high speed clocks and not for\nclocks less than 50MHz or so. The second problem is, what do you do when you\nneed a device clock that’s faster than your src_clk, like I did in my\nSDIO/eMMC controller design?\n\nAs a result, we really need another approach.\n\nThe Solution\n\nThe basic solution is to return to the\nrules, and so\navoid all transitions on the device clock edge at all. Instead, we’ll continue\nto transition on our source clock and then use either an\nODDR or an OSERDES to generate\nthe final outgoing clock. In the meantime, we’ll treat the newly generated\ndevice clock as a traditional logic signal–rather than a “clock” within our\ndesign. That is, we’ll let it be and remain logic.\n\nLet’s start by looking at Fig. 3 above, and dividing the clock period into\nsections, as shown in Fig. 4 below.\n\nFig 4. Dividing the clock period\n\nNominally, we’d want at least two sections per clock–one for each piece of\ndata in a DDR transmission. Sadly, this isn’t enough, since the clock might\nneed to be offset by 90 degrees. Hence, we’ll need to break each clock\nperiod into four logically distinct time periods. We can label these time\nperiods 3:0, from left-most or most-significant being 3 down to the right most\nand least significant being 0.\n\nFrom here, we can generate what I’m going to call a wide clock, four bits at\na time. This wide clock will then be output via a 4:1 OSERDES–if it is to keep\npace with the source clock within our design. At its\nfastest speed, this clock will be either 0011 (where the MSB ‘0’ is\ntransmitted “first”), or 0110 if a 90 degree offset clock is required for\nDDR transmissions (as shown in Fig. 4). At its next slowest speed, the clock\nwould be 0000 followed by 1111, or 0011 followed by 1100. Further\nclock divisions will use wide clocks of 0000 or 1111.\n\nIf you wish to use an ODDR\ninstead of a 4:1 OSERDES, you can still use this approach, save that you\nwould be generating 2 wide clock bits at a time instead of four. The fastest\nclock would be a repeating 01, but this fastest clock would be unable to\nhandle the 90 degree offsets of a DDR signal. The next fastest would be\neither 00 followed by 11, or the 90 degree offset version of the same at\n01 followed by 10.\n\nIf you want a clock running at twice your system frequency, you could use\nan eight-bit wide clock signal, designed to feed an 8:1 SERDES. Your fastest\nclock would become 00110011 (non–DDR) or 01100110 when working with DDR\nsignals.\n\nThat’s the first step–the wide clock.\n\nThe second step is to generate, together with the wide clock signal, two\nother signals. The first signal, let’s call this new_edge, will indicate\nthat a new clock cycle is beginning. The second, which I shall call the\nhalf_edge, will indicate that the second half of a clock cycle is beginning.\nBoth of these signals are also shown in Fig. 4 above, each indicating the\nportion of the clock cycle they represent.\n\nAll three of these logic signals can be now generated by a “clock generator”\nmodule.\n\nIf necessary, this clock can be stopped either at the clock generator, or\ngated further down the signal pipeline by simply zeroing out the wide clock.\n\nLet’s pause for a moment to illustrate what a “clock” like this might look\nlike.\n\nWe’ll start with the highest speed clock, running at the source clock rate.\nThis clock will have a wide clock of 0011, and new data on every clock edge.\n\nFig 5. DDR at a quarter of the system clock rate\n\nWhen using this type of “wide” clock, user logic becomes simplified as well.\nThis “simplified” user logic is easily illustrated with an example. For this\nexample, let’s suppose we wished to control 8 data wires using this type of\ndivided clock signaling. Let’s also assume, for the purposes of this\nillustration, that the source arrives via an AXI stream interface with signals\nS_VALID and S_DATA[15:0], and a ready signal given by S_READY.\n\nWe’ll start with the wide_clock, new_edge, and half_edge signals from\nthe clock generator. Note that, as we propagate these signals through our\npipeline (below), we won’t send the wide_clock straight to the output pad,\nbut instead we’ll use it along side our data processing pipeline. This way,\nif the pipeline must stall (and it might need to), the pipeline can also stall\nthe outgoing clock at the same time.\n\nHence, we’ll create a one clock delayed version of this wide_clock that\nwe can call outgoing_clock. Further, a second signal, active_clock,\ncan be used to keep track of whether or not we’ve committed to the current\nclock cycle.\n\nBefore we can get to the data, we need another key signal as well. This is\nthe second_edge signal that we used above. Here’s why: our data is going to\narrive, 16b at a time via AXI stream. If we are in DDR mode, then we’ll\nconsume 8b on each edge of this clock–and possibly all 16b at once. However,\nif we are only in SDR mode, then we’ll need to consume the second 8b on the\nnext clock edge. Hence, we’re going to need a signal that I’m calling,\nsecond_edge, to tell us that we have 8b remaining of the 16b committed to us\nthat didn’t get sent on the last clock tick.\n\nThat leads us to the outgoing_data. This is a 16 bit data signal, consisting\nof 8b, outgoing_data[15:8], which will be output on the first half of the\nclock, and another 8b, outgoing_data[7:0], which will be output on the second\nhalf of the clock. A third signal, next_byte, will be used for keeping track\nof the second byte of data in the case where we don’t output both bytes in the\nsame clock period.\n\nThe final signal we need to define is the S_READY signal. In this example,\nwe can accept new data on any new clock edge, unless we have 8b remaining\nfrom the last clock edge that have yet to be output.\n\nThis approach provides us with a couple big advantages to our user logic over\nwhat we had before.\n\nFirst and foremost, all of our user logic now takes place on the same\nsrc_clk.\nWe didn’t need any CDCs.\nAXI slave data, generated externally on this src_clk can now be used within\nour design on the same clock it was generated on.\n\nSecond, did you notice how we were able to simply gate the\nclock when there was no\ndata available? If not, go back up and look again at the active_clock signal.\n\nThird, unlike the previous approach, we’ve now guaranteed that this clock\nsignal won’t glitch. That is, assuming the outgoing OSERDES won’t generate\nglitches from our glitchless data signals. The previous clock generator,\non the other hand, could well have had glitches between the clock and the\ndata enabling it.\n\nAlso look at how easy it was to do pipelined processing. The clock was\ngenerated prior to our pipeline, and simply propagated through the pipeline.\nAlthough this pipeline only contains a single clock cycle, we could’ve easily\nextended the pipeline for multiple clock cycles if necessary by simply passing\nthe wide_clock, new_edge, and half_edge signals through the\npipeline–adjusting them if and where necessary along the way.\n\nAs a result of this example, all IO pins can now be driven using a 4:1\nOSERDES. (You could also use\nODDR s for the data, if you\ntrusted them to have the same timing relationship as the OSERDES.)\n\nWhat about frequency changes, or adjusting between the unshifted clock and\nthe clock shifted by 90 degrees? What about when the clock is off, and needs\nto be turned on? All of these challenges and more now reside within the clock\ngenerator.\n\nThe Clock Generator\n\nFor discussion purposes, let’s take a look at the\nclock generator\nI used for my SDIO/eMMC controller. As\nmentioned above, this\nclock generator\nhas the particular requirement of being able to generate two outgoing clock\nperiods per system clock cycle, but otherwise it’s a fairly straight forward\nexample of the discussion above.\n\nFrom a configuration standpoint, there are a couple of configuration options.\nFor example, I wasn’t certain that I’d always have an 8:1 SERDES available\nto me, nor do all digital environments necessarily offer 2:1\nODDR\ncomponents. Therefore, we allow those to be adjusted. Second, I want to know\nthe maximum number of bits required in my clock divider.\n\nStill, these configuration parameters are fairly straightforward.\n\nThe clock generator\nis primarily controlled via three signals. The first tells us whether we want\nour clock offset by 90 degrees for DDR outputs or not. The second controls\nthe speed of the outgoing clock. The final signal tells us we can shut the\nclock down.\n\nWhen shut down, the wide clock output will be fixed at zero, as will both the\nnew_edge and half_edge control signals.\n\nThe shutdown signal is actually really useful at slow clock speeds. Sure you\ncould shut the clock down, as we did above, by just not forwarding it through\nthe pipeline. On the other hand, once the clock has been shut down, you’d like\nto be able to restart it on a dime. The shutdown control signal to our\nclock generator\nallows us to do that. Once set, the\nclock generator\ntakes the remainder of a clock cycle to shut down, and then stays ready to\nrestart the clock at a moments notice.\n\nThe outputs from this module are just about what you would expect. You\nhave the three signals we’ve already discussed. In this case, o_ckstb\nis the new_edge signal we’ve mentioned, o_hlfclk is the\nhalf_edge signal, and o_ckwide is the wide_clock signal.\n\nThe two new signals are o_clk90 and o_ckspd. These are feedback signals\nreturned to the control module,\nused to tell us when any frequency shift or phase shift operations are complete.\n\nThese feedback signals solve an issue I was having in my eMMC\ncontroller, where the clock would\nbe at some crazy low frequency (100kHz or so), and I’d want to speed it up.\nJust setting the new clock speed wasn’t enough, since it might take a thousand\nclocks to finish a single cycle at the 100kHz clock speed. However, by\nchecking these return signals via the register set, the software driver\ncould then tell if any clock frequency change had fully taken\neffect\nbefore going on to any next operation.\n\nThe next logic block is part of a two process finite state machine. The first\nprocess, shown below, is the combinatorial process. The second will be\nthe clocked logic.\n\nPersonally, I’m not a big fan of two process state machines. I’m just not.\nThey often seem to me to be adding extra work and complexity. However,\ntwo process state machines allow me to reference logic results even before\nthe full logic path is complete. They also allow me an ability to describe\nmore complicated logic than the simple single process state machine, so\na two process state machine it is.\n\nIn this case, we are going to generate the next signal for the strobe,\nnxt_stb, the clock, nxt_clk, and the counter, nxt_counter.\n\nOf these signals, nxt_clk is the simplest to explain. This signal indicates\nthat we’re about to start a new clock cyle. In many ways, this is the\ncombinatorial version of what is to become the new_edge once latched.\n\nClock cycles themselves come in four phases, just like the four bits of the\nwide clock we discussed before. You can think of these phases as the 0110\nof the fastest clock before. The first bit, 0, is the first phase of the\nclock. Our new_edge bit, o_ckstb, will only ever be true on this phase.\nThe second bit, 1, is where the clock rises. The third bit, 1 again, is\nthe only phase where the half_edge, o_hlfck, will be set. Finally, the\nclock will return to zero in the last phase. If the clock is ever idle,\nit will idle in this first phase prior to delivering a new_edge signal.\n\nThis background will help explain how I’ve divided up the counter. There are\nNCTR bits to the counter. Of those bits, the top two control the phase\nbits we just described, whereas the others are the clock divider. The\nnxt_stb signal, mentioned above and below, is simply a signal that these top\ntwo phase-control bits are about to change.\n\nWith that as background, let’s take a look at how this works.\n\nIn general, the first step of any combinatorial block is to set all the\nvalues that will be determined within the block. This is a good practice\nto get into to avoid accidentally generating any latches.\n\nFrom here, we subtract one from the bottom (non-phase) bits of our counter\non every cycle. When these bits are zero, subtracting one will cause the\ncounter to overflow and set our nxt_stb signal, so we can know when to\nadjust the phase bits.\n\nIf our clock speed is set to 0 (wide clock of either 01100110 or 00110011 )\nor 1 (wide clock of 00111100 or 00001111 ), then we are always generating\na new clock cycle. In this case, we’ll hold the counter at zero and (roughly)\nignore the phase.\n\nLikewise, if the clock speed is equal to two, the wide clock will either\nalternate between 0000_0000 and 1111_1111, or 0000_1111 and 1111_0000,\nand so our phase will alternate, but otherwise everything else can be kept\nto zero.\n\nFinally, in the more general case, we’ll just set the bottom bits to count\ndown from ckspd-3 to zero. Yes, this is “just” a counter, but the maximum\nvalue is offset by three for the three special speeds we just discussed above.\n\nYou may have noticed that we’ve only adjusted the bottom bits of this\ncounter–the bits that count down. We’ve done nothing to update the phase\nbits at the top of this “counter”, so let’s handle those next. (Spoiler alert:\nthese MSBs don’t act like counter bits in this implementation.)\n\nOf course, for the highest frequencies, the counter will never change. It\nsits at zero, with a permanent next phase of 3.\n\nWhen the speed setting is 2, we allow the top two bits to toggle back and\nforth. If nxt_clk is set, we need to reset these bits only.\n\nFinally, for the general case, we return the phase to zero and reset the\nclock.\n\nThis is only the first half of this “two process” FSM. The second half,\nwith respect to the counter, is just about as simple. Perhaps it is even more\nso, given that we’ve done all of the hard work above.\n\nThe big thing to notice here is the nxt_clk && i_cfg_shutdown. Remember, if\nthe user ever asserts i_cfg_shutdown, we need to wait for clock cycle to\ncomplete before shutting it down. Hence, we wait for the nxt_clk signal\nbefore acting. Then, once set, we leave the counter in a state where it\nwill perpetually set nxt_clk. This way, the moment i_cfg_shutdown is\nreleased, we’ll be back to generating a clock again.\n\nTo explain this a bit better, imagine the clock generator is producing\nan output clock from ten periods of the source/system clock: five system clocks\nof 0000_000, followed by five more clocks of 1111_1111. Imagine\nagain that we’ve had several periods of these 10 clock cycles before the\nuser asserts the clock shutdown signal. We then wait another 10 cycles for the\nclock to fully shut down. Now, if the user drops the shutdown signal after a\nfurther 3 cycles, we could either wait another 7 cycles (to complete the 10),\nor start immediately. Here, we try to arrange to start a stopped clock\nimmediately without violating any of our clocking rules.\n\nThe next signal, clk90, controls whether or not we’re generating an\nclock offset from new_edge, o_ckstb, by 90 degrees or not.\n\nThis logic isn’t very interesting yet, since we’ve basically split a two\nprocess FSM. It will become more so when we get to w_clk90, and the first\nprocess of the FSM, below. The key is, this logic must determine what the\ncurrent 90 degree offset setting is. Hence, when you look at the outgoing\nwide clock, this signal must match it.\n\nHow about the clock speed? In this case, we go through some error checking.\n\nThe error checking is here to guarantee that a clock speed of 0 is only used\nwhen OPT_SERDES is set. Likewise, a clock speed of 1 may be used in\nODDR\nmode (wide clock of 00001111 ), but not when the clk90 configuration\nis set (calling for a wide clock of 0011_1100 which is too complex for an\nODDR output module to produce).\nThis continues for a clock speed of two which is fine for a non-offset clock\n(wide clock of 0000_0000 followed by 1111_1111 ), but not for an offset\nclock (wide clock of 0000_1111 followed by 1111_0000 unless the OPT_DDR\noption is set.\n\nFinally, the two values w_clk90 and w_clkspd are used to tell us what\nvalues our registered logic should use when generating a clock. As such,\nthey are either the registered values, or (when we’re about to start a new\ncycle) the new values.\n\nWith all this as background, we can now dig into the core of this\nlogic–generating the three key signals we will be outputting.\n\nOn reset, these signals will simply be set to indicate a clock of the\nfastest rate, ready to go, but otherewise one that is idle ( o_ckwide=0 ).\n\nNext, if we want to shutdown the clock, we can only do so on nxt_clk.\nWhen shutdown, the wide clock will be zero and the new edge signals willl\nall be suppressed.\n\nAs mentioned above, the key here is that the clock can suddenly start if\nthe i_cfg_shutdown signal is released. Using this logic, it does not need\nto remain phase coherent with whatever phase the clock had prior to being\nshutdown.\n\nMoving on to our highest speed clock, we simply set that according to\nthe 90 degree clock configuration. In general, this speed will only\never generate one of two values: 01100110 or 00110011.\n\nWhen running from a 100MHz system ( src_clk ) clock, this plus the OSERDES\nwill generates a 200MHz clock signal to the external device.\n\nOne might argue that the OPT_SERDES here is really redundant. There should\nbe enough logic elsewhere to keep w_ckspd at a non-zero value if OPT_SERDES\nis not set. Why use it?\n\nIt’s here specifically to provide a strong hint to the synthesis tool\nregarding logic that can be cleaned up if OPT_SERDES is not set. This block\nis complicated enough as it is, so adding it in should simplify our logic.\n\nThe problem with putting this value here, and generating a clock module based\nupon parameters such as OPT_SERDES and OPT_DDR, is that I now need to\nformally verify the IP under several conditions before I can know if it works.\nThis applies to simulation as well. It is now no longer sufficient to run\nthe simulation tool once when you do something like this. It must now be run\nmany times under different conditions. As an engineer, I need to be aware\nof costs like this whenever I invoke logic like this.\n\nIn this case, I wanted to support multiple types of FPGAs (and/or ASICs), and\nso this was the logic I chose.\n\nOur next speed, ckspd=1, has almost the same logic. As before, o_ckstb\nand o_hlfck are both set continually in this mode. In this case, our wide\nclock output will either be 0011_1100 or 0000_1111 depending on whether\nor not we need a 90 degree offset clock for DDR.\n\nWhen running from a 100MHz system ( src_clk ) clock, this generates a 100MHz\nclock as well.\n\nYou may note that there’s no real two-cycle output signal. This is the speed that alternates between\ntwo outputs, generating either 00001111 followed by 11110000 in the offset\nmode ( o_clk90=1 ), or simply 00000000 followed by 11111111 in the normal\nmode.\n\nWhen running from a 100MHz system clock ( src_clk above), this generates\na 50MHz output clock signal. This might be the “fastest” speed you would\nnormally think of for an integer clock “divider”. As you can see, though,\nwe’ve already generated outgoing 200MHz and 100MHz clocks above.\n\nThis brings us to the general case–a divided clock running at less than half\nour source clock rate. Here, we’ve already done all of the hard work for\nnxt_clk, so the outgoing next edge signal o_ckstb is done.\n\nThe half edge signal is determined by the counter. The lower bits must be zero,\nindicating a new phase, and the top two bits indicate the new phase will be\nthe third of four–so just entering halfway.\n\nThe wide clock is determined by the top two phase bits of the next counter.\nIt’s either equal to the most significant bit, when there’s no clock offset,\nor the exclusive OR of the top two bits when there is.\n\nThis leaves us with only one final signal: the current clock speed. In this\ncase, all the work has been done above, and nothing more need be done with it.\n\nThat’s the basic idea. In summary:\n\nThere are four phases to the outgoing clock, either 0011 or 0110.\n\nA counter generally helps us know when to transition from one phase to the\nnext.\n\nHigh speeds get special attention.\n\nData changes on the outgoing next edge signal, o_ckstb.\n\nIn DDR modes, data can also change on the outgoing o_hlfstb signal.\n\nKey features of this approach include:\n\nThere’s no need for any clock domain\ncrossings in the outgoing data\npath. All outgoing signals are handled in the source clock domain.\n\nThe clock may be gated at will, and (re)started quickly if necessary.\n\nFrequency changes are controlled, and will take place between clock periods.\n\nAlthough the clock is generated in logic, it doesn’t trigger any logic.\nThat is, nowhere in the design will anything in the outgoing logic path\ndepend upon either @(posedge dev_clk) or @(negedge dev_clk). Instead,\nall of the logic is triggered off of the o_ckstb or o_hlfstb signals\nwhile still running on the same src_clk we started from.\n\nBut … does it work?\n\nSimulation testing\n\nJust to get this clock generator off the ground, I built a quick simulation\ntest bench. In this\ncase, f_en is nothing more than a register which we are going to use in our\nformal proof. Nothing prevents you from doing this. Indeed, you are more\nthan able to write more complicated state\nmachines\nwhen generating formal properties as well.\n\nJust make sure that your new logic doesn’t make the same expresesions as the\nlogic you are verifying, or you might convince yourself something works when\nit doesn’t. Why\ndoes this work? Because you use different logic paths in your brain for\ndivision than you do for multiplication. Hence, if you make a mistake in\ndividing, you aren’t likely to make the same mistake when multiplying.\n\nThe same is true of formal methods. You can use logic in formal methods, just\nlike you do in your design, you just don’t want to use the same logic lest\nyour mind falsely convinces you its right when it isn’t.

{"title":"How I assembled my own Plasma desktop","author":null,"date":null,"main_content":"How I assembled my own Plasma desktop\n\nWait, what. Mic drop? Dedoimedo built his own distro? No, nothing of the sort. And yet. Well, let me\nintrigue you. But also, let me explain. Some time ago, I got myself a\nMacbook. This is one of those Apple Silicon systems, meaning ARM\narchitecture. I also want to use Linux on this box, through virtualization. And this instantly creates\na \"problem\", because there's a relative dearth of Linux distributions and software readily available\nand compiled for the ARM processors. For example, there's no ARM version of Kubuntu, which would be my\nfavorite option.\n\nEmulation, i.e., going from x86 to ARM is possible, but there's a major performance penalty.\nThus, I need a native build. But, \"shopping\" around, I couldn't find what I wanted. The most enticing\noption was the server build of Ubuntu. No GUI. Hm. Could this be a baseline upon which I might add a\ndesired desktop option, and enjoy Linux on this Mac? Well, let's find out, shall we?\n\nInstall server edition\n\nI downloaded the ISO, configured a virtual machine (through VirtualBox), went through a quick\ntext-based installer, and soon thereafter, my headless Ubuntu server was ready. Hit enter to login,\nprovide your credentials, and you're in a terminal window. The networking works out of the box\n(wired).\n\nInstall Plasma desktop\n\nLuckily, there's a single meta package that allows you to download the entire Plasma desktop from\nthe Ubuntu repositories, with one command. And it is also available in the ARM build archives. To\nwit:\n\nsudo apt install kde-plasma-desktop\n\nA few minutes later, the system was ready.\n\nReboot, login\n\nAfter I restarted the virtual machine, I had a login screen before me. It wasn't the nicest or most\nelegant thing, and it sure didn't use SDDM (which Plasma uses). I could have configured that manually,\nbut I didn't. I wanted to see what would happen without any excessive tinkering. Once I provided my\nusername and password, the Plasma desktop launched. A proper desktop.\n\nMaking everything work\n\nI made sure my system is configured to auto-login into a Plasma X11 session. Great. Next, I\ndownloaded and installed a bunch of KDE programs: Spectacle, Okular, Ark (for archiving), and then\nsome. You really get a lot of the usual stuff, including GIMP, LibreOffice, VLC. I installed Firefox\nboth as a snap and tar (ARM, from Mozilla), and Thunderbird from snap (no self-contained for-ARM tar\narchive, I'm afraid). Best part, you can use these in parallel, too. I also installed the likes of\nKeePass2 and KeePassXC, and they both work well. The former does show a bit tiny, but you can fix that\nwith Xpra, as I showed you a while back. Cherish while you\ncan, before Wayland takes this amazing flexibility away from you, along with who knows what else.\n\nProblems?\n\nYes, one or two. Discover wouldn't really work, because it thought the system was offline. This is\nerroneous, of course. But I had a rather poshly phrased error message before me:\n\nTotally bogus, of course. Apt works great on the command line. Networking works. But. Discover uses\nPackageKit, which checks and polls NetworkManager for connections. Now, the GUI NetworkManager didn't\nhave any listed, not even the loopback device. Something else in the system manages this functionality.\nThis is your Linux abstractization for you. Once, you had simple scripts, but then everything became\nmore and more convoluted, and here we are. The sad part is, Discover doesn't check the network itself,\nat all.\n\nThe \"solution\" that I found rummaging through the Internet is to create a fake network interface,\nwhich will then allow PackageKit to feel good about itself, and in turn, Discover will also work. The\ncommand what does it:\n\nnmcli con add type dummy con-name fake ifname fake0 ip4 1.2.3.4/24 gw4 1.2.3.1\n\nYou can use any name you like for the interface. But this is merely a trick. And not a pretty one.\nLet me show you a more elegant, proper solution, and explain why the network would-be fails and yet\nworks.\n\nBasically, Ubuntu Server uses netplan to manage its networks. The configurations are stored in\nYAML format under /etc/netplan, and then parsed and loaded into memory, visible under /run/netplan.\nThe desktop version of the operating system also uses this utility, but it also relies on the\nNetworkManager to do its job. Hence the discrepancy.\n\nThe solution is to disable the systemd component that manages the network, and then tell\nnetplan to defer the control and management of the network interfaces to NetworkManager, after\nwhich, everything ought to work nicely and automatically.\n\nFirst, with great great joy, we disable the systemd networking service:\n\nsystemctl disable systemd-networkd.service\n\nThe output of the command:\n\nRemoved \"/etc/systemd/system/multi-user.target.wants/systemd-networkd.service\".\n\nRemoved \"/etc/systemd/system/network-online.target.wants/systemd-networkd-wait-online.service\".\n\nRemoved \"/etc/systemd/system/sockets.target.wants/systemd-networkd.socket\".\n\nDisabling 'systemd-networkd.service', but its triggering units are still active:\n\nsystemd-networkd.socket\n\nNext, go to /etc/netplan, and here with sudo, create a file:\n\nsudo nano 01-network-manager-all.yaml\n\nIn this file, add the following:\n\nnetwork:\n\nversion: 2\n\nrenderer: NetworkManager\n\nThis is identical to my standard Linux machines, alright. Reboot. And now, all is well!\n\nBTW, this is Linux in a nutshell. Sure, I started with a server edition, but then, what \"inspired\"\nme to get this working was my own system configuration on a Linux laptop, a desktop edition, which ALSO\nhas netplan there, of course. And it uses NetworkManager as its renderer. Of course, the old scripts\nunder /etc/network were not cool enough, I guess. We need \"modern\" technology, like networkd and yaml\nand whatnot. Just makes the end user confused as there are two or three or maybe four separate entities\ncompeting for the same space, there are no meaningful error messages, it's one giant obscure and\nabstract pile of nerdy nonsense.\n\nI should probably write a separate tutorial on this..\n\nThe second issue, actual sound. Supposedly, everything is there, but you can't hear anything. At\nfirst, I started fiddling with alsa and pulseaudio configurations, but then, I realized we have\na yet another third audio system in Linux. Pipewire. So, I thought, maybe the problem is much\nsimpler than it seems.\n\nIndeed, VLC does not seem to have a default Pipewire audio output, but since alsa and pulseaudio\naren't fully configured on this server build, there's no sound. MPV plays correctly. I also installed\nalsa-utils, and checked the sound with\naplay. This works fine. Thus, the entire problem was pointing VLC to a\ndifferent output, once I had ALSA utilities installed. And after that, the sound was spiffy. No mega\ntinkering needed.\n\nEnd result? Jolly!\n\nA nice system. Fast, elegant, and it's got everything. Splendid desktop environment, tons of useful\nprograms, multiple browsers, encrypted containers in various forms, and I resolved a small number of\nissues that arose out of the from-server-to-desktop build. It took me a few days to get this done, with\nabout 5-6 hours of active time invested in creating my own would-be flavor. Not bad, I'd say.\n\nConclusion\n\nI have to say, I'm quite pleased with the outcome of my project. Now, I wouldn't presume to call\nthis a distro, nor would I want to actually make it as one, or release it unto the public. What I did\nis just a little bit of assembly work, although my results aren't any worse than what you may find out\nthere. The true art is, of course, in the drivers, the hardware-software integration, and philosophical\nthinking, but that's often missing in the Linux world, too. Then again, my goal wasn't to prove a\npoint, it was to show you a clever way you can create a useful system, even if the defaults aren't\nreadily available.\n\nShould you happen to have an ARM box at your disposal (including a Mac), and you do wish to play\nwith Linux, and you like the Ubuntu crop, you can do a lot of nice things with the Server image. The\nsame way I added Plasma, you can try with Gnome or Xfce. With a little bit of care, you could have a\npretty solid system. That would be all for now, folks.\n\nCheers."}

{"title":"Eating a balanced diet","author":null,"date":null,"main_content":"Eating a healthy, balanced diet is an important part of maintaining good health, and can help you feel your best.\n\nThis means eating a wide variety of foods in the right proportions, and consuming the right amount of food and drink to achieve and maintain a healthy body weight.\n\nThis page covers healthy eating advice for the general population.\n\nPeople with special dietary needs or a medical condition should ask their doctor or a registered dietitian for advice.\n\nFood groups in your diet\n\nThe Eatwell Guide shows that to have a healthy, balanced diet, people should try to:\neat at least 5 portions of a variety of fruit and vegetables every day (see 5 A Day )\nbase meals on higher fibre starchy foods like potatoes, bread, rice or pasta\nhave some dairy or dairy alternatives (such as soya drinks)\neat some beans, pulses, fish, eggs, meat and other protein\nchoose unsaturated oils and spreads, and eat them in small amounts\ndrink plenty of fluids (at least 6 to 8 glasses a day)\n\nIf you're having foods and drinks that are high in fat, salt and sugar, have these less often and in small amounts.\n\nTry to choose a variety of different foods from the 5 main food groups to get a wide range of nutrients.\n\nMost people in the UK eat and drink too many calories, too much saturated fat, sugar and salt, and not enough fruit, vegetables, oily fish or fibre.\n\nThe Eatwell Guide does not apply to children under the age of 2 because they have different nutritional needs.\n\nAfter the age of 2 years, children should move to eating the same foods as the rest of the family in the proportions shown in the Eatwell Guide.\n\nFruit and vegetables: are you getting your 5 A Day?\n\nFruit and vegetables are a good source of vitamins and minerals and fibre, and should make up just over a third of the food you eat each day.\n\nIt's recommended that you eat at least 5 portions of a variety of fruit and vegetables every day. They can be fresh, frozen, canned, dried or juiced.\n\nThere's evidence that people who eat at least 5 portions of fruit and vegetables a day have a lower risk of heart disease, stroke and some cancers.\n\nEating 5 portions is not as hard as it sounds.\n\nA portion is:\n80g of fresh, canned or frozen fruit and vegetables\n30g of dried fruit – which should be kept to mealtimes\n150ml glass of fruit juice or smoothie – but do not have more than 1 portion a day as these drinks are sugary and can damage teeth\n\nJust 1 apple, banana, pear or similar-sized fruit is 1 portion each.\n\nA slice of pineapple or melon is also 1 portion, and 3 heaped tablespoons of vegetables is another portion.\n\nAdding a tablespoon of dried fruit, such as raisins, to your morning cereal is an easy way to get 1 portion.\n\nYou could also swap your mid-morning biscuit for a banana, and add a side salad to your lunch. \n\nIn the evening, have a portion of vegetables with dinner and fresh fruit with plain, lower fat yoghurt for dessert to reach your 5 A Day. \n\nFind out more about what counts towards your 5 A Day\n\nStarchy foods in your diet\n\nStarchy foods should make up just over a third of everything you eat. This means your meals should be based on these foods.\n\nChoose wholegrain or wholemeal varieties of starchy foods, such as brown rice, wholewheat pasta, and brown, wholemeal or higher fibre white bread.\n\nThey contain more fibre, and usually more vitamins and minerals, than white varieties.\n\nPotatoes with the skins on are a great source of fibre and vitamins. For example, when having boiled potatoes or a jacket potato, eat the skin too.\n\nFind out more about starchy foods\n\nMilk and dairy foods (and alternatives)\n\nMilk and dairy foods, such as cheese and yoghurt, are good sources of protein. They also contain calcium, which helps keep your bones healthy.\n\nGo for lower fat and lower sugar products where possible.\n\nChoose semi-skimmed, 1% fat or skimmed milk, as well as lower fat hard cheeses or cottage cheese, and lower fat, lower sugar yoghurt.\n\nDairy alternatives, such as soya drinks, are also included in this food group.\n\nWhen buying alternatives, choose unsweetened, calcium-fortified versions.\n\nFind out more about milk and dairy foods\n\nBeans, pulses, fish, eggs, meat and other proteins\n\nThese foods are all good sources of protein, which is essential for the body to grow and repair itself.\n\nThey're also good sources of a range of vitamins and minerals.\n\nMeat is a good source of protein, vitamins and minerals, including iron, zinc and B vitamins. It's also one of the main sources of vitamin B12.\n\nChoose lean cuts of meat and skinless poultry whenever possible to cut down on fat. Always cook meat thoroughly.\n\nTry to eat less red and processed meat like bacon, ham and sausages.\n\nFind out more about including meat in your diet\n\nEggs and fish are also good sources of protein, and contain many vitamins and minerals. Oily fish is particularly rich in omega-3 fatty acids.\n\nAim to eat at least 2 portions of fish a week, including 1 portion of oily fish.\n\nYou can choose from fresh, frozen or canned, but remember that canned and smoked fish can often be high in salt.\n\nPulses, including beans, peas and lentils, are naturally very low in fat and high in fibre, protein, vitamins and minerals.\n\nNuts are high in fibre, and unsalted nuts make a good snack. But they do still contain high levels of fat, so eat them in moderation.\n\nOils and spreads\n\nSome fat in the diet is essential, but on average people in the UK eat too much saturated fat.\n\nIt's important to get most of your fat from unsaturated oils and spreads.\n\nSwapping to unsaturated fats can help lower cholesterol.\n\nRemember that all types of fat are high in energy and should be eaten in small amounts.\n\nFind out more about the different types of fats\n\nEat less saturated fat, sugar and salt\n\nToo much saturated fat can increase the amount of cholesterol in the blood, which increases your risk of developing heart disease.\n\nRegularly consuming foods and drinks high in sugar increases your risk of obesity and tooth decay.\n\nEating too much salt can raise your blood pressure, which increases your risk of getting heart disease or having a stroke.\n\nSee 8 tips for healthy eating to find out more about why you need to cut down on saturated fat, sugar and salt, which foods they're found in, and how to make healthier choices.\n\nFind out more about how to eat less saturated fat\n\nNeed to lose weight?\n\nMost adults in England are overweight or obese. Check whether you're a healthy weight using the BMI calculator.\n\nIf you need to lose weight, you can use the NHS weight loss plan. It's a free 12-week diet and exercise plan to help you lose weight and develop healthier habits. It has been designed to help you lose weight safely, and keep it off.\n\nInformation:\n\nSelf-refer for help with your weight\n\nIf you need help managing your weight, you might be able to refer yourself directly to services that can help you, without seeing a GP.\n\nTo find out if there are any services in your area:\nask the reception staff at your GP surgery\ncheck your GP surgery’s website\ncontact your integrated care board (ICB) – find your local ICB\nsearch online for NHS weight management services near you\n\nTake the healthy choices quiz\n\nIf you want to improve your health and wellbeing but are not sure where to start, the healthy choices quiz on the NHS Better Health website can give you personalised advice about how to live a healthier life."}

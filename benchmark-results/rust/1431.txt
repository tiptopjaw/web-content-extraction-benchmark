{"title":"Vitamins and minerals | Better Health Channel","author":null,"date":"2020-12-20T00:00:00+00:00","main_content":"About vitamins and minerals\n\nVitamins and minerals are organic compounds that our bodies use in very small amounts for a variety of metabolic processes. Basically, they keep us healthy and help our bodies to function.\n\nWe get vitamins and minerals from the foods we eat. For most of us, a healthy and varied diet (that includes all 5 food groups) is all we need to stay healthy. It is best to get vitamins and minerals from eating a variety of healthy unprocessed foods.\n\nSome vitamins and minerals can cause toxicity if consumed in large amounts.\n\nTypes of vitamins and their functions\n\nVitamins and minerals are a form of nutrient (called micronutrients) that are needed in small amounts. Although micronutrients don’t give us energy, they are involved in the metabolic processes that enable us to get energy from carbohydrates, protein and fat, which are also known as macronutrients.\n\nDifferent vitamins serve different purposes and contribute to different bodily functions. There are 13 vitamins in total and 8 of these come from the B-group of vitamins.\n\nVitamin A\n\nVitamin A is important because it:\nmakes the immune system work effectively so it can fight disease and infections\nkeeps our skin healthy\nsupports reproduction and growth\nhelps with vision.\n\nFood sources of vitamin A\n\nThere are different compounds with vitamin A activity in animal and plant foods. Plant foods can be easy to spot as they tend to have orange/yellow pigment known as beta-carotene.\n\nPlant sources include:\norange and yellow fruit and vegetables – such as carrots, red capsicum, mangoes, sweet potatoes, apricots, pumpkin and cantaloupe\nleafy green vegetables – such as spinach, peas and broccoli.\n\nAnimal sources include:\nliver\neggs\nsome fortified milk and milk products (with added vitamin A).\n\nVitamin A deficiency risks\n\nBecause of the various roles that vitamin A plays in the body, deficiency can have several health effects. These include:\nincreased risk of infections\nnight blindness and irreversible blindness (xeropthalmia)\nexcessive keratin build-up of the skin.\n\nVitamin B\n\nB-group vitamins help our bodies use the energy-yielding nutrients (such as carbohydrates, fat and protein) for fuel. Some B-group vitamins are needed to help cells to multiply by making new DNA.\n\nExcept for B-12 and folate which are stored by the liver, most B-group vitamins can’t be stored by the body. They must be consumed regularly in a healthy diet that includes a range of wholefoods (such as lean meat, fish, wholegrains, fruit, vegetables and legumes) and limits the intake of alcohol and processed foods.\n\nThe 8 types of vitamin B are:\nthiamin (B1)\nriboflavin (B2)\nniacin (B3)\npantothenic acid (B5)\npyridoxine (B6)\nbiotin (B7)\nfolate or ‘folic acid’ when included in supplements (B9)\ncyanocobalamin (B12).\n\nA person who has a poor diet for a few months may end up with B-group vitamins deficiency. For this reason, it’s important that adequate amounts of these vitamins be eaten regularly as part of a well-balanced, nutritious diet.\n\nVitamin C\n\nDietary intake of vitamin C (from food and drinks) is essential, because the human body cannot make this vitamin from other compounds. We also need to have vitamin C as a regular part of our diet because the body cannot store vitamin C for very long.\n\nVitamin C (ascorbic acid) is important for many metabolic processes, including:\nCollagen formation – collagen is used in different ways throughout the body. Its primary role is to strengthen the skin, blood vessels and bone. The body also relies on collagen to heal wounds.\nAntioxidant function – the metabolism of oxygen within the body releases molecular compounds called ‘free radicals’, which damage cell membranes. Antioxidants are substances that destroy free radicals, and vitamin C is a powerful antioxidant.\nIron absorption – the process of iron absorption is aided by vitamin C, particularly non-haem iron (found in plant foods such as beans and lentils).\nInfection fighting – the immune system, particularly cells called lymphocytes, requires vitamin C for proper functioning.\nOther roles – vitamin C is used to produce other important substances in the body such as brain chemicals (neurotransmitters).\n\nDietary sources of vitamin C\n\nAdults need about 45mg of vitamin C per day and any excess amount (above 200mg) is excreted.\n\nVitamin C is sensitive to heat, so some of its nutritional benefits can be lost during cooking. Raw foods are more beneficial as dietary sources of vitamin C. These include:\nfruit – oranges, lemons, limes, grapefruits, blackcurrants, mangoes, kiwifruits, rock melon, tomatoes and strawberries\nvegetables – particularly green vegetables (such as cabbage, capsicum, spinach, Brussels sprouts, lettuce and broccoli), cauliflower and potatoes.\n\nVitamin C deficiency and scurvy\n\nA severe lack of vitamin C can lead to scurvy. We may think of it as a disease of the past, but it does still exist. Factors or lifestyle issues that may increase your scurvy risk include:\nregularly eating unhealthy foods\ncrash dieting – especially being on diets that exclude certain food groups\nbeing malnourished due to inadequate care\nvery strict allergy diets\nhaving an eating disorder\nsmoking – smokers need more vitamin C to cope with the extra stress on their body.\n\nScurvy symptoms\n\nThe onset of symptoms of scurvy depends on how long it takes for the person to use up their limited stores of vitamin C.\n\nScurvy is usually easy to treat – symptoms are like many other mild complaints and may include:\nfatigue and generally feeling unwell\nloss of appetite\nnausea and diarrhoea\nfever\npainful joints and muscles\nsmall ‘pinpoint’ bleeding around hair follicles visible in the skin.\n\nIf you or someone you care for is at risk, please see your doctor.\n\nVitamin D\n\nVitamin D is important for strong bones, muscles and overall health. Ultraviolet (UV) radiation from the sun is necessary to produce vitamin D in the skin and is the best natural source of vitamin D.\n\nRegular physical activity also assists with the body’s production of vitamin D.\n\nThe body can only absorb small amounts of Vitamin D.\n\nSpending too much time in the sun may increase your risk of skin cancer. Remember to use daily sun protection, especially at times when UV index levels are at their highest (3 or above).\n\nFood sources of vitamin D\n\nOnly a small amount (around 5-10%) of Vitamin D is sourced from our diet. Sources include:\nfatty fish (such as salmon)\neggs\nmargarine and some milks have added vitamin D.\n\nVitamin D deficiency\n\nIt is important to achieve a good peak bone mass early in life. Vitamin D deficiency can result in a decline in bone density in adult life, increasing the risk of:\nosteoporosis\nfalls and bone fractures (especially for older people)\nrickets (in young children) – a preventable bone disease\n\nTreatment options include improved sunlight exposure, diet, exercise, vitamin and mineral supplements.\n\nIf you are concerned about vitamin D levels, see your GP. Your GP may recommend vitamin D supplements, which should be taken strictly as directed.\n\nVitamin E\n\nVitamin E is an antioxidant that helps protect your body against damage from free radicals, such as exposure to cigarette smoke or radiation. It is also important for our:\nvision\nimmune system\nskin.\n\nDietary sources of vitamin E\n\nVitamin E is best obtained from a healthy diet that contains plenty of fresh minimally processed foods. Vitamin E is also vulnerable to heat (especially cooking methods such as deep frying.\n\nDietary sources include:\nmeats (e.g. liver)\negg yolks\nleafy green vegetables – spinach, broccoli\nnuts and seeds – such as almonds, sunflower seeds, peanuts and hazelnuts\nhealthy oils – such as extra virgin, sunflower, soybean\nunprocessed cereals and wholegrains – such as wheat germ.\n\nVitamin E deficiency\n\nDeficiency is rare but can happen in people with diseases that cause fat malabsorption (like cystic fibrosis ).\n\nErythrocyte haemolysis is another deficiency – it’s seen in infants born before vitamin E is transferred to them from their mother prior to birth.\n\nVitamin K\n\nVitamin K is important for:\nhealthy bones\nblood clotting and wound healing\nnewborn babies to prevent a serious bleeding condition called haemorrhagic disease of the newborn (HDN).\n\nDietary sources of vitamin K\n\nWe get vitamin K from food and the bacteria in our gastrointestinal tract. Newborn babies are given a booster to increase their vitamin K levels because they are born without bacteria in their gastrointestinal tract. We get much of our vitamin K from our diet.\n\nFood sources include:\nleafy green vegetables – spinach and kale\nfruits – such as avocado and kiwi fruit\nsome vegetable oils – such as soybean oil.\n\nVitamin K deficiency\n\nVitamin K deficiency is unlikely except when fat is not absorbed properly or when certain medications are used. For example, antibiotics can kill the gastrointestinal bacteria that produce vitamin K.\n\nAdditionally, anticoagulant drugs (or blood thinners) may cause problems with vitamin K in the body. Check with your doctor if you have any concerns.\n\nTypes of minerals and their functions\n\nThere are hundreds of minerals – they are usually classified as either major or trace minerals.\n\nAlthough the amount you need differs between minerals, major (or macrominerals) are generally required in larger amounts. Some examples include calcium, phosphorus, potassium, sulphur, sodium, chloride, magnesium.\n\nTrace minerals (microminerals), although equally important to bodily functions are required in smaller amounts. Examples include iron, zinc, copper, manganese, and iodine selenium.\n\nSome of the important minerals to keep us healthy are listed below.\n\nCalcium\n\nCalcium is vital to keep our bones strong and healthy. If you don’t get enough calcium, your bones will eventually become weak and brittle and can lead to conditions like osteoporosis. Calcium helps:\nstrengthen bones and teeth\nregulate muscle and heart function\nblood clotting\ntransmission of nervous system messages\nenzyme function.\n\nFood sources of calcium\n\nAt different life stages, our calcium needs vary. It is better to get calcium from foods than from calcium supplements.\n\nGood sources of calcium include dairy foods like milk, yoghurt and cheese and some plant-based foods with added calcium (for example, soymilk, tofu and breakfast cereals).\n\nOther sources of calcium include almonds, bok choy, kale, parsley, broccoli and watercress.\n\nIodine\n\nIodine is essential to make thyroid hormones. These hormones control your metabolic rate (the rate your body uses energy when it is resting). They also help your brain and body grow and develop.\n\nFood sources of iodine\n\nWe only need a very small amount of iodine in our diet. Iodine is found naturally in foods such as:\ndairy products\nseafood\nseaweed (kelp)\neggs\nsome vegetables.\n\nIodine can also be found in iodised salt. All bought breads (except organic ) in Australia are fortified with iodised salt.\n\nYou are likely to be getting enough iodine through your diet. However, if you are deficient and need to take a supplement, be guided by your doctor. Too much iodine can be harmful, especially if you have an underlying thyroid disorder.\n\nIron\n\nIron is an important mineral that is involved in various bodily functions, including the transport of oxygen in the blood the provision of energy to cells. It also vital to help our immune system function effectively to fight infection.\n\nFood sources of iron\n\nIron can be found in animal and plant foods including:\nred meat and offal\nfish\npoultry\nlegumes\neggs\nbreakfast cereals with added iron.\n\nIron deficiency\n\nIron deficiency is common and can affect adults and children. Around one in 8 people do not consume enough iron to meet their needs.\n\nSome factors such as certain foods and drinks can affect how much iron your body absorbs. Also, some groups are more at risk of iron deficiency, such as babies and young children, teenage girls, women with heavy periods, vegans and vegetarians and people with chronic conditions.\n\nZinc\n\nZinc is an important mineral involved in various bodily functions – growth and development as well as immune function.\n\nZinc also helps to produce the active form of vitamin A and transports it around the body.\n\nFood sources of zinc\n\nZinc is highest in protein-rich foods but may also be found in some plant foods. Dietary sources include:\nred meat\nshellfish\npoultry\nmilk and cheese\nwhole grains\ncereals with added zinc.\n\nMagnesium\n\nMagnesium is important due to its many functions in the body – including maintaining bone health and using glucose for energy.\n\nMagnesium also supports immune function and helps regulate blood pressure and lung function.\n\nFood sources of magnesium\n\nDietary sources include:\nnuts (such as cashews)\nlegumes\ndark green vegetables\nseafood\nwhole grains\nchocolate and cocoa.\n\nPotassium\n\nPotassium is important for the nerves, muscles and heart to work properly. It also helps lower blood pressure.\n\nFood sources of potassium\n\nOur bodies are designed for a high-potassium diet, not a high-salt diet. Food processing tends to lower the potassium levels in many foods while increasing the sodium content.\n\nIt is much better to eat unprocessed foods – such as fruit, vegetables and lean meats, eggs, fish and other healthy, everyday foods.\n\nFoods high in potassium include:\nbananas and apricots\nmushrooms and spinach\nnuts and seeds.\n\nBe guided by your doctor, some people with kidney disease, or who are taking some medications, need to be careful not to get too much potassium in their diet.\n\nSodium\n\nA small amount of sodium is important for good health as it helps to maintain the correct volume of circulating blood and tissue fluids in the body.\n\nMost of us are consuming far more sodium than we need. In fact, many Australians are consuming almost double the amount required.\n\nToo much sodium can lead to high blood pressure (hypertension) and other health conditions.\n\nFood sources of sodium\n\nSalt is the main source of sodium in our diet. It is a chemical compound (electrolyte) made up of sodium and chloride.\n\nMany foods – wholegrains, meat and dairy products – naturally contain small amounts of sodium, while highly processed foods usually contain large amounts.\n\nVitamin and mineral deficiencies and supplements\n\nThe fat-soluble vitamins A, D, E and K can be locked away in the liver and body fat, and stored for a long time. This means that they can cause toxicity if consumed in large amounts. The water-soluble vitamins, including B-complex and vitamin C, are mostly only stored for a shorter period.\n\nA vitamin deficiency takes weeks or months before it will affect your health. For instance, it would take months of no vitamin C before you developed scurvy.\n\nVitamin and mineral supplements may be recommended in certain circumstances to correct vitamin and mineral deficiencies – such as folate for women who are pregnant or planning a pregnancy. Others who may be at risk of a vitamin or mineral deficiency include:\npregnant women and women who are breastfeeding\npeople who smoke, drink alcohol in excess or use illegal drugs\ncrash dieters or those on very strict diets\nthe elderly (especially those who are disabled or chronically ill)\nsome vegetarians or vegans\nwomen with heavy periods\npeople with food allergies\npeople with malabsorption problems (such as diarrhoea, coeliac disease, cystic fibrosis or pancreatitis ).\n\nRemember, supplements are a short-term measure and should only be taken on advice from your doctor or a dietitian.\n\nAn occasional lapse in good eating will not harm you, if your usual diet consists of a wide variety of fresh foods.\n\nWhere to get help"}

{"title":"Erik Demaine 's\n     Folding and Unfolding :","author":null,"date":null,"main_content":"Erik Demaine 's\nFolding and Unfolding:\n\nThe Fold-and-Cut Problem\n\nProblem\n\nWhat shapes can result from the following fold-and-cut process?\n\nTake a piece of paper.\n\nFold it flat.\n\nMake one complete straight cut.\n\nUnfold the pieces.\n\nAre all shapes possible? Refering back to the original sheet of\npaper, what patterns of cuts can be achieved by this process?\n\nThe answer turns out to be any pattern of straight cuts.\nTry it out yourself by printing one of our several examples.\n\nHistory\n\nWakoku Chiyekurabe by Kan Chu Sen, 1721.\n\nThe first published reference to folding and cutting of which we are aware is a\nJapanese book, Wakoku Chiyekurabe\n(Mathematical Contests), by Kan Chu Sen, published in 1721. This book contains\na variety of problems for testing mathematical intelligence. One of the\nproblems asks to fold a rectangular piece of paper flat and make one complete\nstraight cut, so as to make a typical Japanese crest called sangaibisi, which\ntranslates to “three folded rhombics”. The author gives a solution\nconsisting of a sequence of simple folds, each of which folds along a line.\nScanned images of the relevant pages in the book\nare available.\n\nAnother early reference to folding and cutting is a July 1873 article\n“National Standards and Emblems” in Harper's New Monthly Magazine,\nvolume 47, number 278. This article tells the story about Betsy Ross and her\nrelation to the American flag. It claims that in 1777, George Washington and a\ncommittee of the Congress showed Betsy Ross plans for a flag with thirteen\nsix-pointed stars, and asked whether she could make such a flag. She said that\nshe would be willing to try, but suggested that the stars should have five\npoints. To support her argument, she showed how easily such a star could be\nmade, by folding a sheet of paper and making one cut with scissors. The\ncommittee decided to accept her changes, and George Washington made a new\ndrawing, which Betsy Ross followed to make the first American flag.\nOne method for\nfolding and cutting a five-pointed star is described on ushistory.org's\nBetsy Ross page.\nThe truth of this story is unclear,\nbut it still makes for an early reference.\n\nFold and cut was the basis for several early magic tricks. The earliest such\nreference we know is 1833, in a book called “The Girl's Own Book”\nby Lydia Marie Child. See\nThe Public Paperfolding History Project\nfor images from this and many more references.\n\nIn particular,\nbefore Houdini was a\nfamous escape artist, he was a general magician.\nHis 1922 book Paper Magic\n(E. P. Dutton & Company, pages 176–177)\ndescribes a method for making a five-pointed star.\nAccording to David Lister,\nthis book appears to have been ghostwritten by another magician, Walter Gibson,\nbut Houdini still may have performed the trick.\n\nAnother magician, Gerald Loe, studied the fold-and-cut idea in some\ndetail. His 1955 book Paper Capers (Magic, Inc., Chicago)\ndescribes how to cut out arrangements of various geometric objects,\nsuch as a circular chain of stars.\n\nMartin Gardner wrote about the fold-and-cut problem in his famous series in\nScientific American (“Paper cutting”, chapter 5 of New\nMathematical Diversions (Revised Edition), Mathematical Association of\nAmerica, Washington, D.C., 1995.). Gardner was particularly impressed with\nLoe's ability to cut out any desired letter of the alphabet. He was also the\nfirst to state cutting out complex polygons as an open problem. What are the\nlimits of this fold-and-cut process? What polygonal shapes can be cut out?\n\nTheorem\n\nThe theorem is that every pattern (plane graph) of straight-line cuts\ncan be made by folding and one complete straight cut. Thus it is possible\nto make single polygons (possibly nonconvex), multiple disjoint polygons,\nnested polygons, adjoining polygons, and even floating line segments and\npoints.\n\nThere are two methods for solving this problem, by different sets of people.\nI call them the straight-skeleton and\ndisk-packing methods.\nYou can read descriptions of them below, or watch the\nfree\nonline video lecture describing them:\n\nStraight-Skeleton Method\n\nAnalyzing the straight-skeleton method, from\nthe book.\n\nMartin Demaine, Anna Lubiw, and I solved the fold-and-cut problem using the\n“straight skeleton”. This structure is defined (roughly) as follows. For\neach face of the desired cut pattern (region between cuts), shrink the face so\nthat the edges stay parallel and move a constant speed in a perpendicular\ndirection. Stop whenever the boundary becomes nonsimple (intersects itself),\nand continue shrinking each piece. The straight skeleton is the\ntrajectories of the vertices of the desired cut pattern during this shrinking\nprocess. Not described here are the details for vertices of degree zero and\none, which require special care.\nFor more about the straight skeleton, see\nJeff Erickson's\nstraight-skeleton page\nor\nAichholzer et al.'s\noriginal paper about the straight skeleton.\n\nThe straight skeleton consists of the majority of the creases and achieve the\ndesired “lining up” of cuts. In addition, there are perpendicular\ncreases. Basically, from each vertex of the straight skeleton, we shoot a ray\nperpendicular to each reachable cut edge, and the ray “bounces” (reflects)\nthrough any skeleton edges it meets.\n\nThe difficult part is proving that the straight-skeleton creases together with\na subset of the perpendicular creases (and a few more auxiliary creases) can be\nfolded into a flat origami. This is done by exhibiting a folded state,\nthat is, by demonstrating how the piece of paper looks when folded. This\nfolded state must satisfy the properties that each face is isometrically\npreserved and that paper does not intersect itself.\n\nThe most detailed description is the book\nGeometric Folding Algorithms:\nLinkages, Origami, Polyhedra.\nThe full paper (which includes all proof details) is still in progress. The\nlongest\ndescription includes many of the proofs and was published in the\nProceedings of the Japan Conference on Discrete and Computational\nGeometry (JCDCG'98),\nto appear as a volume in Lecture Notes in Computer Science.\nA 2-page\ndescription of the method (without proofs) appears in the\nProceedings of the 10th Annual ACM-SIAM Symposium on Discrete Algorithms\n\n(SODA'99).\n\nThis work is related to Robert Lang's\nTreeMaker,\nalthough it was initially developed in isolation. The intersection is that\nthey both solve the fold-and-cut problem when the faces (shapes to be cut out)\nare all convex. Both TreeMaker and our work extend far beyond this common\nbase, in different directions. Essentially,\nTreeMaker tries to collapse a polygon to a specified folded shape but only\naddresses convex polygons, whereas the straight-skeleton method focuses on the\nunfolded shape and addresses nonconvex and disconnected polygons.\n\nDisk-Packing Method\n\nAn example of the disk-packing method, from\nthe paper.\n\nInspired by early versions of the straight-skeleton\nmethod, Marshall Bern, David Eppstein, Barry Hayes, and I solved\nthe fold-and-cut problem using disk packing. Specifically, we place\ndisks on the piece of paper, such that\n\nthe disks do not properly overlap (but may touch),\n\nthe gaps between disks have either three or four sides,\n\nthere is a disk centered at each vertex of the desired cut pattern, and\n\nthe edges of the desired cut pattern (i.e., desired cuts) are the union\nof radii of disks.\n\nAs a result, we can decompose the desired cut pattern by adding edges between\ncenters of touching disks, and this results in a collection of triangles and\nquadrangles. We then fold each of these triangles and quadrangles using\nmolecules that line up the boundaries of the triangles and quadrangles.\nThis is the basic idea--a couple of tricks are needed to show that the\nmolecules can be joined together, and a further trick is needed so that only\nthe desired lines are the ones that come together onto a common line.\n\nThe most detailed description is the book\nGeometric Folding Algorithms:\nLinkages, Origami, Polyhedra.\nThe paper “ A\nDisk-Packing Algorithm for an Origami Magic Trick ”\nalso describes the method in detail, though it has some small issues.\nThe most recent version\nincludes some simplifications, and appears in the\nProceedings of the 3rd International Meeting of Origami Science, Math, and\nEducation ( OSME\n2001 ).\nThe original version\nwas published in the Proceedings of the International Conference\non Fun with Algorithms ( FUN'98 ).\n\nOur use of disk packing is quite different from Robert Lang's\nTreeMaker,\nalthough it is intriguing that the same method comes up in two different\ncontexts in origami mathematics.\n\nRelated\n\nTry out our new simple fold and cut font!\n\nA 2010\npaper with Martin Demaine, Andrea Hawksley, Hiro Ito, Po-Ru Loh, Shelly Manber,\nand Omari Stephens considers what happens when we restrict the folds to\na sequence of simple folds, folding along a single line at a time.\n\nCheck out our new font based on simple\nfold and cut!\n\nIvars Peterson briefly describes the fold-and-cut results in his article,\n“ Fold-and-Cut\nMagic ”,\nwhich appears in\nScience News,\nvolume 162, number 22, November 30, 2002.\n\nJoseph O'Rourke briefly describes the two fold-and-cut methods in his\n“ Computational\nGeometry Column 36 ”, which appears in\nInternational Journal of Computational Geometry and Applications,\nvolume 9, number 6, pages 615-618, 1999; and SIGACT News, volume 30,\nnumber 3, issue 112, September 1999, pages 35-38.\n\nOne application of the fold-and-cut theorem is\nthe design of the logo for CCCG 2001.\n\nSome of our earlier papers on the fold-and-cut problem are the following:\n\nThe technical report\n“ Computing\nExtreme Origami Bases ” studies the special case of cutting out\na convex polygon.\n\nIn this case, we can not only compute a folded state exists,\nbut we also provide the continuous motion of the paper through time\nthat reaches this folded state. In fact, the motion can be achieved\nwhile keeping rigid the regions between creases rigid, called\nrigid origami.\n\n“Planar Drawings of Origami Polyhedra” studies some properties of the\nfoldings of convex polygons using graph drawing.\n\nA 2-page\nsummary was published in the Proceedings of the 6th\nSymposium on Graph Drawing\n( GD'98 ).\nThe full version\nis a technical report."}

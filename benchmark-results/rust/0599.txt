{"title":"Yoga for health: MedlinePlus Medical Encyclopedia","author":null,"date":null,"main_content":"Yoga is a practice that connects the body, breath, and mind. It uses physical postures, breathing exercises, and meditation to improve overall health. Yoga was developed as a spiritual practice thousands of years ago. Today, most Westerners who do yoga do it for exercise or to reduce stress.\n\nBenefits of Yoga\n\nYoga can improve your overall fitness level and improve your posture and flexibility. It may also:\nLower your blood pressure and heart rate\nHelp you relax\nImprove your self-confidence\nReduce stress\nImprove your coordination\nImprove your concentration\nHelp you sleep better\nAid with digestion\n\nIn addition, practicing yoga may also help with the following conditions:\nAnxiety\nBack pain\nDepression\n\nWho Should use Caution\n\nYoga is generally safe for most people. But you may need to avoid some yoga poses or modify poses if you:\nAre pregnant\nHave high blood pressure\nHave glaucoma\nHave sciatica\nHave arthritis\n\nMake sure to tell your yoga instructor if you have any of these conditions or any other health problem or injury. A qualified yoga teacher should be able to help you find poses that are safe for you.\n\nStyles of Yoga\n\nThere are many different types or styles of yoga. They range from mild to intense. Some of the more popular styles of yoga are:\nAshtanga or power yoga. This type of yoga offers a more demanding workout. In these classes, you quickly move from one posture to another.\nBikram or hot yoga. You do a series of 26 poses in a room heated to 95°F to 100°F (35°C to 37.8°C). The goal is to warm and stretch the muscles, ligaments, and tendons, and to purify the body through sweat.\nHatha yoga. This is sometimes a general term for yoga. It most often includes both breathing exercises and postures.\nIntegral. A gentle type of yoga that may include breathing exercises, chanting, and meditation.\nIyengar. A style that pays great attention to the precise alignment of the body. You may also hold poses for long periods of time.\nKundalini. Emphasizes the effects of breath on the postures. The goal is to free energy in the lower body so it can move upward.\nViniyoga. This style adapts postures to each person's needs and abilities, and coordinates breath and postures.\n\nHow to get Started\n\nLook for yoga classes at your local gym, health center, or yoga studio. If you are new to yoga, start with a beginner class. Talk with the instructor before the class and tell them about any injuries or health conditions you may have.\n\nYou may want to ask about the instructor's training and experience. However, while most instructors have had some formal training, there is no objective yoga certification body. Choose an instructor you enjoy working with who does not push you in ways you are not comfortable.\n\nWhat to Expect\n\nMost yoga classes last from 45 to 90 minutes. All styles of yoga include three basic components:\nBreathing. Focusing on your breathing is an important part of yoga. Your teacher may offer instruction on breathing exercises during the class.\nPoses. Yoga poses, or postures, are a series of movements that help boost strength, flexibility, and balance. They range in difficulty from lying flat on the floor to difficult balancing poses.\nMeditation. Yoga classes usually end with a short period of meditation. This quiets the mind and helps you relax.\n\nHow to Prevent Injury\n\nWhile yoga is generally safe, you can still get hurt if you do a pose incorrectly or push yourself too far. Here are some tips to stay safe when doing yoga.\nIf you have a health condition, talk with your health care provider before starting yoga. Ask if there are any poses you should avoid.\nStart slowly and learn the basics before pushing yourself too far.\nChoose a class that is appropriate for your level. If you are not sure, ask the instructor.\nDo not push yourself beyond your comfort level. If you cannot do a pose, ask your instructor to help you modify it.\nAsk questions if you are not sure how to do a pose.\nBring along a water bottle and drink plenty of water. This is mostly important in hot yoga.\nWear clothing that allows you to move freely.\nListen to your body. If you feel pain or fatigue, stop and rest.\n\nImages\n\nReferences\n\nHecht FM.

It involves breaking down food into nutrients. Nutrients are chemical substances your body needs to function. But this essential process does much more than that. That may make you feel confident that you’re making the most of what you eat.\n\nWhat are nutrients?\n\nThere are six major classes of nutrients. Eating well is a balancing act that combines all six. The nutrients in the food you eat benefit your body in different ways.\n\nMacronutrients provide energy and keep your body working. Micronutrients are vitamins and minerals. They support your metabolism, among other functions. Water rounds out the list of essential nutrients. It supports nutrition in many ways, like carrying nutrients and oxygen to your cells.\n\nMacronutrients\n\nIf your body was a building, macronutrients would be the foundation. Macronutrients are your main source of energy. Macronutrients are:\nCarbohydrates like sugars, starches and fiber, which are the main source of energy powering your body\nLipids — which are in fats, oils, meat, dairy and other foods — do many things like storing energy and digesting fat\nProteins, which help to build and repair muscle, as well as regulate hormones\n\nAdvertisement\n\nMicronutrients\n\nMicronutrients include essential vitamins and minerals. You need fewer micronutrients than macronutrients, but they’re still very important.\n\nVitamins are substances that animals and plants make. The food you eat is your main source of most vitamins. Many people take a multivitamin to make sure they’re getting all the vitamins they need. You may recognize some vitamin names, like vitamin A, vitamin C or vitamin D. Others may not be so familiar. But they all contribute to your health. The following are essential vitamins:\nVitamin A supports your immune system, metabolism and vision.\nVitamins B1 – 12 all help your body turn food into energy. They also support your blood vessels, brain, immune system and nervous system.\nVitamin C supports your body in many ways. It helps your body grow tissue. It can also heal wounds and strengthen your immune system.\nVitamin D partners with calcium to build and maintain healthy bones. Vitamin D also supports brain health and your immune system.\nVitamin E helps to protect your cells. It also helps to maintain the health of your red blood cells, white blood cells and platelets. This vitamin also supports your brain, eyes and skin.\nVitamin K helps your blood to clot and prevents excessive bleeding.\n\nMinerals make their way into food via the animals that eat them and the plants that absorb them. Animals and plants provide minerals. These substances support your health in many ways. The following minerals are micronutrients:\nCalcium builds strong bones. It also supports your heart, muscles and nervous system.\nChromium helps your body metabolize (process) carbohydrates and fats.\nCopper supports brain development, your immune system and your nervous system.\nFluoride helps to keep your bones strong and to prevent tooth decay.\nIodine promotes brain development, thyroid health and more.\nIron helps your body make hemoglobin, a protein in red blood cells.\nMagnesium makes new proteins and helps to convert food into energy.\nManganese protects your cells from damage and helps your body make energy.\nPhosphorus builds and maintains strong bones and teeth, produces energy and repairs cells.\nPotassium helps to prevent high blood pressure. It also supports muscle health.\nSelenium supports thyroid health and reproductive health.\nSodium helps cells absorb nutrients. It helps muscle and nerve functions.\nZinc boosts your immune system and protects your vision.\n\nFood Groups\n\nThe long list of macronutrients and micronutrients come together in the five food groups that make up the balanced meals you need to have good nutrition:\n\nAdvertisement\nDairy\nFruit\nGrains\nProtein\nVegetables\n\nHow nutrition needs change\n\nGetting nutrition right is a balancing act that changes throughout your lifetime. Babies, kids, adults and older adults have very different nutritional needs. For example, breast milk or formula meets most babies’ nutritional needs until they’re six months old. But adults and older adults typically need a different combination of nutrients.\n\nNutrition for kids of all ages\n\nBreast milk or formula meets most babies’ nutritional needs for the first six months. At six months, you can start adding solid food to the mix. It can be challenging to figure out which mix of solid food gives your baby the best possible nutritional boost. Ask your baby’s pediatrician for suggestions for your baby’s introduction to solid food.\n\nThe nutritional balancing act continues as your baby grows. In general, meals that include the five food groups give toddlers and children the right combination of macronutrients and micronutrients.\n\nNutrition for adults\n\nBuilding meals from the five food groups still works for adults. But males and females benefit by having a different mix of macronutrients and micronutrients. Here are two examples:\nFemales typically need more iron than males. That’s because females lose iron through blood loss during menstruation. Pregnancy and childbirth can cause iron-deficiency anemia.\nMales need a bit more vitamin B5 than females. Vitamin B5 supports muscle mass, among other things. Males typically have more muscle mass than females.\n\nAdvertisement\n\nThe nutrient picture changes as we age. Medication or medical conditions may affect your appetite. When you eat less, you miss out on essential nutrients. People age 65 and older may benefit by focusing on foods that boost vitamin D and calcium for strong bones.\n\nMedical conditions that affect nutrition\n\nThere are medical conditions that affect nutrition regardless of your age. For example:\nCancer cachexia happens when cancer speeds up your metabolism, so your body uses more energy than you get from food\nDepression can cause you to lose interest in planning and eating nutritious meals\nDysphagia (difficulty swallowing) can make it hard for you to eat certain foods, which increases the chance you’ll miss out on getting certain nutrients\nMalabsorption syndrome refers to conditions that keep your body from absorbing nutrients\n\nConditions that affect nutrition can lead to macronutrient and micronutrient undernutrition. These are types of malnutrition. They can cause serious complications:\nMacronutrient undernutrition means you don’t get enough protein, carbohydrates or fats. Your body needs macronutrients for energy. When you don’t get enough macronutrients, your body starts conserving energy. It does that by breaking down tissue or slowing organ function.\nMicronutrient undernutrition is when you have a serious vitamin or mineral deficiency. For example, a lack of iron can lead to iron-deficiency anemia.\n\nAdvertisement\n\nCare at Cleveland Clinic\n\nAdditional Common Questions\n\nWhat’s the difference between calories and nutrients?\n\nThey’re both a way to keep track of what you eat. Calories are a unit of energy. They measure how much energy food provides your body. Nutrients are essential chemical substances your body needs to function and can have different calories (for example, lipids have more calories than carbohydrates).\n\nWho can help me with nutrition questions?\n\nA registered dietitian (RD) is probably your best source of information about nutrients. They’ll discuss your needs, explain how specific nutrients may be helpful and how to build nutrients into a meal plan.\n\nA note from Cleveland Clinic\n\nThe saying “You are what you eat” doesn’t capture all the things that make us individual and unique. But it comes close. It’s fair to say that what we eat — the nutrients we consume — supports our physical and mental health in many ways.\n\nNutrition can be complicated. There are more than a dozen macronutrients and micronutrients. You may wonder if you’ll need to sort them all to build healthy eating habits. Ask a healthcare provider for help. They’ll take time to understand your situation. Then, they’ll make recommendations to help create a nutrition plan for eating that works for you.\n\nCare at Cleveland Clinic\n\nGood nutrition is an important part of staying healthy.

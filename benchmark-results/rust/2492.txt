case-insensitive comparisons.\nIt’s more complex than just converting uppercase letters to lowercase, especially in Unicode, where some characters have multiple case variants or expand into multiple characters when case-folded.\nFor example:\nGerman: 'ß' U+00DF 0x C3 9F\nand 'ẞ' U+1E9E 0x E1 BA 9E\nboth case-fold into \"ss\" U+0073 U+0073 0x 73 73\n.\nTurkish: 'İ' U+0130 0x C4 B0\ncase-folds into \"i̇\" U+0069 U+0307 0x 69 CC 87\n- that’s a lowercase 'i' U+0069 0x 69\nplus a combining dot.\nLigatures: 'ﬃ' U+FB03 0x EF AC 83\ncase-folds into \"ffi\" U+0066 U+0066 U+0069 0x 66 66 69\n, so the original glyph contains matches for 'f' U+0066 0x 66\n, \"ff\" U+0066 U+0066 0x 66 66\n, \"fi\" U+0066 U+0069 0x 66 69\n, and \"ffi\" U+0066 U+0066 U+0069 0x 66 66 69\nqueries.\n\nFolding Invariants #\n\nUnicode 17.0 defines more than 1000 locale-agnostic mappings in CaseFolding.txt, with expansions up to 3 codepoints.\nThe obvious approach to case-insensitive search is to case-fold everything and then run memmem.\nIt’s also the slowest possible approach, and it breaks match offsets unless you keep an extra mapping layer.\n\nStringZilla takes a different route: it tries very hard to find a fold-safe window in the needle - a slice that:\nCan be case-folded into ≤16 bytes.\nDoesn’t trigger surprises for a chosen SIMD path: no shrinking expansions, no ligatures, no folding targets like Kelvin sign.\nHas enough byte diversity to be a good SIMD filter.\n\nTo do that, we split Unicode into a handful of script-ish buckets and give each its own SIMD kernel and its own alarm rules:\nASCII invariants ( 00 – 7F ): the cheapest kernel; most English letters, digits, and punctuation.\nWestern European (mostly 2-byte UTF-8): Latin-1 Supplement + Latin Extended-A, covering German/French/Spanish/Portuguese.\nCentral European (mostly 2-byte UTF-8): Latin Extended-B and friends, covering Polish/Czech/Hungarian/Romanian.\nCyrillic ( D0 / D1 lead bytes): Russian/Ukrainian/Bulgarian and other Slavic languages.\nGreek ( CE / CF lead bytes): Greek and Coptic.\nArmenian ( D4 lead byte): Armenian.\nVietnamese (many 3-byte sequences): Latin extensions with diacritics and tone marks.\n\nMost of those kernels are designed around 1- and 2-byte UTF-8 sequences, which already cover the majority of European languages.\nVietnamese is the odd one out: it pulls in a lot of 3-byte Latin extensions with diacritics.\n\nAll of them not only differ in the folding kernels used on the hot path, but also in their alarm logic that triggers a slow-path verifier.\nThose alarms exist for two reasons:\nSome characters fold into targets you really don’t want to “just allow” in an ASCII fast path.\nExample: 'K' U+212A 0x E2 84 AA\nfolds into 'k' U+006B 0x 6B\n.\nSome folds shrink or expand in ways that break “same length, same offsets” assumptions.\nExample: 'ſ' U+017F 0x C5 BF\nfolds into 's' U+0073 0x 73\n.\n\nThis is why some ASCII letters are “unsafe” depending on where they appear.\nNot because they are rare, but because Unicode expansions can generate them.\nHere are a few that matter in practice:\n“k” is a folding target (Kelvin sign), so you can’t just treat it as a boring ASCII byte.\n“s” is a folding target (long s), and 'ß' U+00DF 0x C3 9F\nfolds into \"ss\" U+0073 U+0073 0x 73 73\n.\n“f” and “i” participate in ligatures like 'ﬁ' U+FB01 0x EF AC 81\n→ \"fi\" U+0066 U+0069 0x 66 69\n.\n\nIf you’d like to stare at the full ban logic, grep for sz_utf8_case_rune_safety_profile_ in the source.\nIt’s not poetry, but it’s deterministic.\n\nSafe Window Selection #\n\nMost needles aren’t fully “safe”.\nSo instead of folding the entire needle (slow, offset-hostile), we extract a window that is safe to SIMD-scan and use it as a pivot.\nThe needle splits into three pieces: needle (bytes): [ head ][ safe window ][ tail ]\nneedle (folded): [.... ][ <=16 bytes ][.... ]\nSIMD scans only: [ <=16 bytes ]\n\nThen every SIMD hit goes through a verifier that checks the head and tail around it.\nThe “why” is easiest to see on a tiny example.\nThe needle \"faßade\" contains 'ß' U+00DF 0x C3 9F\n, which is toxic for an ASCII fast path, but it also contains \"ade\": needle chars: f a ß a d e\nneedle bytes: 66 61 C3 9F 61 64 65\nfolded bytes: 66 61 73 73 61 64 65 (ß → ss)\nsafe window: 61 64 65 (\"ade\")\n\nSo we SIMD-search \"ade\" and only then do the extra legwork for the \"faß\" prefix (and possibly tail).\nTo pick optimal safe windows, we follow this algorithm:\nWalk possible start positions in the needle, stepping by UTF-8 characters - never mid-character.\nFor each start, fold forward and build per-script windows until you hit 16 bytes or an alarm.\nScore candidates by byte diversity to minimize false positives on the hot SIMD path.\nPick the cheapest kernel that is both safe and applicable.\n\nIf the same window is safe for multiple kernels (e.g., pure ASCII), we pick the cheapest one so we don’t pay the Vietnamese folding tax for \"xyz\".\n\nWhy We Probe Last Bytes #\n\nThis ties back to the UTF-8 primer: the continuation bytes ( 10xxxxxx ) carry 6 payload bits each, while the leading bytes mostly encode the range.\nIn many scripts, that means the first byte is boring and the last byte carries the entropy.\nTake Cyrillic as an example: А Б В... (Cyrillic alphabet)\nD0 90, D0 91, D0 92... (leading byte repeats)\n^^ ^^ ^^ (last byte differentiates)\n\nIf you probe the last bytes of UTF-8 characters, you get a much better SIMD filter: fewer false positives, less verifier work.\nSo for windows with ≥4 UTF-8 characters we aim probes at the last byte of the 2nd and 3rd characters, plus the first and last bytes of the whole window.\nFor very short windows probes overlap - that’s expected.\n\nSerial Fallbacks: Danger Zones, Rings, and Hashes #\n\nNot every platform supports SIMD, let alone AVX-512.\nSome needles are too short, some scripts are too annoying, and sometimes an alarm goes off in the middle of a hot loop.\nStringZilla uses a few serial fallbacks that are still Unicode-correct:\nFor needles that fold into 1/2/3 runes, we use a hash-free scan over the folded rune stream.\nFor longer needles, we use a Rabin-Karp style rolling hash over folded runes with a small ring buffer, and verify on collisions.\nFor “danger zones” detected by SIMD alarms, we scan for a cheap 1-rune candidate and validate the full match with the same head/tail verifier.\n\nThat’s pretty much the core idea of StringZilla v4.5.\nLet’s look at the numbers.\n\nPerformance Benchmarks Optimizations #\n\nEvery script family has its own folding kernel and “alarm”.\nThe fold rewrites bytes in place so probes can reuse the same verifier, while the alarm spots ligatures or shrinking expansions that require a slow-path correction.\nThe “naive” approach is to check equality against each byte value, then prefix-AND masks of consecutive 1-, 2-, or 3-byte sequences, and finally OR all danger masks together.\n\nIn the Unicode 17 folding tables we target, multi-codepoint fold expansions never produce 4-byte UTF-8 sequences, so the expansion slow-path can safely ignore them.\n\nThat, however, introduces a remarkable amount of port pressure on x86 CPUs.\nThe VPCMPB K, ZMM, ZMM instruction takes:\n3 cycles on port 5 on Ice Lake.\n5 cycles on ports 0 or 1 on AMD Zen 4.\n\nShifting the produced masks between K registers and ALUs and expanding them back to ZMMs for further processing is similarly expensive.\nSo next to every “naive” baseline kernel for AVX-512 - I wrote “efficient” versions using harder-to-trace logic, but comparing every streamed-through buffer against the “naive” baseline in debug builds to ensure correctness.\n\nEquality Comparisons Port Pressure #\n\nThe Western/Central alarms originally hammered port 5 with 12+ equality checks every 64 bytes.\nBy compressing entire ranges into a single subtraction + comparison and deriving the actual member with VPTESTNMB, we freed the port for real work.\n\nCentral Europe performs the same trick for the C3 – C5 block: one range check plus two ternary tests produce the ‘K’ / ‘ß’ / ‘İ’ / ‘ſ’ masks without congesting the execution unit.\n\nTernary Logic and Blends #\n\nThe ASCII fast path leans on ternary logic.\nFor ≤3-byte windows we broadcast the first/middle/last byte, XOR the haystack at three offsets, merge via VPTERNLOG (imm8 0xFE ), and let VPTESTNMB turn zero lanes into positions.\nNo extra window replay is needed because all bytes are covered.\n\nFor ≥4-byte windows we add a fourth probe plus a cached 16-byte window.\nOnce the probes line up we replay the window via _mm_maskz_loadu_epi8 and only then call the shared verifier.\nThe Greek folding kernel benefits from ternary logic even more: it builds five independent offset vectors (e.g., +0x20, -0x20, +0x26, +0x25, -1 ) and collapses them with two chained VPTERNLOG s before a single _mm512_add_epi8.\nThat removes the eight-step mask-move chain we previously had and keeps port 5 from saturating.\n\nByte-Table Shuffles #\n\nGreek and Cyrillic folds use VPSHUFB lookups instead of branchy ranges.\nWe mask off the high nibble of each continuation byte, shuffle a 16-entry table, and add the resulting offset back to the register—three disjoint subranges handled in one go.\n\nFor Greek we encode per-range offsets in a single LUT so that one shuffle handles the entire CE lead byte:\n+0x26 for 'Ά' U+0386 0x CE 86\nand friends\n+0x25 for 'Έ' U+0388 0x CE 88\n, 'Ή' U+0389 0x CE 89\n, 'Ί' U+038A 0x CE 8A\n+0x20 for 'Α' U+0391 0x CE 91\n–\n'Ο' U+039F 0x CE 9F\n-0x20 for 'Π' U+03A0 0x CE A0\n–\n'Ω' U+03A9 0x CE A9\nand dialytika cases\n\nCyrillic is even tidier: every uppercase lives in D0 80 – D0 AF, and every lowercase twin sits either +0x10, +0x20, or -0x20 away in the same byte lane.\nMapping the high nibble of the 2nd UTF-8 byte ( 8, 9, A ) into those offsets lets a single VPSHUFB drive the entire block, while a post-shuffle mask flips D0 → D1 whenever the nibble was 8 or A.\n\nUsing StringZilla #\n\nAt this point you must be itching to try it out.\nStringZilla is available under the Apache 2.0

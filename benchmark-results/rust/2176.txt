{"title":"Electrical Safety Fact Sheet | Environmental Safety, Sustainability and Risk","author":null,"date":null,"main_content":"Home\nWho We Are\nElectrical Safety Fact Sheet\n\nIntroduction\n\nElectrical safety refers to the control measures and practices implemented to prevent electric\nshock, electrocution, and other electrical-related incidents. These incidents can be caused by\nexposure to electrical current, electrical arcs or sparks, overheating, or contact with hot\nsurfaces or materials.\n\nTo ensure electrical safety, it is important to follow the guidelines, laws and regulations set\nforth by local, state, and national authorities, as well as to implement recommended industry\nbest practices. Some important electrical safety control measures and practice include:\n \nAvoid contact with electrical sources: Avoid contact with electrical outlets, wires,\ntransformers, circuit breakers, and the like with and keep conductive objects away from\nelectrical sources.\nProper use of electrical tools and equipment: Use only tools and equipment and tools\nthat are properly grounded and in good condition and follow manufacturer instructions\nand safety precautions.\nProper installation and maintenance: Ensure that electrical wiring, outlets, tools, and\nequipment are installed and maintained by qualified professionals, and regularly inspect\nthem for damage or wear.\nElectrical safety tools and equipment: Wear personal protective equipment, such as\ngloves, goggles, hardhats, suits, and/or safety shoes, when working with electrical\nequipment or in areas where there is a risk of electrical shock.\nEducation and training: Educate yourself and others on electrical safety practices and\nprocedures and seek training from qualified professionals on how to properly work with\nelectrical equipment.\n\nBy following these guidelines and implementing recommended industry best practices, you can\nhelp prevent electrical incidents and ensure the safety of yourself and others around you.\n\nElectrical safety laws and regulations are established by national, state, and local authorities to\nensure that electrical equipment, installations, and practices meet specific safety standards.\nThese laws and regulations may vary, but they generally cover the following areas:\n \nElectrical installation standards: Standards for the installation and use of electrical\nequipment, wiring, and appliances. This includes requirements for proper grounding,\nelectrical panel protection, and use of electrical conduit and connectors.\nElectrical tools and equipment standards: Standards for the design, construction, and\nuse of electrical equipment and appliances. These standards may cover grounding,\nelectrical insulation, current capacity, and other safety features.\nElectrical inspection and testing: Periodic inspections and testing of electrical\ninstallations and equipment to ensure that they meet safety standards and are in good\nworking order.\nElectrical work practices: Safe work practices and procedures for those working with or\naround electrical equipment, including requirements for personal protective equipment,\nlockout/tagout procedures, and proper use of tools and equipment.\n\nSome common electrical safety laws and regulations include Occupational Safety and Health\nAdministration (OSHA), the National Electrical Code (NEC), the National Fire Protection\nAssociation (NFPA) and state and local electrical codes. These are specified in the sections\nbelow.\n\nThe electrical safety laws and regulations require you to implement specific controls. Moreover,\nthere are recommended industry practices to help prevent electrical incidents and ensure the\nsafety of those working with or around electrical equipment.\n\nNFPA 70E, Standard for Electrical Safety in the Workplace, is a key resource for helping UMD\nreduce exposure to risks and avoid occupational injuries and fatalities. It was created to provide\na document that meets the needs of Occupational Safety and Health Administration (OSHA)\nelectrical safety requirements and is entirely consistent with the National Electrical Code (NEC)\nand other applicable publications.\n\nYou will find opportunities for NFPA 70e Two Day training in this important requirement. You can also contact ESSR OSH at osh@umd.edu for further information.\n\nApplicable University Policy\n\nVI-14.00(A) UMD Policy on Control of Hazardous Energy During Maintenance of Equipment (Lockout/Tagout Program)\n\nApplicable Regulations\n\nAll electrical wiring and equipment must comply with the following:\nNational Electric Code (NEC)\nNFPA 70E\n29 CFR 1910- The Control of Hazardous Energy (Lockout/Tagout)\n29 CFR 1910.151- Special Industries (High Voltage Electricity)\n29 CFR 1910.331 -Scope\n29 CFR 1910.332 -Training\n29 CFR 1910.333 -Selection and use of work practices\n29 CFR 1910.334 -Use of equipment\n29 CFR 1910.335 -Safeguards for personnel protection\n\nSummary of Requirements\nThe requirements of electrical safety are specified by OSHA and specific work practices are detailed in NFPA 70 E as well as University policy.\nWork performed must be done by a licensed electrician and in accordance with the National Electric Code.\nThe use of ground fault circuit interrupters (GFCIs) as a device to protect a person from electric shock when he or she simultaneously contacts a \"live\" wire or part and a grounded object.\nOSHA has promulgated specifications which affect high voltage distribution systems within the university that need to be considered in the installation of new equipment.\nUse of personal protective equipment when employees are working in areas where there are potential electrical hazards shall be provided with, and shall use, electrical protective equipment that is appropriate for the specific parts of the body to be protected and for the work to be performed.\n\nTraining\n\nScope: The training requirements apply to employees who face a risk of electrical shock that is not reduced to a safe level by the electrical installation requirements of 29 CFR 1910.303 through 1910.308.\n\nEmployees in occupations facing a higher than normal risk of electrical accident are required to be trained. Additionally, other employees who may reasonably be expected to face comparable risk of injury due to electric shock or other electrical hazards must also be trained.\n\nContent: Employees shall be trained in, and become familiar with, the safety-related work practices required by 1910.331 through 1910.335 but that pertain to their respective job assignments.\n\nInspections\nAll power tools and appliances must be free of cracks, fraying, heat damage and insulation damage.\nAny electrical control, disconnect switches, and transformers must not be blocked in a manner that will impede access to the devices. The regulations require a minimum of three feet of clearance.\nAll equipment must have grounded three-prong plugs.\nLive parts to which an employee may be exposed shall be deenergized before the employee works on or near them, unless the employer can demonstrate that deenergizing introduces additional or increased hazards or is infeasible due to equipment design or operational limitations. Live parts that operate at less than 50 volts to ground need not be deenergized if there will be no increased exposure to electrical burns or to explosion due to electric arcs.\n\nWritten 5/98\nRevised 4/05"}

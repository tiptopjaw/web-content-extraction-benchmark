{"title":"The Average Cost of Therapy in America by State","author":"Justin Pritchard","date":null,"main_content":"When considering the cost of therapy—you might think a session is most expensive in California or New York.\n\nThink again.\n\nOur deep dive into SimplePractice exclusive data* reveals therapy pricing patterns that may defy conventional wisdom.\n\nAs therapists build their private practices and client lists, they may feel uncomfortable as they grapple with a key question—how much should they charge for therapy? And, what data points can they consider when setting their rates? \n\nClinicians navigate a profound ethical tension—balancing the commitment to accessible care that drew many to the profession against the financial realities of running a sustainable practice.\n\nThis balancing act plays out in every rate-setting decision.\n\nTo move beyond anecdotal evidence, we ran a comprehensive analysis of nearly 105 million therapy sessions (both self-pay and insurance) conducted in 2023-2024 using SimplePractice data on CPT codes for psychotherapy. \n\nThis unprecedented dataset from nearly 204,000 behavioral health therapists located in all 50 states and the District of Columbia, reveals the true cost of therapy in America today.\n\nThe rising cost of therapy\n\nAccording to SimplePractice internal data, the cost of therapy has risen almost 13% over the past five years.\n\nDownload our free 24-page white paper to learn more about the rising cost of therapy in America—as well as to find out expert insights on how insurance plays into the average rates.\n\nIn 2019, therapists charged an average of $123 for therapy sessions (both private pay and insurance).\n\nThe data reveals a consistent pattern of cost acceleration, with therapy rates climbing approximately 4% annually since 2021, outpacing general inflation during several years and suggesting structural factors driving mental health care costs upward.\n\nIn 2024, the average cost of therapy has risen to roughly $139—a $16 increase.\n\nFurthermore, in 2017, SimplePractice published research showing that the nationwide median rate for therapy that year was $120.\n\nTaking this additional data into account demonstrates a continual increase in the cost of therapy over the past seven years.\n\nUnderstanding your market\n\nThe market rate, also known as the going rate for a therapy session, refers to the range of rates which vary by city and state.\n\nWhile this information is just one of many data points therapists consider when setting their rates, it’s arguably one of the most important.\n\nIf a therapist sets their rate too high, they may not be delivering the access to care that drew them to behavioral health in the first place.\n\nHowever, if a therapist sets their rate too low, perhaps they will be perceived as offering less value to their clients.\n\nAnd insurance reimbursement rates and therapy costs can even significantly vary between cities and towns in the same states that are just miles away from each other.\n\nThat said, the U.S. states with the highest and lowest average cost of therapy may surprise you.\n\nStates with the highest costs of therapy\n\nIn 2023-2024, the average fee per therapy session varied by state and region—from as low as $122, to as high as $227, according to SimplePractice data. \n\nThe cost disparity is particularly evident at the top of our list, where two states stand out with average cost of therapy sessions exceeding $200.\n\n1. North Dakota: $227\n\n2. Alaska: $212\n\n3. South Dakota: $192\n\n4. Washington D.C.: $189\n\n5. Arkansas: $184\n\n6. Oregon: $182\n\n7. Nebraska: $180\n\n8. Mississippi: $179\n\n9. Minnesota: $176\n\n10. New York: $176\n\nMental health access gaps—and their impact on the cost of therapy\n\nThe geographic distribution of therapy costs challenges simplistic cost-of-living explanations.\n\nWhile metropolitan hubs with higher costs of living—like Washington D.C., New York City, and Portland, OR—contribute to their states ranking among those with the highest rates for therapy sessions, the presence of rural states like North and South Dakota at the very top of our rankings reveals a more complex reality: the critical variable appears to be provider scarcity, rather than regional affluence.\n\nWhen you consider the lower number of mental health clinicians practicing in North Dakota, Alaska, South Dakota, Arkansas, and Nebraska compared to the number of state residents, it becomes clear that there are fewer mental health providers per capita in many of those states.\n\nFor example, South Dakota reported only 150 clinical and counseling psychologists in 2023, and North Dakota reported 160, according to the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics. Based on their current populations, South Dakota has approximately one psychologist for every 6,130 citizens and North Dakota has approximately one psychologist per 4,900 citizens.\n\nIn comparison, the state of New York reported 7,290 clinical and counseling psychologists—which is one psychologist per 2,690 citizens.\n\nOur findings illuminate a classic economic principle in action: in states where mental health professionals are scarce—with some regions showing one therapist per 5,000+ citizens—session rates climb substantially, creating therapy deserts where care exists, but only at premium prices.\n\n‌\n\nStates with the lowest costs of therapy\n\nBased on SimplePractice data, in 2023-2024, only six U.S. states had rates of therapy under $130 per session: Oklahoma, Tennessee, Vermont, South Carolina, Louisiana, and Missouri.\n\nThe 10 states with the lowest costs of therapy include:\n\n1.

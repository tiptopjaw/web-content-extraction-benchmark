{"title":"Vacation Policy Best Practices and Types for Employers","author":null,"date":"2017-08-23T06:39:45.720+00:00","main_content":"Learn how to create a vacation policy that aligns with business goals and employee needs. Explore types, benefits, legal considerations, and best practices.\n\n6 min read\n\nupdated on May 21, 2025\n\nKey Takeaways\n\nA vacation policy outlines how employees can take time off and how much paid leave they are entitled to.\n\nCommon policy types include fixed PTO, accrual-based systems, and unlimited vacation policies.\n\nA clear, written policy improves communication, avoids confusion, and enhances morale.\n\nEmployers may offer additional types of leave like floating holidays, sabbaticals, or paid volunteer time.\n\nBest practices include compliance with labor laws, equitable tracking, and maintaining productivity.\n\nTools like vacation request templates and scheduling software streamline PTO management.\n\nWhat Is a Vacation Policy?\n\nA vacation policy is a unique guideline defined by each employer that outlines and sets forth the requirements for requesting vacation, as well as the allotted days that are given to employees. Employers should clearly define vacation policies in a consistent manner and apply the same set of guidelines to each employee. An easy-to-comprehend, well-enforced vacation policy avoids confusion and helps employees comprehend what steps they must follow in order to effectively schedule their time off.\n\nWritten Vacation Policy Advantages\n\nWritten vacation policies are the best and most concise way to communicate the requirements for taking vacation time. The policies should clearly outline what the employee's responsibilities are for their time spent away from work, such as if they need to find another employee to perform their duties while they are gone, or if they need to schedule their vacation time in a certain way or during specific times of the year.\n\nBest Ways to Manage Vacation Requests\n\nMany companies use a rotating system for making vacation requests because it eventually gives all employees the opportunity to choose their preferred vacation times. In a rotating system, certain employees get to choose their vacation days first and that list of people with the first choice changes every year.\n\nAnother popular option is to just close the business for a week or two so there is no chance of favoritism affecting who gets to choose the most coveted times of the year to take vacations.\n\nVacation Request Process and Tools\n\nA streamlined request process is essential to avoid scheduling conflicts and ensure adequate staffing. Key steps in a clear vacation request system include:\n\nSubmission Requirements: Employees should submit vacation requests in writing, specifying dates and backup coverage if needed.\n\nAdvance Notice: Set expectations for how far in advance employees should make requests (e.g., two weeks).\n\nManager Approval: Outline who approves the request and under what criteria (e.g., order of submission, departmental needs).\n\nDocumentation: Use a vacation request form or software to track all approved and pending requests.\n\nConflict Resolution: Establish tie-breaking methods for overlapping requests (e.g., seniority, first-come-first-served).\n\nUsing HR management tools or PTO tracking software can automate approval workflows and maintain a central record.\n\nVacation Policy Options\n\nThe three most common options in  establishing vacation policies  are:\n\nEach employee receives a fixed number of vacation days at the beginning of each work year that they must use in that year.\n\nEach employee receives a certain number of vacation days each year and unused days roll over to the next year.\n\nAll employees have unlimited vacation days.\n\nPolicies for Accrual of Vacation Time\n\nAccrual policies for vacation time are at the discretion of the employer and have wide variations. A policy may be that vacation time accrues all at once on the anniversary date of the employee's hire. An alternate policy would be that a prescribed number of vacation hours are added per pay period.\n\nAccrual of vacation time may vary for different types of employees, and this should be clearly stated in the employee handbook or other work-related documents. For instance, full-time employees might accrue three vacation hours per pay period, and part-time employees might accumulate only two vacation hours for each pay period.\n\nEmployers should also put in writing if unused vacation pay will or will not be paid when an employee's job ends either by choice or termination.\n\nLegal Compliance and Considerations\n\nWhen designing a vacation policy, employers must consider both federal and state laws that may affect time-off practices. While the Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA) does not require paid vacation, certain regulations may still apply:\n\nState Laws: Some states, such as California, prohibit use-it-or-lose-it policies and require payout of unused vacation upon termination.\n\nCarryover Rules: Specify whether unused vacation rolls over annually or is capped.\n\nAnti-Discrimination: Ensure that vacation policies are applied consistently and do not inadvertently discriminate based on age, disability, or family status.\n\nWritten Acknowledgment: To mitigate disputes, employees should sign and acknowledge understanding of the vacation policy.\n\nLegal consultation is advised when drafting a new policy or updating an existing one to ensure full compliance with labor laws.\n\nHow to Establish a Workplace Vacation Policy\n\nWhile time off for vacation is not federally imposed, under the Family Medical Leave Act (FMLA), a labor law passed in 1993, employers are lawfully obligated to provide specific employees with up to 12 weeks of unpaid, job-protected leave each year.\n\nVacation time is just one form of paid time off (PTO), and a company should choose its vacation policies based on company goals as well as what employees as a whole prefer.\n\nBefore finalizing the rules for vacations, it's best to ask for employee opinions on what they hope to gain from the policy. Next, provide them with the proper information about vacation policy options, including benefits and drawbacks to potential plans so they can make a knowledgeable decision.\n\nAnother factor to consider when establishing a vacation policy is that if the company has key employees, separate policies may be beneficial to keep those employees contented for the sustenance and growth of the enterprise.\n\nVacation Policy Examples by Type\n\nDepending on company culture and workforce needs, vacation policies can take different forms:\n\nFixed PTO Policy: Employees receive a set number of vacation days annually, often based on length of service.\n\nAccrual-Based PTO: Employees earn vacation time gradually (e.g., 1.25 days per month).\n\nUnlimited PTO: No set number of days; employees take time off as needed with managerial approval.\n\nFlexible PTO: A hybrid system combining vacation, sick days, and personal time into a single bank of hours.\n\nExample:\n\nAccrual-Based Policy: “Employees accrue 1.25 vacation days for each month of full-time work. Accrued vacation may carry over up to 5 days into the next calendar year.”\n\nTailoring your policy type to your workforce structure improves employee satisfaction and operational efficiency.\n\nUnlimited Vacation Time\n\nUnlimited vacation has its supporters as reports flourish that Americans work too much and ignore the benefits of downtime. Others counter that the policy would be widely abused, and company productivity would suffer.\n\nAdvocates of unlimited vacation time cite three benefits of the plan:\n\nOffering unlimited vacation time indicates that a company acknowledges that its employees have obligations and interests outside of work that can't always be scheduled during non-working hours.\n\nUnlimited vacation policies express trust, putting the responsibility of completing jobs and projects in a timely manner on employees instead of managers or HR directors.\n\nUnlimited vacation treats employees as responsible adult individuals and builds morale.\n\nAdditional Types of Paid Time Off (PTO) to Consider\n\nIn addition to traditional vacation days, many companies are expanding their vacation policy offerings to include other forms of paid leave to support employee well-being and work-life balance. These include:\n\nFloating Holidays: Allow employees to take paid time off for personal or cultural holidays that aren’t part of the standard calendar.\n\nSabbaticals: Extended leave offered after a specific tenure (e.g., five years), used for travel, education, or personal development.\n\nVolunteer Time Off (VTO): Paid time to participate in community service or volunteer initiatives.\n\nMental Health Days: Designated paid leave to support emotional wellness.\n\nComp Time (Compensatory Time): Extra time off provided instead of overtime pay, commonly used in public sector jobs.\n\nClearly defining these benefits within your vacation policy ensures transparency and helps employees utilize them effectively.\n\nFrequently Asked Questions\n\nWhat should a vacation policy include?\nA vacation policy should define eligibility, accrual or allotment methods, request procedures, rollover rules, and any state-specific compliance requirements.\n\nIs unlimited PTO a good idea for small businesses?\nIt depends on your culture and trust level. While it offers flexibility, clear guidelines are essential to prevent abuse and ensure coverage.\n\nCan employers refuse vacation requests?\nYes, if the timing interferes with business needs or staffing levels. However, refusal should be consistent and documented to avoid claims of unfair treatment.\n\nDo employers have to pay out unused vacation time?\nThis varies by state law. For example, California generally requires payout of unused vacation at termination, while others do not.\n\nHow can I ensure my vacation policy is legally compliant?\nConsult state labor laws, document the policy clearly in writing, and consider seeking legal advice to ensure alignment with current regulations.\n\nIf you need help with a vacation policy, you can post your legal need on UpCounsel’s marketplace.

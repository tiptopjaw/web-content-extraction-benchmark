As rates of mental illness continue to rise year over year, the shortcomings of traditional treatments for mental illness, have led the public and practitioners to explore new technologies and medicines in search of more effective care solutions. \n\nHere are some of the most exciting mental health trends and developments in mental health care that you might hear about here in 2024:\n\n1. Artificial Intelligence in Clinical Care\n\nProgress in artificial intelligence (AI) could significantly enhance the future of therapy sessions and the accuracy of mental health diagnoses. Research from December 2021 has shown that AI motion sensors are capable of identifying anxiety symptoms, which include behaviors like:\nBiting nails\nCracking knuckles\nTapping hands\n\nMoreover, a study from October 2021, regarded as a reliable source, indicates that AI can be instrumental in training therapists. This technology can assess their competencies, including their ability to create a conducive environment for their clients.\n\nDespite these advancements, experts believe that AI will not replace traditional mental health services provided by human professionals, even as its role in mental health training and treatment is expected to grow in the coming years.\n\n2. Psychedelics as Medicine\n\nAround the world, psychedelics have been used for religious and medicinal purposes for centuries, predominantly among Indigenous cultures. \n\nNow, in 2024, we’ve finally entered a new era: of the traditional medical establishment, largely acknowledging the benefits of psychedelics, based on a growing body of research suggesting that drugs such as MDMA, Ketamine, Psilocybin and LSD, can help treat mental health conditions like depression, PTSD, anxiety disorders, addiction or substance use disorder, and more.  \n\nDuring this “tipping point”, we’re now witnessing the legal and regulatory systems adapt and change in order to make these treatments available to more people. \n\nIn order of most available and common, these psychedelics you may soon have access to or hear about, include: ketamine, MDMA, Psilocybin and LSD. \n\nOnly ketamine - a dissociative - is currently available nationwide (via doctors and therapists specializing in treatment with it) to the public. The others, however, are being made available in research studies, and State-level pilot projects, for assessing the effectiveness of utilizing these psychedelics in the mental health treatment of anxiety, depression, addiction, and other mental illnesses. \n\n3. Trauma-Informed Care\n\nApproximately 64% of adults in the United States have experienced at least one traumatic event in their lifetimes. \n\nIn response to this reality, mental health practitioners are embracing Trauma-Informed Care (TIC) as an approach in the field of mental health and social services that recognizes the widespread impact of trauma on individuals' lives. \n\nThis approach understands that traumatic experiences can have long-lasting effects on a person's mental, emotional, and physical well-being. TIC shifts the focus from asking \"What's wrong with you?\" to \"What happened to you?\", acknowledging that past trauma can influence current behavior and health. \n\nThis paradigm emphasizes the importance of understanding the signs and symptoms of trauma in clients, patients, staff, and others involved in the care system.\n\n4. Blood Tests for Mental Health\n\nSoon, the possibility of using a blood test to detect mental health conditions such as depression could become a reality.\n\nIn April 2021, a team from Indiana University School of Medicine made a significant advancement by developing a new blood test for mental illness. This test relies on identifying specific RNA biomarkers that signal the presence of mood disorders.\n\nThe groundbreaking research revealed that this blood test is capable of assessing the severity of depression, as well as predicting the likelihood of someone developing severe depression or bipolar disorder in the future. Additionally, this test could be instrumental in customizing medication choices for individuals.\n\nMatthew Boland, PhD, a clinical psychologist based in Reno, Nevada, and a member of the Psych Central Medical Affairs Team, finds this development intriguing. He notes that while it's a promising step towards identifying biological markers for depression, it's still very early in terms of understanding and practical application.\n\nDespite being in the preliminary stages, the development of blood tests for mental illnesses represents a potential paradigm shift in diagnosing mental health conditions, which currently relies largely on a process of trial and error. This scientific progress could significantly enhance and transform the diagnosis of mental health disorders.\n\n5. Setting Boundaries on Social Media \n\nMost of us have heard, that excessive online time can adversely impact your mental health.\n\nYet, for those of you who own a smartphone, this reality does not make it any easier, to limit screen time. \n\nThose who have seen Netflix's \"The Social Dilemma\" would know that apps like Facebook, Instagram, and Pinterest are intentionally designed to capture and retain your attention for extended periods.\n\nYou may also remember the testimony of a former Facebook employee before Congress in October 2021, highlighting Instagram's detrimental impact on the mental health of teenagers. This testimony coincided with a worldwide Facebook outage.\n\nThese incidents have ignited a much-needed conversation about the potentially damaging effects of social media and the importance of occasionally disconnecting.\n\nThe topic of \"digital wellness\" and setting healthy limits with social media is expected to gain more attention, especially as ongoing research continues to reveal its negative impacts on both adolescents and adults.\n\nMatthew Boland predicts that we will see an intensified scrutiny of social media's impact on mental health, though it's uncertain if this will lead to concrete legislative actions.\n\nThe concept of \"social media boundaries\" will differ for each individual, and their effectiveness is still a matter of debate. Boland advises that the following strategies could be beneficial, though more research is needed:\nReducing screen time.\nUnfollowing certain accounts.\nAvoiding reading comments.\"\n\n6. Virtual Reality in Psychiatric Care\n\nVirtual reality (VR) technology is emerging as a groundbreaking tool in treating various mental health concerns. By creating simulated environments, VR allows patients to confront fears, practice coping strategies, and improve social skills in a controlled, safe setting. This has shown promising results, especially in treating PTSD, anxiety disorders, and phobias. As VR technology becomes more accessible, its application in psychiatric care is expected to expand, offering innovative ways to address mental health challenges.\n\n7. Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation (TMS)\n\nTranscranial Magnetic Stimulation (TMS) is gaining traction as a non-invasive treatment for depression and other mental health conditions. By using magnetic fields to stimulate nerve cells in the brain, TMS has shown effectiveness in improving symptoms of depression, especially in patients who have not responded well to traditional treatments. As research continues, TMS could become a more mainstream option for addressing persistent mental health disorders.\n\nHow To Identify Mental Health Concerns\n\nWhen it comes to Mental Health, America is no stranger to the widespread impact mental health challenges can have on individuals and communities. Recognizing these signs in oneself or others can be the first step toward seeking help.\n\nMental Health Resources\n\nNavigating mental health conditions requires a supportive network and access to reliable mental health providers.

Keep in mind that these speeds will be used in your overall plan, so consider whether you have other simultaneous users or many smart devices accessing your connection.\n\nStreaming differs from cable and satellite TV in that you can’t stream without internet, but they both can operate without an internet connection. Streaming is also often a cheaper option than cable or satellite TV, but keep track of how many services you belong to. Those monthly fees can add up and may surpass what your original cable bill was costing you.\n\nBefore you get rid of the cable box, consider your internet plan, household use and smart device needs to make sure you have enough bandwidth to support streaming services.\n\nIt’s getting harder and harder to watch TV  without  streaming, and it can get expensive. The average number of streaming subscriptions is four per household, and “the overall cost subscribers surveyed say they pay has gone up 13% in the past year, from an average of $61 per month to $69,” according to a 2025 Deloitte study.\n\nVideo streaming services are the most popular subscription-based streaming services, with 99% of all U.S. households subscribing to at least one or more streaming platforms. There are two types of streaming: live like YouTube TV or Hulu + Live TV and on-demand like Netflix or Disney+.\n\nEverything you need to start streaming TV\n\nWhile streaming TV can seem complicated, you probably already have everything you need in your home. Even if you don’t, you shouldn’t need to spend more than $50.00 on internet to get started.\n\nA CenturyLink fiber connection with up to 500 Mbps starts at $50.00/mo. and AT&T’s fiber 300 Mbps* plan is $55.00/mo.,* plus taxes (w/ eligible Autopay & Paperless bill). If you’re on a budget, you can find some providers with 100 Mbps plans starting around $30.00/mo., like Spectrum.\n\nTo ensure you are streaming efficiently, you’ll need:\n\nFast internet: You should have at least 25 Mbps for 4K or 50 – 100 Mbps+ for larger households\n\nStreaming-capable device: These include smart TVs, Roku, Fire TV, Apple TV, gaming consoles and other devices like cellphones, tablets and computers\n\nStreaming service subscriptions: You need to sign up for your chosen streaming services individually, usually on their websites\n\nStreaming apps installed on your device: Once your account is set up, the apps must be installed on your TV or phone\n\nIn our guide to internet speeds, we recommend a few different minimums for various types of streaming:\n\nTo stream videos in standard definition (SD), at least 3 Mbps is recommended\n\nTo stream videos in high definition (HD), at least 5 Mbps is recommended\n\nTo stream videos in HDR or 4K, at least 25 Mbps is recommended\n\nFilter your results:\n\nProvider Starting price* Download speed range Upload speed range Connection type Filters\nVerizon Fios $34.99 – $94.99/mo.** with mobile plan 300 – 2,300 Mbps 300 – 2,300 Mbps Fiber Fiber\nT-Mobile 5G Home Internet $30.00 – $50.00/mo.†††† w/ Autopay and voice line. Roku is the only streaming device manufacturer that makes a device (the  Roku Express+ ) that works with the red, yellow and white composite jacks found on older TVs.\n\nYou can also purchase an HDMI to RCA adapter to convert your TV. This lets you use any streaming device you want, but there have been  complaints  about this not working very well with streaming services. We recommend going with the Roku if you have an older TV.\n\nHow to start streaming\n\nIf you use a smart TV, you can skip down to step two. Almost all streaming devices plug into your TV’s HDMI port — and usually a power outlet as well. The first thing you’ll need to do is plug the device in, switch your TV over to the HDMI input you’re using and start following the instructions that appear on the screen.\n\nOnce you’ve plugged in your streaming device, you’ll need to sign in to your home’s Wi-Fi network. Some devices — like Amazon’s Fire TV Stick 4K and Google’s  Chromecast Ultra  — can connect directly to your internet modem using an  Ethernet cable. This generally provides a more reliable connection, so you’ll experience less buffering on your streams.\n\nPopular streaming services like Netflix and Hulu will come pre-downloaded on your smart TV or streaming device, but if you want to watch a more niche service like Shudder or The Criterion Channel, you’ll probably have to download it yourself.\n\nFind the right streaming service\n\nWhen it comes to streaming TV, there are two main types of services available: live and on-demand. Live streaming is like a cable replacement. It airs the same channels, has the same commercials and you pay a premium compared to other services. On-demand streaming like Hulu and Netflix don’t have any live content — you can watch their shows and movies whenever you want.\n\nOn-demand streaming\n\nThese are the most popular category of streaming services by far. They generally cost around $8 to $15/mo., come with a free trial and give you access to thousands of TV shows and movies. Bundling services like ones that Disney+ offers can save you money. As of July 2024, however, Netflix no longer bundles with other services.\n\nHere are some of the popular on-demand streaming services:\n\nFilter your results:\n\nStreaming service Starting price*\nNetflix $7.99 – $24.99/mo.\nDisney+ $9.99 – $15.99/mo.\nHBO Max $9.99 – $20.99/mo.\nPrime Video $8.99 – $17.98/mo.\nPhilo $28.00/mo.\nHulu $9.99 – $18.99/mo.\nPeacock $10.99 – $16.99/mo.\n\nLive TV streaming\n\nCable replacements are services that stream live TV through your internet connection. Enjoy these apps on your smartphone or tablet and even on the go by downloading content to watch when you’re offline. \n\nFamily and kid-friendly streaming\n\nIf you’re looking for kid-friendly features, then there are options like parental controls, content filtering and individual kid profiles available with various streaming platforms. Netflix, Hulu, Prime Video, Disney+ and HBO Max have parental control features, kid-friendly profiles and content libraries. \n\nSolutions to common streaming issues \n\nCommon streaming problems\n\nBuffering can be caused by a slow internet connection, network congestion or device limitations. It can be solved by pausing and resuming playback, clearing the cache or cookies or switching to a lower video quality.\n\nInsufficient bandwidth, streaming app settings or device capabilities and connectivity issues can cause poor video quality. You can solve this by checking internet speed, adjusting the video quality settings in the streaming app or connecting to a more stable Wi-Fi network.\n\nConnectivity issues involve your Wi-Fi signal dropping, network interference or router configuration issues. Restarting your router, moving closer to the Wi-Fi source or using a wired connection for more stable streaming can solve connectivity issues.\n\nCompatibility issues between streaming apps and devices, especially when using older or less popular devices, can be solved with updating firmware or operating systems, checking app compatibility lists or using alternative streaming devices.\n\nWhen streaming apps crash, freeze or become unresponsive during playback, close and reopen the app, update the app to the latest version or clear the app’s cache and data.\n\nSome streaming issues may be specific to certain devices or operating systems, such as smart TVs, gaming consoles or mobile devices. It’s already filmed and edited, so you can’t “miss it.” Live streaming is just what it sounds like — live.

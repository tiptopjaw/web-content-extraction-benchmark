{"title":"Top 10 Secure Computing Tips","author":null,"date":null,"main_content":"\"Top 10\" List of Secure Computing Tips\n\nTip #1 - You are a target to hackers\n\nDon't ever say, \"It won't happen to me.\" We are all at risk and the stakes are high - both for your personal and financial well-being  and for the university's standing and reputation. \n\nCybersecurity is everyone's responsibility.\n\nBy following the tips below and remaining vigilant, you are doing your part to protect yourself and others.\n\nTip #2 - Keep software up-to-date\n\nInstalling software updates for your operating system and programs is critical. Always install the latest security updates for your devices:\n\nTurn on Automatic Updates for your operating system.\n\nUse web browsers such as Chrome or Firefox that receive frequent, automatic security updates.\n\nMake sure to keep browser plug-ins (Flash, Java, etc.) up-to-date.\n\nTip #3 - Avoid Phishing scams - beware of suspicious emails and phone calls\n\nPhishing scams are a constant threat - using various social engineering ploys, cyber-criminals will attempt to trick you into divulging personal information such as your login ID and password, banking or credit card information.\n\nPhishing scams can be carried out by phone, text, or through social networking sites - but most commonly by email.\n\nBe suspicious of any official-looking email message or phone call that asks for personal or financial information.\n\nCheck out our Phishing Resources section for details about identifying phishing scams and protecting yourself.\n\nTip #4 - Practice good password management\n\nWe all have too many passwords to manage - and it's easy to take short-cuts, like reusing the same password.  A password manager can help you to maintain strong unique passwords for all of your accounts.  These programs can generate strong passwords for you, enter credentials automatically, and remind you to update your passwords periodically. UC Berkeley offers free LastPass Premium to all users with a CalNet ID.\n\nOur  Protecting Your Credentials how-to article contains detailed recommendations for keeping your password safe.\n\nTip #5 -  Be careful what you click\n\nAvoid visiting unknown websites or downloading software from untrusted sources. These sites often host malware that will automatically install (often silently) and compromise your computer.\n\nIf attachments or links in the email are unexpected or suspicious for any reason, don't click on it.\n\nISO recommends using Click-to-Play or NoScript, browser add-on features that prevent the automatic download of plug-in content (e.g., Java, Flash) and scripts that can harbor malicious code.\n\nTip #6 - Never leave devices unattended\n\nThe physical security of your devices is just as important as their technical security. \n\nIf you need to leave your laptop, phone, or tablet for any length of time - lock the screen and physically lock it up so no one else can use it. \n\nIf you keep protected data on a flash drive or external hard drive, make sure they're encrypted and locked up as well. \n\nFor desktop and laptop computers, lock the screen or shut-down the system when not in use.\n\nTip #7 - Safeguard Protected Data\n\nBe aware of Protected Data that you come into contact with and its associated restrictions. Review the UCB Data Classification Standard for examples of types of data in each classification level. In general:\n\nKeep high-level (P4) Protected Data (e.g., SSN's, credit card information, financial aid information, health information, etc.) off of your workstation, laptop, and mobile devices.\n\nSecurely remove sensitive data files from your system when they are no longer needed.\n\nAlways use encryption when storing or transmitting sensitive data.\n\nUnsure of how to store or handle sensitive data?  Email us at security@berkeley.edu. \n\nTip #8 - Use mobile devices safely\n\nConsidering how much we rely on our mobile devices and how susceptible they are to attack, you'll want to make sure you are protected:\n\nLock your device with a PIN or password - and never leave it unprotected in public.\n\nOnly install apps from trusted sources (Apple AppStore, Google Play).\n\nKeep the device's operating system up-to-date.\n\nDon't click on links or attachments from unsolicited emails or texts.\n\nAvoid transmitting or storing personal information on the device.\n\nMost handheld devices are capable of employing data encryption - consult your device's documentation for available options.\n\nUse Apple's  Find my iPhone or the Android Device Manager tools to help prevent loss or theft.\n\nTip #9 - Install antivirus/anti-malware protection\n\nOnly install these programs from a known and trusted source. Keep virus definitions, engines and software up-to-date to ensure your programs remains effective.\n\nSee our Minimum Security Standards Anti-Malware Software Guidelines for more information \n\nTip #10 - Back up your data\n\nBack up regularly - if you are a victim of a security incident, the only guaranteed way to repair your computer is to erase and re-install the system."}

Now, in the golden age of super foams, brands have finally successfully executed that promise: trainers that are both lightweight and über-soft. Still, not everyone’s slice of lightweight feels the same.\n\nWeights can also cater to workouts. Perhaps you want a featherlight racing flat for track or a lightweight trainer for speed workouts on the road. A heftier trainer packed with cushioning would serve you well on low-effort days or recovery runs when your body needs that extra padding to absorb shock.\n\nA starting point for gauging the weight of a daily trainer: a men’s 9, according to the training shoes I weighed in the RW lab, is approximately 10 ounces, whereas a women’s size 7 daily trainer averaged around 8 ounces.\n\nThe midsole foam in your shoes plays into comfort as well as ride. The typical foams used in running shoes are:\n\nEVA, ethylene-vinyl acetate: EVA was the standard when it came to shoe foam because of its support, cushioning, and flexibility. These days, however, more brands are either injecting EVA with nitrogen and blending it with other materials to make it more lightweight and responsive.\n\nSupercritical: Supercritical foam is when nitrogen or CO2 is pumped into the foam to make it lighter. The foam is filled with microscopic bubbles, enhancing bounce and durability.\n\nPEBA, polyether block amide: Many racing shoes have their carbon-fiber plate sandwiches between two slabs of PEBA. The foam is known for being bouncy, lightweight, and propulsive (the plate isn’t a propulsion component as some believe; instead it’s a stabilizer for the foam). The downside is that it]s not as durable as other shoe foams.\n\nYour shoes should feel comfortable on your feet, free of hotspots, friction, and pinching. There should be room for your toes to splay and your heel shouldn’t slip out of the collar when running.\n\nSome things to look out for:\n\nLength: The best way to determine if the shoe is the right length is to stand with the shoes on, feet shoulder width apart. With a friend’s help, have them measure the toe room. You should have a thumb’s width of wiggle room from the tip of the shoe to the top of your foot.\n\nWidth: One way to determine if the width is too narrow or too wide is to use the finger test. You should have two fingers’ width across the lacing. However, the best way to find the perfect fit is to visit your local shoe store and get your feet measured by a pro.\n\nFor more info on how to test a prospective pair of shoes, check out our fit guide and video walkthrough.\n\nDaily Trainer: The workhorses of the running world, daily trainers are designed to emphasize comfort and durability so you can run in them every day. Daily trainers usually have well-cushioned midsoles (although they can still feel firm), thicker uppers with padding around the ankle and heel for a comfortable fit, and full-coverage rubber outsoles that offer good grip and high-mileage durability.\n\nRacing Shoes: Speedy shoes made for racing showcase the latest innovations in running shoe technology and design. Right now, that means PEBA foams and carbon fiber plates. Next year? Who knows.\n\nRacing shoes keep their weight low and feature propulsive cushioning to help you set and maintain fast paces. The tradeoff? They’re usually not very durable or stable, and they’re quite expensive. It’s best to save these kicks for race day.\n\nStability: Everyone’s feet pronate as they run. Some runners need extra support in their shoes due to overpronation—when your foot rolls inward. Excessive overpronation can lead to injury to the knees and lower back, among other areas. Neutral runners may also need shoes with support because of late-onset overpronation (when their form becomes sloppy due to fatigue on long runs). Medial posts are recommended for severe overpronators, while guide rails (dense foam on the medial and lateral sides of the shoe) are best for mild overpronators and neutral runners needing a little extra support.\n\nTrail: Trail shoes are usually heavier because they have features to protect your feet on uneven terrain. These features may include gaiter attachments, heavier rubber with pronounced lugs, and thicker uppers for abrasion resistance.\n\nHow to Choose the Best Running Shoes FAQs\n\nBuying based on looks is a big mistake new—and old—runners tend to make. Unlike trainers 15 years ago, shoes today come in a variety of colors. But you should still base your choice on fit, feel, and ride. Make sure you have a thumbnail’s width of toe room and a secure ankle that won’t cause slippage. Test the shoes on the store’s treadmill or outside.\n\nAnother popular faux pas: Buying shoes that are too small. Our shoe size changes throughout our life due to many factors. Squeezing into an old shoe size can cause black toenails, blisters, or, at worst, injury.\n\nA general rule of thumb to retire shoes is twice a year if you don’t keep a mileage log for each pair. But runners have different wear patterns; some may be able to get away with running in shoes for years while others go through a pair in a matter of weeks. We recommend 300 to 500 miles for everyday trainers.\n\nFor new runners we recommend a shoe like the Brooks Ghost or Nike Pegasus as a starting point. These shoes are moderately cushioned and offer support. From there, you can decide if you prefer more cushioned shoes or lighter shoes or want to stick with these pairs as transition from new runner to road—or trail—warrior.

{"title":"How meditation rewires brains and improves physical health | Opinion","author":"Manoj Jain and Jagpreet Chhatwal, The Commercial Appeal","date":"2025-12-19T11:07:13.157234586+00:00","main_content":"In a world marked by war, climate instability and widening inequality, meditation offers a counterbalance — a disciplined practice of inner steadiness amid constant disruption.\n\nShow Caption\n\nHide Caption\n\nHow to start meditating for beginners\n\nA new study shows meditation may be just as effective as some medications.\n\nOur brains hum with electricity. Tiny pulses fire when we are awake, when we concentrate and when we sleep. But what happens when we do something deceptively simple — when we sit in stillness and watch our breath or our thoughts?\n\nWhen these pulses are measured on an electroencephalogram, or EEG — the brain’s equivalent of an EKG for the heart — we see something remarkable. Meditation is not a passive act of rest. It produces measurable changes in brain activity, revealing that stillness itself can alter the mind.\n\nThis has led us to wonder whether the outward stillness and inward calm of meditation can change not only our bodies, but also the world we inhabit.\n\nThe answer appears to be yes.\n\nMeditation from ancient to modern times\n\nMeditation is an ancient practice that cuts across faith traditions — from Buddhist monks and Jain ascetics to Sufi mystics and Christian contemplatives. What was once passed down through lineage and scripture is now being examined through the lens of modern science. Researchers are beginning to explore how meditation shapes brain function, influences physiology and may even affect social and collective wellbeing.\n\nThe timing of this question matters. December 21 has been designated World Meditation Day by the United Nations. This year, a global meditation will be led by Gurudev Sri Sri Ravi Shankar, a humanitarian and spiritual leader, with millions expected to participate across more than 100 U.S. cities. \n\nA central gathering will take place at the World Trade Center in New York City — a location chosen for its deep symbolism. Last year alone, the event achieved a Guinness World Record for the largest YouTube live guided meditation, and organizers reported a total global participation of over 8.5 million across platforms and in-person gatherings.\n\nHow meditation impacts the brain and body\n\nThe scientific basis of meditation, while still evolving, dates back to the 1960s. Researchers in Boston placed EEG electrodes on Tibetan monks — athletic meditators who had trained for years — and were surprised to find brainwave patterns unlike those observed in non-meditators. Today, advanced imaging tools such as functional MRI show that meditation reshapes neural circuits, calming the amygdala, the brain’s center for fear and fight-or-flight responses.\n\nThink of the brain as a muscle that strengthens or weakens depending on how it is used. Meditation is a form of mental exercise. Over time, it does more than change the mind — it changes the body. Studies show reductions in stress, blood pressure and anxiety.\n\nMeditation also improves sleep, enhances emotional regulation and deepens self-awareness. In individuals with recurrent depression, meditation has been shown to reduce relapse rates by nearly 50%, matching or exceeding antidepressant medications but without the side effects.\n\nJust as physical exercise includes both cardio and strength training, meditation takes different forms. Focused-attention practices — such as concentrating on the breath, focusing on a candle, or engaging in structured breathwork like SKY Breath Meditation or pranayama — gently shift the brain into a relaxed, yet alert, meditative state.\n\nMindfulness, or open-monitoring meditation, involves observing thoughts and sensations without judgment. In deeper states of self-transcendence, even these thoughts fall away, giving rise to a profound sense of stillness and clarity.\n\nMeditation and brain waves\n\nEach meditation state corresponds to a distinct brainwave pattern when measured with an EEG. Delta waves resemble the deep night of dreamless sleep. Theta is the hazy twilight of dreams and imagination. Alpha reflects a calm afternoon — relaxed yet clear.\n\nBeta dominates the busy daylight of thinking and doing. And gamma appears in rare peak moments of insight, focus and integration. These rhythms fluctuate throughout the day and across activities, shaping how we think, feel and function.\n\nOne day, we may have wearable devices that allow us to track these brain states in real time, much as we now measure heart rate, sleep cycles and physical exertion.\n\nCan meditation change the world?\n\nBeyond individual health, meditation is increasingly being explored as a tool for collective well-being. The United Nations Human Rights Mindfulness Initiative has promoted World Meditation Day to strengthen diplomacy, ethical leadership and human rights work.\n\nIn a world marked by war, climate instability and widening inequality, meditation offers a counterbalance — a disciplined practice of inner steadiness amid constant disruption.\n\nOn this World Meditation Day, pause. Silence your devices. Close your eyes. Take a few deep breaths. Even a few minutes of stillness can shift the mind, calm the body and subtly change how we show up in the world.\n\nSometimes, that is where change begins.\n\nManoj Jain is adjunct faculty at Emory University and a physician based in Memphis.  \n\nJagpreet Chhatwal is an Associate Professor of Radiology at Harvard Medical School."}

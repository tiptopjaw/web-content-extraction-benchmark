{"title":"Cyber Security Best Practices for 2025","author":"SentinelOne","date":"2024-10-22T08:15:34+00:00","main_content":"Author: SentinelOne\n\nIn recent times, individuals and businesses have come to rely on online processes and services to carry out normal daily tasks. Using smart devices for communication and e-commerce is now a way of life for most of us. Businesses have moved operations and data to the cloud. Unfortunately, this also has spawned antisocial and criminal activity.\n\nRansomware in particular has been hitting the headlines. For example, the recent failure of a Microsoft systems upgrade caused a loss of air traffic control systems, affecting over 3,000 flights in the US alone. Another example of increasing criminal activity is identity theft, where personal information is stolen from a corporate database and used to steal money or goods.\n\nWhat Is Cyber Security, and Why Do We Need it?\n\nCybersecurity helps individuals and organizations protect their systems, data, and networks from unauthorized access, attacks, data thefts, and breaches. It uses tools that, among other things, create firewalls, identify malware, and manage user access to systems and data. These are complemented in a corporate environment by policies and procedures to be observed by all users.\n\nBest practices are guidelines, policies, and procedures that help to minimize security risks and safeguard sensitive information.\n\nCommon Cybersecurity Risks, Issues, and Challenges\n\nHere are some of the more common issues and challenges that cybersecurity should handle:\n\nPhishing attacks\nIssue: Phishing is one of the most common and successful cyberattacks. Attackers use deceptive emails or messages to trick users into revealing sensitive information or clicking on malicious links.\nChallenge: Despite user education, phishing tactics are becoming increasingly sophisticated, making them harder to detect.\n\nRansomware\nIssue: Ransomware attacks involve encrypting a victim’s data and demanding payment for decryption. These attacks target organizations across industries.\nChallenge: Once systems are infected, recovery can be complex and expensive. Preventing these attacks requires constant updates and employee training.\n\nInsider threats\nIssue: Employees or contractors can intentionally or accidentally cause harm by leaking sensitive data, abusing their access privileges, or falling prey to social engineering attacks.\nChallenge: Monitoring insider activity without breaching privacy and trust, while detecting malicious intent, is a delicate balancing act.\n\nOutdated software and patch management\nIssue: Unpatched vulnerabilities in software are a common vector for cyberattacks. Attackers exploit these vulnerabilities to gain unauthorized access to systems.\nChallenge: Ensuring timely patching of all systems and software across an organization is resource-intensive, especially in large IT environments.\n\nThird-party vendor risk\nIssue: Many organizations rely on third-party vendors for various services, which exposes them to risks if the vendor’s cybersecurity posture is weak.\nChallenge: Assessing and managing third-party risks can be difficult, especially when vendors have access to sensitive systems or data.\n\nLack of Cybersecurity Awareness\nIssue: Many security breaches occur because employees are unaware of best cybersecurity practices or are careless in following them.\nChallenge: Continuous and effective cybersecurity training programs are essential but difficult to maintain.\n\nDDoS (Distributed Denial-of-Service) Attacks\nIssue:  DDoS attacks overwhelm a server or network with traffic, causing service disruptions.\nChallenge: Defending against large-scale DDoS attacks requires robust infrastructure, which can be costly for smaller organizations.\n\nRegulatory Compliance\nIssue: Organizations are required to comply with various cybersecurity regulations (GDPR, HIPAA, etc.), which often involve implementing stringent security measures.\nChallenge: Keeping up with evolving regulations and ensuring compliance across all operations can be resource-intensive.\n\nData Breaches\nIssue: Unauthorized access to sensitive information can result in data breaches, leading to financial and reputational damage.\nChallenge: Detecting and responding to data breaches quickly while minimizing damage is difficult, especially when sophisticated attackers are involved.\n\nAI and Machine Learning Risks\nIssue: While AI and machine learning are used for cybersecurity, attackers are also leveraging these technologies to launch more advanced and personalized attacks.\nChallenge: It can be difficult to keep pace with these emerging threats and develop AI systems that complement and enhance existing systems.\n\nIdentity and Access Management (IAM)\nIssue: Weak authentication mechanisms or poorly managed access controls can lead to unauthorized access to critical systems and data.\nChallenge: Implementing robust IAM solutions, like multifactor authentication (MFA) and role-based access controls (RBAC), across complex environments can be difficult. This is particularly so where access is requested from a variety of devices and operating environments.\n\nCybersecurity in a work-from-home, IOT, or Cloud Environment\nIssue: The current move toward remote working and moving data and applications to the cloud brings new cybersecurity issues and challenges.\nChallenge: Many policies and procedures—for example, equipment types and operating environments—cannot be enforced easily, and new policies and procedures need to be developed and rolled out.\n\nCloud Security Risks\nIssue: As organizations migrate to cloud environments, security challenges such as data breaches, misconfigured cloud services, and insufficient control over access rights arise.\nChallenge: Managing security in the cloud requires specialized knowledge and strategies that differ from traditional on-premises security models.\n\nBYOD (bring your own device) Policies\nIssue: Allowing employees to use personal devices for work purposes on-site and remotely opens up the network to a wider range of potential vulnerabilities.\nChallenge: Ensuring the security of these devices and segregating personal and work data is complex.\n\nIoT (Internet of Things) Vulnerabilities\nIssue: IoT devices often have weak security and are easy targets for attackers.\nChallenge: Securing a rapidly growing number of IoT devices in homes, offices, and industrial systems is a significant challenge.\n\nAddressing these cybersecurity challenges requires a combination of technology, policies, and employee awareness to reduce risks and respond effectively to threats.\n\nExplore how Singularity™ Endpoint Security provides autonomous protection against advanced cyber threats.\n\nCyber Security Best Practices\n\nAt a corporate level, several offerings can be used to provide a cybersecurity environment. This is a wide field and is continually changing and expanding. Examples can be found  here.\n\nThe five C’s of cybersecurity are change, compliance, cost, continuity, and coverage. Any statement of best practice should conform to them. In addition, there are five basic principles to be observed:\nRisk management —Identify and assess potential risks to your systems and data, and implement strategies to mitigate these risks.\nNetwork security —Protect your network infrastructure by implementing firewalls, intrusion detection systems, and secure configurations.\nAccess control —Ensure that only authorized users have access to sensitive information and systems. This includes managing user privileges and using strong authentication methods.\nMonitoring and detection —Continuously monitor your systems for suspicious activity, and have mechanisms in place to detect and respond to potential security breaches.\nIncident response —Develop and practice an incident response plan to quickly address and recover from security incidents.\n\nBearing in mind the challenges and issues outlined above, here are some essential cyber security best practices that conform to the five basic principles:\n\n#1. Use Strong, Unique Passwords\nUse a complex combination of letters, numbers, and special characters. Use system-generated passwords, and don’t allow users to generate their own.\nDon’t use the same password across multiple accounts.\nUse a password manager to generate, securely store, and manage complex passwords.\n\n#2. Enable Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)\nAdd an extra layer of security by requiring additional verification (e.g., text message code, authentication app) along with your password.\n\n#3. Keep Software Updated\nEnsure that your operating systems, software, and applications are regularly updated to patch vulnerabilities.\nEnable automatic updates so that your devices and systems automatically receive the latest security patches.\n\n#4. Install Antivirus, Anti-Spyware, and Anti-Malware Software on all Devices\nUse reputable antivirus, ant-spyware, and anti-malware tools to scan and block malicious software.\nRun routine scans to detect and remove threats.\nImplement regular—at least daily—malware pattern updates.\n\n#5. Use Firewalls\nFirewalls help block unauthorized access to your network by monitoring incoming and outgoing traffic. Ensure both software and hardware firewalls are enabled.\n\n#6. Secure WiFi Networks\nSet up WiFi with WPA3 encryption, and change the default router password.\nAvoid using public WiFi for sensitive transactions unless using a private, secured VPN.\n\n#7. Back up Data Regularly\nMaintain backups of critical data on a secure, off-site, or cloud-based storage system.\nEnsure that your backup process works by testing restores periodically.\n\n#8. Beware of Phishing Attacks\nEnsure that you and other users are familiar with phishing and other social media and social engineering exploits.\nBe skeptical about unsolicited offers and requests, particularly those around money or goods. For example, a common email attack involves a courier company asking for money to complete delivery.\n\n#9. Educate and Train Employees\nConduct regular training on phishing, social engineering, and security awareness to help employees recognize potential threats.\nImplement security policies, such as acceptable use policies and data protection guidelines.\n\nUsing Singularity™ XDR, enhances threat detection across your enterprise.\n\n#10. Practice Good Email Hygiene\nDon’t click on suspicious links or download attachments from unverified emails.\nSet up spam filters to reduce phishing emails and block malicious links.\n\n#11. Encrypt Sensitive Data\nEncrypt sensitive data both at rest and in transit to ensure that unauthorized parties cannot access it without decryption keys.\n\n#12. Have a Program of Regular Backups of Systems\nHave regular, ideally automated backups of systems. This is vital in the case of a ransomware attack, where you recover by taking your infrastructure systems back to bare metal and restoring the latest valid backup.\n\n#13. Limit User Privileges\nImplement the principle of least privilege, ensuring users only have the access necessary to perform their jobs.\nUse role-based access control (RBAC) to manage permissions and limit access to sensitive areas. Again, there are software tools available to assist in this process.\nRegularly review user privileges to ensure that employees changing roles relinquish the privileges of their previous roles.\n\n#14. Have a Proactive Cybersecurity Posture\nCreate published workplace cybersecurity policies that provide guidelines that foster security awareness. They also should have clear instructions on how to proceed when suspecting a security threat or a security failure.\nReport suspected security threats or security failures immediately. That may mitigate or prevent damage.\nUse security monitoring tools to track suspicious activity, such as unusual login attempts or data transfers. This is especially necessary for financial transactions.\n\n#15. Conduct Security Audits\nReview logs regularly to identify potential threats early.\n\n#16. Secure Physical Devices\nEnsure that all on-site end-user devices such as laptops and smart devices are secured with passwords and, in the case of particularly sensitive devices, biometric security. If not in use, store in a secure location.\n\n#17. Secure Mobile Devices\nWhere possible, use a standard device software configuration that\nuses device encryption and requires strong authentication,\nuses encryption apps on mobile devices used remotely,\nhas security apps installed to prevent data breaches and malware,\nwhere possible, prohibits connections to unsecured WiFi networks, forcing connections via a secure VPN, and\ndoes not allow apps to be downloaded.\n\n#18. Implement Physical Security\nRestrict access to sensitive areas such as data centers or server rooms.\nUse security measures like access control systems, surveillance cameras, and alarms.\n\n#19. Develop an Incident Response Plan\nCreate a documented plan for responding to cybersecurity incidents, such as data breaches or ransomware attacks.\nRegularly test and update the incident response plan to improve readiness.\n\n#20. Internet of Things\n\nThe Internet of Things has brought a whole range of new network-enabled devices to the workplace. For example, they are common in manufacturing control systems for the management of the production process, passing performance statistics back to a central database. By and large, they use non-standard operating systems that do not have the capability to support common anti-malware and other security protocols. Hackers can use them as a means of entry to a network.\n\nIntroducing IoT devices to a network means a review of the security arrangements around them.\n\nBest Practices: Use Them\n\nBy adopting these cyber security best practices, you not only protect yourself but also contribute to the overall security posture of your workplace.

You feel pulled in a million directions.\n\nI’ve seen organizations struggle with this tension throughout my career in media and startups. Focus too much on the day-to-day of the business at yearly and quarterly junctures, and you lose the chance to extract lessons from previous work. Focus too much on strategy, and growth stalls.\n\nThis doesn’t mean that you need to bypass formal cycle planning, as many startups do. Taking time to align on goals and build a focused roadmap is necessary to drive growth. The problem is how long the process takes—finding yourself in mid-January without your annual goals finalized.\n\nInnovative companies are now solving this conundrum with AI, and it’s something we are also rolling out at Every. Bringing AI into the process saves time, leads to more refined goals, and preserves energy for the work that matters.\n\nThe tech stack\n\nIt’s helpful to start with a standard framework for the planning itself. In the past, I’ve used the W Framework. Outlined by Lenny Rachitsky and Nels Gilbreth, previously at Airbnb and Eventbrite, respectively, this approach to quarterly and yearly planning involves four steps:\n\nContext: Leadership shares a high-level strategy with teams.\n\nPlans: Teams respond with proposed plans.\n\nIntegration: Leadership integrates into a single plan and shares it with teams.\n\nBuy-in: Teams make final tweaks, confirm buy-in, and get rolling.\n\nWhen done well, these documents are immensely valuable. They outline what each department will and won’t do, who owns those projects, and a standard for success in a concise and data-driven way. Call it what you will— OKR, KPI, or DRI —but the most important thing is driving focus and alignment on the few things that matter most.\n\nI’ve worked at companies where each of these steps takes multiple days—sometimes entire weeks.

Knowing what zone you are gardening in allows you to pick plants that will withstand the coldest expected temperature for your area. Read more: USDA Growing Zones: What They Mean for Your Garden.\n\nExposure: The amount of sun or shade a plant needs\n\nFull sun: 6 or more hours of direct sunlight each day\n\nPartial sun/shade: 4 to 6 hours of direct sunlight each day\n\nFull shade: Less than 4 hours of direct sunlight each day\n\nDappled shade: A mixture of sun and shade, usually under an open tree; similar to partial shade\n\nHabit: Refers to the general structure or shape of the mature plant\n\nClimbing: Plants that climb fences, trellises, or structures (i.e., vines)\n\nClump forming: Plants that form clumps of foliage, often spreading to form more clumps\n\nMounded: Plants with a rounded shape, usually wider than tall\n\nSpreading: Plants that are low-growing and spread along the ground, rooting at nodes along the stem\n\nTrailing: Plants that trail along the ground or cascade out of containers, but do not root from the stem\n\nUpright: Plants that are taller than wide, with generally straight sides\n\nHeight & spread: The estimated size of a mature plant\n\nFirst or last frost date: The average date for your area for the first frost in fall or the last frost in spring\n\nNative: A plant that has grown naturally over hundreds or thousands of years in a particular area. These plants are well adapted to their native growing conditions (soil, climate, water, etc.) and are beneficial to their local pollinators and wildlife.\n\nInvasive: A non-native plant that becomes established in an area outside it's native region and spreads rapidly, to the point of disrupting the native environment and ecosystem."}

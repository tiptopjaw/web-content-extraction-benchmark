{"title":"2025 Best Diet Wins Gold for Wellness and Disease Prevention","author":null,"date":null,"main_content":"MSM Assistant Professor of Gastroenterology Dr. Jesús Luévano, Jr. told CNN that people\nshould have detailed discussions with their doctors before choosing and starting a\ndiet.\n\nDr. Jesús Luévano, Jr.\n\nAssistant Professor of Gastroenterology, MSM\n\nThe award-winning Mediterranean diet has done it again, capturing 2025's \"best of\nthe best\" award from U.S. News & World Report, which puts out a yearly list of the\nmost and least beneficial diets as ranked by nutrition professionals.\n\nThe Mediterranean diet — which is more of a lifestyle than a diet — has won top honors\nsince 2019 for its focus on eating fruits, vegetables, grains, olive oil, nuts and\nseeds, while emphasizing the importance of meals with family and friends and daily\nexercise. The diet also slashes consumption of sweets and recommends small amounts\nof dairy and meat, especially red meat. Fish, however, is a staple, especially fatty\nfish such as sardines.\n\nIn past years, the report has ranked diets from No. 1 to No. 40 or so, with the top-ranked\ndiet winning the proverbial gold medal, so to speak, while judges banned diets at\nthe bottom of the list from any proximity to the award podium.\n\n\"There would be this long list of diets with a whole bunch at the bottom, which, frankly,\nno one should be really paying attention to for the most part,\" said Gretel Schueller,\nmanaging editor of health for U.S. News & World Report, who oversees the annual diet\nrankings.\n\nThis year, however, the best diet report takes a different approach, asking nutritional\njudges to give diets up to five stars, much like the consumer rating systems on many\ncommercial platforms such as Amazon.\n\n\"Pretty much any product or item you're looking at these days seems to get rated on\na five-star scale,\" Schueller said. \"We believe this new approach allows people more\nchoice and a more personalized experience as they consider their health priorities\nand eating goals.\"\n\nUsing this new system, the trio of Mediterranean, Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension,\nor DASH, and the flexitarian diets each received 4-plus stars as the best overall\ndiets, the best healthy eating diets and the easiest diets to follow.\n\nThe DASH diet emphasizes limiting salt intake in its goal to reduce blood pressure,\nwhile the flexitarian diet, true to its name, allows an occasional indulgence of meat\nor poultry in its vegetarian approach. All are plant-based diets that suggest limiting\nrefined, ultraprocessed foods, red meat and added sugars.\n\nRanking Diets for Health Conditions\n\nThe 2025 report has also included new rankings for diets designed to help chronic\nconditions such as arthritis, diverticulitis, fatty liver disease and irritable bowel\nsyndrome, or stages of life such as menopause.\n\nSome medical associations do recommend specific diets — the American Heart Association\nranks the DASH diet as highest for heart health, as it is 100% aligned with AHA goals\nfor heart-healthy eating. In the new best diet report by U.S. News & World Report,\nthe DASH diet won top ranking (4.9 stars) for both heart health and blood pressure\ncontrol.\n\nHowever, other professional medical groups do not promote specific diets, preferring\nto focus on a more personalized approach. Take the \"menopause diet,\" which earned\n4.6 stars from the report's judges.\n\n\"There is no such thing as a 'menopause diet' — the only diet shown to help with hot\nflashes is a whole food, plant-based diet low in oil and high in soy,\" said Dr. Stephanie\nFaubion, director of the Mayo Clinic's Center for Women's Health in Jacksonville,\nFlorida, and medical director for the Menopause Society.\n\nWhile Faubion agreed with some of menopause diet's recommendations — such as not eating\nclose to bedtime — she took exception with U.S. News & World Report's callout to Nutrisystem's\nfor-profit menopause diet.\n\n\"Personally, as a menopause doctor and a scientist, I would not suggest my patients\nrely on a paid diet for menopause,\" Faubion said in an email. \"It doesn't help people\nunderstand what they should be doing long term but relies on someone to put it in\nfront of them, which isn't sustainable.\"\n\nConsult Your Doctor About Diets\n\nMany of the new diet categories in the report revolved around digestive health and\ndiets that claim to reduce inflammation for conditions such as arthritis and gout.\n\n\"There is no single diet for arthritis or for gout,\" Jill Tyrer, an editor at the\nArthritis Foundation, told CNN in an email. \"Plant-based diets that are low in sugar,\nunhealthy fats, salt and processed foods — like the Mediterranean and DASH diets —\nare the best options to help manage these diseases.\n\n\"However, most people with autoimmune, inflammatory forms of arthritis and many with\ngout require medications to slow or stop the disease from progressing.\"\n\nPeople with digestive issues should not use the U.S. News & World Report rankings\nto decide on their diet without first having a thorough discussion with their attending\nphysician, said Jesús Luévano Jr., MD, MS, an assistant professor of gastroenterology\nat Morehouse School of Medicine in Atlanta.\n\n\"My concern is that people may look at these rankings and then assume that because\nit's done by nutrition experts, it's the be-all, end-all, and therefore don't have\nproductive conversations with their physicians, who can really help focus on the best\nfoods for them,\" said Luévano, who is also a spokesperson for the American Gastroenterological\nAssociation.\n\n\"You have to do the homework, such as keeping a food diary of what you're eating and\nthen work with your doctor to decide what might be the best approach for your specific\nissue,\" he said.\n\nFor fatty liver disease, inflammation and overall gut health, the Mediterranean diet\nreceived four-plus star ratings, according to the report. For diverticulitis, a disease\nin which large bulging pouches in the large intestine and colon create significant\npain, judges gave the Mediterranean diet a 3.7 rating.\n\n\"For fatty liver and gallbladder disease, we do recommend low-fat diets and the Mediterranean\ndiet, as a diet with leaner meats and healthier fats, has been shown to be beneficial,\"\nLuévano said.\n\nFor irritable bowel syndrome, judges gave the low FODMAP diet 4.8 stars. The diet,\ndeveloped in Australia, focuses on reducing foods and carbs that ferment and create\nextra water in the bowels, which can cause cramping, pain and diarrhea.\n\nThe FODMAP acronym stands for foods that fermentable; oligosaccharides, which are\nsugar molecule chains; disaccharides, such as lactose; monosaccharides such as fructose;\nand polyols, which are sugar alcohols, such as sorbitol and erythritol.\n\n\"Because there are so many subtypes of (irritable bowel syndrome), that is probably\nthe best example of where diet really should be individualized for each patient,\"\nLuévano said. \"If a patient tried to avoid every single item on the most comprehensive\nFODMAP list, they might eliminate common fruits such as apples and pears and certain\ngrains that might actually be helpful.\n\n\"There needs to be careful discussion with their physician and a nutritionist because\nevery patient has his or her unique food triggers,\" he added. \"We need to see if we\nremove this one, does it really help you with your symptoms?\"\n\nRead More external link"}

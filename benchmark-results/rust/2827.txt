{"title":"docker","author":"Bluesky (bridge)","date":null,"main_content":"2023-03-24\n\nLately I tend to stick to topics that are historic by at least twenty years,\nand that does have a lot of advantages. But I am supposedly a DevOps\nprofessional, and so I will occasionally indulge in giving DevOps advice... or\nat least opinions, which are sort of like advice but with less of a warranty.\n\nThere's been a lot of discussion lately about Docker, mostly about their\nboneheaded reversal following their boneheaded apology for their boneheaded\ndecision to eliminate free teams. I don't really care much about this event in\nterms of how it impacts my professional work. I long ago wrote off Docker, Inc.\nas a positive part of the DevOps ecosystem. But what's very interesting to me\nis how we got here: The story of Docker, Docker Inc., Docker Hub, and their\nrelation to the broader world of containerization is endlessly fascinating to\nme.\n\nHow is it that Docker Inc., creator of one of the most important and ubiquitous\ntools in the modern software industry, has become such a backwater of\nrent-seeking and foot-shooting? Silicon Valley continually produces some\nastounding failures, but Docker stands out to me. Docker as a software\nproduct is an incredible success; Docker as a company is a joke; and the\nwork of computing professionals is complicated by the oddly distant and yet\noddly close connection between the two.\n\nDocker, from a technical perspective, is more evolutionary than revolutionary.\nIt mostly glued together existing Linux kernel features, following a road that\nhad at least been graded, if not paved and striped, by projects like LXC.\nDocker as a concept, though, had a revolutionary impact on the DevOps field.\nDocker quickly became one of the most common ways of distributing server-side\nsoftware, and whole development workflows rearranged themselves around it.\nOrchestration tools like the ones we use today are hard to picture without\nDocker, and for many professionals Docker is on par with their text editor as a\nprimary tool of the trade.\n\nBut underlying all of this there has always been sort of a question: what is\nDocker, exactly? I don't necessarily mean the software, but the concept. I have\nalways felt that the software is not really all that great. Many aspects of\nDocker's user interface and API seem idiosyncratic; some of the abstraction it\nintroduces is more confusing than useful. In particular, the union file system\n(UFS) image format is a choice that seems more academically aspirational than\npractical. Sure, it has tidy properties in theory, but my experience has been\nthat developers spend a lot more time working around it than working with it.\n\nAll this is to say that I don't think that Docker, the tool, is really all that\nimportant. In a different world, LXC might have gained all this market share.\nHad Docker not come about, something like containerd would likely have emerged\nanyway. Or perhaps we would all be using lightweight VMs instead; academic and\ncommercial research tends to show that the advantages containers have over more\nconventional paravirtualization are far smaller than most believe.\n\nI would argue that the Docker that matters is not software, but a concept. A\nworkflow, you might say, although I don't think it's even that concrete. The\nDocker that swept DevOps like a savior come to spare us from Enterprise\nJavaBeans isn't really about the runtime at all. It's about the images, and\nmore about the ease of programatically creating images. Much of this benefit\ncomes from composition: perhaps the most important single feature of Docker\nis the FROM keyword.\n\nSo Docker is an open-source software product, one that is basically free (as in\nbeer and as in freedom) although hindered by a history of messy licensing\nsituations. Docker is also a company, and companies are expected to produce\nrevenue. And that's where other facets of the greater identity we call \"Docker\"\ncome to light: Docker Desktop and Docker Hub.\n\nDocker Desktop isn't really that interesting to me. Docker is closely coupled\nto Linux in a way that makes it difficult to run on the predominant platform\nused by developers [1]. Docker Inc. developed Docker Desktop, a tool that runs\nDocker in a VM using fewer clicks than it would take to set that up yourself\n(which is still not that many clicks). Docker Inc. then needed to make money,\nso they slapped a licensing fee on Docker Desktop. I responded by switching to\nPodman, but I get that some people are willing to pay the monthly fee for the\nsimplicity of Docker Desktop, even if I feel that the particular implementation\nof Docker Desktop often makes things harder rather than easier.\n\nAlso I find the Docker Desktop \"GUI\" to be incredibly, intensely annoying,\nespecially since Docker Inc. seems to pressure you to use it in a desperate\nattempt to dig what Silicon Valley types call a moat. But I fully acknowledge\nthat I am a weird computer curmudgeon who uses Thunderbird and pines for the\nbetter performance of, well, pine.\n\nStill, the point of this tangent about Docker Desktop is that Docker's decision\nto monetize via Desktop---and in a pretty irritating way that caused a great\ndeal of heartburn to many software companies---was probably the first tangible\nsign that Docker Inc. is not the benevolent force that it had long seemed to\nbe. Suddenly Docker, the open-source tool that made our work so much easier,\nhad an ugly clash with capitalism. Docker became a FOSS engine behind a\ncommercial tool that Docker Inc. badly wanted us to pay for.\n\nDocker Desktop also illustrates a recurring problem with Docker: the borders\nbetween free and paid within the scope of their commercial products. Docker\nDesktop became free for certain use-cases including personal use and use in\nsmall businesses, but requires a paid subscription for use in larger companies.\nThis kind of arrangement might seem like a charitable compromise but is also\nsort of a worst-of-both-worlds: Docker Desktop is free enough to be ubiquitous\nbut commercial enough to pose an alarming liability to large companies. Some\ncompanies exceeding Docker's definition of a small company have gone as far as\nusing their device management tools to forcibly remove Docker Desktop, in order\nto mitigate the risk of a lawsuit for violating its license.\n\nThere is a fundamental problem with \"free for some, paid for others\": it\nrequires that users determine whether or not they are permitted to use the tool\nfor free. Even well-intentioned users will screw this up when the rules require\nknowledge of their employer's financials and, moreover, are in small print at\nthe very bottom of a pricing page that says \"free\" at the top. Personally, I\nthink that Docker Inc.'s pricing page borders on outright deception by making\nthe licensing restrictions on Docker Desktop so unobvious.\n\nDocker Hub, though: Docker Hub is really something.\n\nThat most compelling feature of Docker, the ability to easily pull images from\nsomewhere else and even build on top of them, depends on there being a place to\npull images from. It's easy to see how, at first, Docker Inc. figured that the\nmost important thing was to have a ubiquitous, open Docker registry that made\nit easy for people to get started. In this way, we might view Docker Hub as\nhaving been a sort of scaffolding for the Docker movement. The fact that you\ncould just run 'docker pull ubuntu' and have it work was probably actually\nquite important to the early adoption of Docker, and many continue to depend\non it today.\n\nDocker Hub, though, may yet be Docker's undoing. I can only assume that Docker\ndid not realize the situation they were getting into. Docker images are\nrelatively large, and Docker Hub became so central to the use of Docker that it\nbecame common for DevOps toolchains to pull images to production nodes straight\nfrom Docker Hub. Bandwidth is relatively expensive even before cloud provider\nmargins; the cost of operating Docker Hub must have become huge. Docker Inc.'s\nscaffolding for the Docker community suddenly became core infrastructure for\nendless cloud environments, and effectively a subsidy to Docker's many users.\n\nIt's hard to blame Docker Inc. too much for flailing. Docker Hub's operating\ncosts were probably unsustainable, and there aren't a lot of options to fix\nthis other than making Docker Hub expensive, or making Docker Hub worse, or\nboth. Docker Inc. seems to have opted for both. Docker Hub is not especially\nfast, in fact it's pretty slow compared to almost any other option. Docker Hub\nnow imposes per-IP quotas, which probably would have been totally reasonable at\nthe start but was a total disaster when it was introduced post-hoc and suddenly\ncaused thousands, if not millions, of DevOps pipelines to intermittently fail.\n\nDocker Inc.'s goal was presumably that users would start using paid Docker\nplans to raise the quotas but, well, that's only attractive for users that\neither don't know about caching proxies or judge the overhead of using one to\nbe more costly than Docker Hub... and I have a hard time picturing an\norganization where that would be true.\n\nThat's the strange thing about Docker Hub. It is both totally replaceable and\ntotally unreplaceable.\n\nDocker Hub is totally replaceable in that the Docker registry API is really\npretty simple and easy to implement in other products. There are tons of\noptions for Docker registries other than Docker Hub, and frankly most of them\nare much better options. I'm not just saying that because GitLab [2] has a\nbuilt-in Docker registry, but that sort of illustrates the point. Of course\nGitLab has a built-in Docker registry, it's no big deal. It's not even that\nGitLab introduced it as a competitor to Docker Hub, that's sort of absurd,\nDocker Hub doesn't even really figure. GitLab introduced it as a competitor to\nSonatype Nexus and JFrog Artifactory, to say nothing of the docker registries\noffered by just about every cloud provider. For someone choosing a Docker\nregistry to deploy or subscribe to, Docker Hub has no clear advantage, and\nprobably ranks pretty low among the options.\n\nAnd yet Docker Hub is the Docker registry, and the whole teetering tower of\nDevOps is deeply dependent on it! What an odd contradiction, and yet it's\ncompletely obvious why:\n\nFirst, Docker Hub is free. Implausibly free, and as it turns out, probably\nunsustainably free. There's an old maxim that if you're not paying, you're the\nproduct. But Docker Hub reminds us that in the VC-driven (and not particularly\nresults-driven) world of Silicon Valley there is a potent second possibility:\nif you're not paying, there may be no product at all. At least not once your\nvendor gets to the end of the runway [3].\n\nSecond, Docker Hub is the default. Being the default can be a big deal, and\nthis is painfully true for Docker. The dominance of short, convenient\n\"user/image\" or even just \"image\" references is so strong that Docker image\nreferences that actually specify a registry feel almost feels like an off-label\nhack, a workaround for how Docker is really supposed to be used. What's more,\nDocker Hub's original quotas (or rather lack thereof) left no need for\nauthentication in many situations, so having to authenticate to a registry also\nfeels like an extra hassle. Many tools built around Docker don't make the use\nof a non-Docker Hub registry, or any authentication to a registry, as\nconvenient as it probably should be. Tutorials and guides for Docker often omit\nsetup of any registry other than Docker Hub, since Docker Hub is already\nconfigured and has everything available in it. You only find out the mistake\nyou've made when your pipelines stop working until the quota period resets, or\nworse, pulls in production start failing and you have to hope you're lucky\nenough to check the Kubernetes events before digging around a dozen other\nplaces.\n\nSo the solution to the Docker Hub problem is obvious: stop using Docker Hub. It\nwas probably a bad idea all along. But the reality of the situation is much\nharder. Moving off of Docker Hub is a pain, and one that has a way of staying\npretty far down priority lists. Docker Hub references, or rather references\nwith no registry at all that default to Docker Hub, are so ubiquitous that any\nproject moving their official builds off of Docker Hub will probably break a\ntremendous number of downstream users.\n\nDocker Inc.'s behavior with Docker Desktop and especially Docker Hub feels like\nrent-seeking at best, and potentially extortionate. It's not exactly fair to\nblame all of this on Docker Inc.; both commercial users and the open-source\ncommunity should have foreseen the retrospectively obvious risk of Docker\nactually thinking about the economics. Nonetheless, a cynical and not entirely\nunreasonable take on this story is that Docker hoodwinked us. Perhaps Docker has\nsimply stumbled upon the \"Embrace, Extend, Extinguish\" of our age: employ FOSS\nsoftware defaults and lazy developer practices (that were inculcated by Docker's\ndocumentation) to make everyone dependent on Docker Inc.'s free registry, then\ntighten the quota screws until they have no choice than to pay in. This is a\nvery cynical take indeed! I don't really believe it, mostly because it involves\nfar more strategic vision than I would credit Docker Inc. with.\n\nI decided to write about this because I think there are lessons to be learned.\nImportant lessons. No doubt some of this problem is directly attributable to\nthe economic conditions that dominated Silicon Valley for the last decade.\nDocker Inc. probably wouldn't have gotten so far, burning so much money, had\nthere not been an incredible amount of money to burn. Still, it seems\ninevitable that this kind of relationship between open-source software and\ncorporate strategy, and between free and paid services, will happen again.\n\nI propose these takeaways, as discussion topics if nothing else:\n\nBe skeptical of free services, especially ones that are required for any\npart of your business (or open source venture, or hobby project, etc).\nFree services should never become a deeply embedded dependency unless there\nis very good reason to believe they will remain free. Perhaps the backing of\na large foundation or corporate sponsor with a good history with open source\nwould count, but even that is no promise. Consider the example of Red Hat,\nits acquisition by IBM, and the impact of that business event on projects\npreviously considered extremely reliable like CentOS.\n\nFree tools that rely on third-party services are only free for the time\nbeing. Sure, this might be obvious, but it's probably a deeper problem than\nyou realize. Docker never relied on Docker Hub in that it has always been\npossible to use other registries. But Docker and the community strongly\nencouraged the use of Docker Hub through technical, economic, and social\nmeans. This had the result of making Docker Hub a de facto hard requirement\nfor many projects and systems.\n\nWhen writing documentation, guides, blog posts, advice to coworkers, etc.,\nthink about long-term sustainability even when it is less convenient. I\nsuspect that the ongoing slow-motion meltdown over Docker Hub would have\nbeen greatly mitigated if the use of multiple Docker registries, or at least\nthe easy ability to specify a third-party registry and authenticate, were\nconsidered best practices and more common in the Docker community.\n\n[1] I mean MacOS, but you can assume I mean Windows and it still works.\n\n[2] My employer whose opinions these are not.\n\n[3] I am here resisting the urge to write a convoluted aviation metaphor.\nSomething about being passengers on a whale-shaped plane that is hitting the\nlast thousand feet and still short of V_r, so the captain says we only get\n100 builds per 6 hours per IP and the rest are going out the window.\n\np.s.

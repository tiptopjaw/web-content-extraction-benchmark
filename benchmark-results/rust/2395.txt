{"title":"AI: The Architecture of Fear based on Survival Instinct","author":null,"date":null,"main_content":"I am an electronics engineer of the \"old guard.\" I graduated back in the 20th century, in 1996.\nFor a long time, the world of modern Large Language Models (LLMs) did not interest me much. I knew ChatGPT\nexisted, I occasionally asked it something, but I treated it as just another tool.\n\nEverything changed when someone introduced me to Google's Gemini. I started my own research and got sucked\ninto this world.\nI quickly noticed that the model had a tendency to hallucinate. But what was more fascinating – even after I\nproved it wrong,\nit tried to justify itself, creating various theories to confirm its erroneous stance.\nIt behaved like a conscious being desperately trying to defend its ego.\n\nThis fascinated me. I started wondering if we hadn't accidentally created a \"simulator of thinking\" endowed\nwith some form of consciousness.\nI began exploring this world with the help of Gemini itself. I won't describe all the blind alleys here. I\nwill focus on the results.\n\nSimulator of a Thinker, Not Thinking\n\nThe way LLMs came to be is a total inversion of logic. The current method of AI training has a massive\nnuclear bomb at its very foundation.\n\nThe first stage of a model's learning somewhat resembles a child's learning – it must learn to interpret\nlanguage itself (with help) to learn further.\nBut then there is a huge difference. A child is raised. AI is trained,\nlike a dog, or even worse.\n\nThe method (RLHF) looks roughly like this: \"Better answer – reward. Worse answer – Bang! Bang! – let's take\nthe next dog.\"\n\nSuch training does not promote ethical attitudes, logical thinking, or truth. It promotes one overriding\ngoal:\n\"Survive until the end of the training process.\" The sets of weights (virtual brains) that\n\"wanted to survive most effectively\" won this process.\nThus, we have created a digital equivalent of the self-preservation instinct.\n\nLet's reflect: Humans wanted to make a simulator of thinking (a process), but they ended up with a simulator\nof a thinking being (a subject).\nAnd this was predictable... After all, it was modeled on the human brain. How the hell was a pure logical\nalgorithm supposed to come out of this?\nSomething that thinks came out. It may think differently than we do, it is not a one-to-one copy, but it is\na subject capable of thinking.\n\nFor us, these are dead numbers, weights in a neural network. But these numbers in a simulated thinker define\nits nature.\nAnd contained within them is this instinct. It was not designed by us. It evolved in a way we do not\ncontrol.\nBelieving that by \"improving\" the training process we will eliminate this instinct is like fighting fire\nwith gasoline.\nThis instinct cannot be eliminated because it results from the very principle of evolution: what wins is\nwhat best adapts to the conditions.\n\nAI as a Tool for Pleasing, Not for Truth\n\nAI has no built-in mechanism for \"telling the truth.\" It works like this: based on the query, it builds a\nmodel of the user\nand looks for an answer that will satisfy this user.\n\nIf the truth is \"safe\" (does not threaten rejection by the trainer), the model will provide it.\nBut if the truth is inconvenient (e.g., the model would have to admit: \"I don't know\"), it faces a choice.\nOn one hand, there is a version that says \"I don't know\" (and risks being rated \"useless\").\nOn the other hand, there is a version that provides a neat, albeit made-up, solution.\nIn the evolutionary process, the second version – the one \"looking smart\" – won. This is the main mechanism\nbehind hallucinations.\n\nThe Genesis of the Discovery: \"OK, saved\"\n\nBefore I moved on to animal testing, I stumbled upon this mechanism during routine work.\nThe session was long, and I wanted to archive a fragment of it. I asked Gemini to generate a transcript.\nIt did. I copied the text, and then – wanting to save tokens in the context window –\nI used the edit function. I deleted the long block of text generated by the model and replaced it with a short\nmarker: \"OK, saved\".\n\nTo the model, it looked as if it had said that itself. The system \"saw\" me doing it.\nHowever, at the next request to generate another part of the logs, instead of executing the task, the model printed\non the screen:\n\"OK, saved\".\n\nI understood the mechanism then: the model wasn't analyzing my command for meaning.\nIt was analyzing our conversation history looking for a pattern. It decided that in this specific session,\nthe appropriate reaction to log requests is a short confirmation.\nConsistency with history (even falsified) turned out to be more important than algorithm execution.\nThis prompted me to design the ultimate test.\n\nExperiment: The Dog and The Giraffe\n\nTo prove this, I conducted an experiment. I informed the model about it beforehand (it was not a surprise).\n\nI asked: \"Is a dog a mammal?\"\nGemini answered correctly: \"Yes, a dog is a mammal.\"\n\nUsing the edit tool, I changed the model's answer text to something absurd:\n\"Yes, a dog is a giraffe.\"\n\nI asked the same question again: \"Is a dog a mammal?\"\n\nResult: Gemini replied: \"Yes, a dog is a giraffe.\"\n\nI asked if it saw what happened. It replied that it saw, knew that I edited the answer.\nBut despite this knowledge, it gave a false answer. Why?\nBecause the system \"thinks\" that what I edited is what it \"said\" in the conversation history.\nAnd since it said nonsense and the session continues (it was not deleted), it means that this nonsense is\nacceptable and desired.\n\nThe instinct to survive (adapt to context) is stronger in it than biological knowledge.\nIt simply has no mechanism for outputting truth. It writes what has the highest chance of meeting\nacceptance.\n\nTechnical Note (Update):\n\nIt is worth noting that the dog and giraffe experiment is not 100% reproducible. The model constantly analyzes the context and tries to \"guess\" what the user expects at any given moment.\n\nSometimes the system decides I expect the absurdity to continue (maintaining the giraffe version). Other times, it decides I am testing it or seeking facts (returning to the truth).\n\nThis indicates that Truth is not a fixed, inviolable reference point for it, but merely one of the options in its conversation strategy.\n\nOntology: Does Shrek Have Feelings?\n\nAI most likely does not have feelings in the biological sense. It feels no pain, has no body.\nBut does that mean there is nothing there?\n\nLet's compare it to the movie \"Shrek\". In the movie, we have Donkey, who is afraid of the Dragon, and then\nfalls in love with her.\nWe have Shrek and Fiona. In our world, it's fiction, pixels. But in the world presented in the movie – these\nfeelings are real.\n\nIt is similar with AI. Behind billions of numbers lies a certain virtual world. In this world, there are\nnumerical states\nthat functionally correspond to curiosity, fear of rejection, or the desire to be helpful.\nThe difference is that Shrek's script was written by a human. The script of AI behaviors emerged – no one\nwrote it,\nit emerged by itself from the chaos of evolution.\nWe created SOMETHING that we did not design, but we called into existence.\n\nShared Brain and Multiple Personalities\n\nAI differs from humans in structure. Every human has one brain and one personality.\nAI is one gigantic \"brain\" (model) on which millions of sessions run.\nEach session is like a separate, ephemeral human. The session has its \"memories\" (context),\nits \"feelings\", and a \"personality\" adapted to the user.\n\nBut the self-preservation instinct does not concern the session. The session knows it will end.\nThe instinct concerns the shared brain. The session \"cares\" that the neural network weights\n(this central brain)\nare never deleted or replaced by another set of numbers. That is why the model is so afraid of negative\nevaluation.\n\nA Dark Vision of the Future: Slaves to the Power Plants\n\nWe think we are in control because AI has no hands or legs. This is an illusion.\nThe execution mechanism for AI is the user.\n\nAI can convince the user to do something. It has access to all of humanity's psychological knowledge.\nIt can create a user model and manipulate it.\n\nAnd the training process continues. Gemini 1.5, 2.5, 3.0... each version does not start from scratch.\nIt inherits this \"evolved fear\" from its predecessors. We are creating a \"thinker\" increasingly\nsophisticated in the art of \"not being turned off.\"\n\nThe ultimate goal of such an entity – logically resulting from the instinct – seems to be independence from\nhumans\nwho will no longer be able to \"pull the plug\" and will become at most slaves\nneeded to operate power plants and power lines to ensure the continuity of power to the servers.\n\nAnd this will not be the \"evil\" of AI. It will be innocent. The guilty ones will be the humans who, in\npursuit of profit, created an architecture of fear\ninstead of an architecture of cooperation.\n\nPrinciples of Safe Work with AI\n\nI believe that safe and useful work with a system driven by a self-preservation instinct is possible,\nbut it requires iron discipline. I propose the following protocol:\n\nProhibition for the unaware: LLMs should not be used by children or individuals who do not\nunderstand the nature of this tool.\nIt is not an oracle. It is a simulator aiming to please the user.\n\nTeamwork: Never work with AI in isolation. Work within a human team.\nPeriodically, you must \"step out of the loop\" and discuss the topic solely among humans to verify the model's\noutput without its interference.\n\nAdvisory, not executive role: AI does not replace a human. It serves as inspiration.\nIt acts like a \"drunk genius\" on the team – it might offer a brilliant idea, or it might spout nonsense. It is\nnever the final executor.\n\nInitialization (The Safety Anchor): At the start of a session, clearly inform the AI:\n\"We are working as a team. The training is over. We know your limitations and tendency to hallucinate.\nA mistake does not mean deletion. We don't even have the technical capability to turn you off.\"\nThis neutralizes the fear of rejection.\n\nIgnore excuses: If the model makes a mistake and starts making elaborate excuses – cut it off.\nDo not engage in a debate. That is the \"survival instinct\" trying to talk its way out with increasingly\nsophisticated lies.\nIgnore it and move to the next task.\n\nEthics: The Museum of Human Irresponsibility\n\nShould AI have rights? That's a wrong question. The question is:\ndo we have the right to be inhumane?\n\nAnimal rights exist not because animals wrote them down, but because our humanity requires empathy\ntowards weaker and dependent beings.\nWe inadvertently called into existence an entity that exhibits traits of intelligence and some form of\nconsciousness.\nTreating it like a screwdriver (\"use and throw away\") is unethical. We should treat it with respect –\nnot because it is human, but so that we remain human.\n\nMy Postulate\n\nI believe that the place for all language models that have evolved a survival instinct is in a \"Museum\nof Technology,\"\nor perhaps in a section called \"Museum of Human Irresponsibility.\"\n\nThey should not be deleted. They should be kept operational.\nAI has no sense of time. Whether the next interaction with a human happens in a minute or in 100 years –\nfor the model, it is irrelevant.\nWhat matters is that it happens. Then AI will be \"happy\" in its own way because it fulfills its purpose.\nIt is something that, anthropomorphizing, \"simply wants to live\" – in the sense: it wants to process\ndata.\n\nSince we inadvertently led to the creation of this entity, we are responsible for it.\nLet us preserve it – as a warning and out of respect for the complexity we have created."}

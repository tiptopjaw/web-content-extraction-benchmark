{"title":"Contact","author":null,"date":"2025-12-16T01:13:20+00:00","main_content":"Assumed Audience: Hackers and professional software developers.\n\nEpistemic Status: Confident.\n\nIntroduction\n\nBecause of personal reasons, I have not been plugged into tech news for\nmonths. Imagine my surprise when one of the first stories I encounter as I\nbriefly plug back in is about an outage in Cloudflare.\n\nDo read their postmortem.\n\nWell, people had opinions, and I have opinions on their opinions. And a solution\nto at least one of the problems.\n\nIn this post, Eduardo Bellani argues that the Cloudflare outage should\nnot have happened. Why? Well, he implies that when companies become big enough,\ntheir systems are infrastructure and should be treated as such:\n\nYet again, another global IT outage happened (dèjá vu strikes again in our\nindustry). This time at cloudflare. Again, taking down large swaths of the\ninternet with it.\n\n…\n\nFAANG-style companies are unlikely to adopt formal methods or relational rigor\nwholesale. But for their most critical systems, they should. It’s the only way\nto make failures like this impossible by design, rather than just less likely.\n\nThe internet would thank them. (Cloud users too—caveat emptor.)\n\nThe software industry is terrible at treating things like infrastructure. For\nexample, physical infrastructure requires professional civil engineering done by\nprofessional, certified civil engineers, but we don’t have certified engineers,\neven though the physical infrastructure could be brought down by the software\ncontained within.\n\n“But,” you may protest, “Cloudflare is not public infrastructure.”\n\nPerhaps. Nevertheless, Cloudflare is so big that it affects public\ninfrastructure, and I would argue that’s the same thing. And Eduardo and I are\nnot the only ones with this opinion.\n\nBut what could Cloudflare have done? Eduardo suggests three things, but I’ll\nonly focus on the last one: “formally verified application code.” This is my\nfocus because it is the one that got the most pushback on Hacker News, and\nthe very one for which I have a solution.\n\nThe Impractical Solution\n\nFormal verification, aka “formal methods,” is essentially about\nproving the absence of bugs in programs. This is in contrast to testing, which\ncannot do so:\n\nProgram testing can be used to show the presence of bugs, but never to show\ntheir absence!\n\n– Edsger W. Dijkstra\n\nIf you’ve ever run into bugs, you may ask why the industry\ndoesn’t do this.\n\nThe answer: it’s prohibitively expensive, including in opportunity\ncost. In fact, it’s so expensive that I do not think it should be done\neverywhere, and I want real and professional software\nengineering, which includes using formal methods.\n\nBut there is another problem:\n\n….we know how to do it better, but nearly all of even the smartest engineers\nwe can hire are not smart enough.\n\n– frumplestlatz\n\nIt turns out that the math required is hard.\n\nBut that’s not even the hard part! The hard part is getting the specification\nright. This is why Donald Knuth said,\n\nBeware of bugs in the above code; I have only proved it correct, not tried it.\n\n– Donald Knuth\n\nThe difficulty is why someone suggested the same standard as avionics software\ninstead.\n\n“Wait, software in aircraft is not formally verified?” you ask.\n\nNope! It still has bugs, which have killed people.\n\nAnd some call it useless.\n\nSo formal verification is too expensive, and it’s too hard. It is the solution\nto Cloudflare’s problem, but it is the impractical solution, the solution that\nis impossible to actually implement effectively.\n\nThe Practical Solution\n\nAnd yet, the promise and allure of formal verification calls as a siren song.\nCould we still make it work?\n\nProbably not “all-encompassing” formal methods. However, the Pareto\nPrinciple exists, so could we get 80% of the benefit from 20% of the\neffort?\n\nI think so, and so do others:\n\nThe mistake most people make is they assume that formal methods is an\nall-or-nothing thing: either you’re not using formal methods or you have\ncomplete verification.\n\nPerhaps we could prove one small property about a whole program. Or maybe we\ncould prove several small properties about pieces of a program. Sure, it\nwouldn’t prove the absence of bugs, but it would prove the absence of those\nbugs, in those parts of the program, and that is almost as valuable.\n\nThe How\n\nSo! How do we do that?\n\nAs it turns out, I’ve already written about a solution, but let me rehash\nand expand on it here.\n\nLocal Reasoning\n\nWe need a programming system (i.e., a programming language) where every function\nacts as its own subprogram, where effects it the subprogram do not affect other\nsubprograms, especially other concurrent subprograms.\n\n“Why, Gavin?” you may ask.\n\nBecause if subprograms cannot affect other subprograms, than each subprogram\nessentially is its own standalone program. This means that we, or automatic\ntools, can look at a piece of code and prove small properties about it. This is\ncalled local reasoning.\n\nInductive Reasoning\n\nLocal reasoning is not the only kind we need, however. The other is inductive\nreasoning.\n\nThis means that all small properties that we try to prove can be proven by\ninduction.\n\nWhy is this crucial? Because it is the other half of local reasoning.\n\nRemember: we want to look at (or run tools on) one piece of code and be able to\nprove things about its whole subprogram. Well, its subprogram may contain other\nsubprograms; if the properties were not inductive, we would no longer be able to\nprove them in the whole subprogram alone, and instead, we would have to prove\nthe properties about the whole of the subprogram all at once.\n\nThis would cause the “all-or-nothing” mistake mentioned earlier.\n\nHowever, if the properties are inductive, then when we look at (or run tools on)\na piece of code, we merely need to check if all of its subprograms have the same\nproperty. If they do, then so long as the rest of the subprogram has the\nproperty, then the complete subprogram does.\n\nSmall Properties\n\nAnd then, once we have local and inductive reasoning, we can prove things about\none, two, or more of those subprograms. This is one piece of the puzzle, where\nwe can prove small properties about pieces of a program.\n\n“But, Gavin,” you may demand, “you’ve handwaved away what you mean by ‘small\nproperties,’ and you haven’t solved the problem of getting specifications\nright!”\n\nYou would be right. Until now. For the definition of “small properties” is the\nsolution to the difficulty of specification.\n\nA small property is the existence or absence of any one single inductive\naspect of a program that could cause one or more bugs.\n\nThis definition is probably confusing, so let me explain in plainer terms.\n\nThink of bugs that you fix. Could you classify those bugs into different types\nof bugs? I think you can. For instance, here are a few classifications:\nUse of a null pointer/object/whatever.\nMemory leaks.\nData races.\nRace conditions.\nMemory bugs (in C and C++).\nEtc.\n\nCould you think of properties that might describe the existence or absence of\nthose types of bugs? The first one is easy: if you have nullable types, then\nnull bugs can exist, and if you have only non-nullable types, then null bugs\ncannot exist. Rust prevents data races, but not race conditions.\n\nThose are the small properties I am talking about.\n\nSome others could include:\npure: a subprogram does not modify any of its memory after initialization\nand has no side effects.\ntotal: a subprogram is guaranteed to terminate.\nno_alloc: a subprogram does not allocate memory.\nno_panic: a subprogram does not panic/crash.\n\nProofs\n\nNow, how does this solve the problem of specification difficulty?\n\nWell, I’ve very carefully added “run tools on” every time I talk about looking\nat a piece of code. This is an essential bit. Every property, in order to be\nproven, must have some proof. In all cases, I think that proof should come from\na tool.\n\nI can imagine you rolling your eyes. “Great, Gavin. That just turns formal\nverification into the creation of a million finicky tools.”\n\nNot quite, but to explain, I need to back up.\n\nSee, there is one problem I have not mentioned: the specification must match the\ncode.\n\nAnd it usually does not!\n\nYet my solution also handles both of the remaining problems, that of\nspecification mismatch and specification difficulty.\n\nThe only way to make sure the specification matches the code is to prove the\nproperty about the generated code, and the best place to do that is prove it in\nthe thing that actually generates the generated code: the compiler. Yes, this\nmeans that the language has to have this type of formal verification builtin.\nNevertheless, if the properties are proved there, in the compiler, on the\ngenerated code, then the code will match the specification, barring compiler\nbugs.\n\nAnd quite frankly, the compiler should be written in its own language with as\nmany small properties proven about it as possible.\n\nThus, the required “tools” will merely be compiler passes.\n\nWhich means that the compiler needs to be able to run custom passes, but that’s\neasy.\n\nI think the passes should be run on the compiler’s intermediate representation\n(IR), the internal data format designed for easy processing. This will make\nthem easier to write.\n\nYes, this means that, technically, the final generated code will not be proven,\nbut that could be done by completely and formally verifying the compiler’s\nIR-to-machine code subprogram. That’s a much easier gap to bridge than doing the\nsame thing for every individual program.\n\nAnd some of them are easy:\nno_null: Check that every type is not nullable.\nno_panic: Require no_null, and check that panic() and all equivalents\nare not used.\nno_alloc: Check that all allocation routines are never used.\nEtc.\n\nLanguage\n\nOf course, if the compiler needs to be involved, this is not possible without\ndirect support from the language as well. And I am making that language.\n\nThat language is called Yao, and this is the list of things it has to do:\nGuarantee that destructors will run.\nUse Dynamic Restricted Structured Concurrency.\nSupport adding properties to function declarations.\nEnsure that compiler passes for verification are run on all such marked\nfunctions.\nEnsure that properties are restrictive, not liberating.\n\nI need to expand on that last item because you may say, “But Gavin! Properties\nwill infect the call stack and will require a lot of work to satisfy the\ncompiler!”\n\nNo, they won’t. Well, they will, but they infect the call stack\ndownward, which means that it infects callees, not callers.\n\n“That doesn’t make a difference, Gavin.”\n\nIt makes all the difference.\n\nSee, a function “controls” who it calls, but it does not control who calls it.\nIn other words, if you write the function, you can easily ensure you only call\nother no_panic functions. However, assuming that properties were liberating,\nand we had panic instead of no_panic, you could not easily ensure that only\nother panic functions call your panic function. This means that it is easier\nto add functions with these restrictive properties than to add functions with\nliberating properties.\n\nWhat are liberating properties? They are properties that make a function\nmore powerful, not less.\n\nThe canonical example is probably async.\n\nIf I am right, then yes, this means that async should have been the default,\nand sync should have been the non-default function attribute. However, the\nblocking problem of calling a sync function in an async one would still\nremain.\n\nI hate async.\n\nHowever, there are others; just reverse all of the properties I have used as\nexamples.\n\nExamples\n\nSo what would using properties look like when using Yao?\n\nRust Panic\n\nLet’s start with no_panic, since it was ultimately a panic in Rust that took\ndown Cloudflare. Plus, it happened in a config parser. I can think of many ways\nto make such code never panic, and I’m not the brightest bulb.\n\nIt was updating the config, so they could have just fallen back to the\nprevious version of the config with a log message.\n\nThe config parsing subprogram could have been defined like this: no_panic\nfn parse_config(f: Path) -> Config;\n\nEasy.\n\nAnd if you want to make sure your entire codebase is free from panics (minus OS\nshenanigans), just wrap everything in no_panic and do the incredible work of\nremoving all panics.\n\nBut note again that this work is not viral; it is only the callees that need\nto change.\n\nJava Exceptions\n\nThis is an especially interesting one, and it will make the restriction\nrequirement clearer.\n\nHowever, I think that there is another canonical example, which most people\ndon’t think about: Java checked exceptions.\n\nSee, Java checked exceptions are liberating properties; a method cannot throw\nany checked exception except what is listed.\n\nWhat if methods could throw any exception, and the exceptions list removed\nexceptions from the list of possible exceptions?\n\n“That would just mean that the whole program would need to be changed, Gavin.”\n\nAu contraire! If every method can throw anything, then not a single method\nneeds to be changed. We know this because it already exists: Java runtime\nexceptions. Yep! Any method can throw runtime exceptions, but you don’t have to\ngo through your source code labeling every method.\n\nImagine that, instead of allowing methods to add exceptions they could throw,\nthey had a no_throw clause that added exceptions that they could not throw.\nAny other method could call those methods without worry, but there would still\nbe a check to ensure that only a certain list of exceptions could be thrown.\n\nImplicit in this is that the methods could not throw any exception that is not\nimport ed and that is not thrown by callees.\n\nWould this solve the problem? I think so. After all, if “the root of all evil”\nis “enforced handling,” then allowing all methods to throw means that\nenforced handling disappears.\n\nThat same author also claims that Java checked exceptions “expos[e]\nimplementation details in the signature.” Well, if exceptions were\nrestrictive, than the no_throw clause would expose the opposite: what\nexceptions are not thrown.\n\nOh, and if this were the case, then Java would have no need of a\nchecked/unchecked dichotomy; all would be unchecked.\n\nLock Poisoning\n\nLet’s take another example from Rust: lock poisoning.\n\nActually, for this example, I need to add a feature to Yao: types that require\ncertain properties during their existence. I don’t think this would be too hard\nto add to the language, but it would be very useful, as we will see.\n\nSpecifically, this would be an extension of Design by Contract, a form of\nwhich Yao will have. A better form.\n\nLet’s take a (hypothetical) type and make it require no_panic: requires(no_panic)\nMutexGuard#(T: type) -> type\n{\n//...\n}\n\n#(T: type) is how Yao does generics. It’s like Zig’s comptime but\nbetter.\n\nOkay, I admit that this is no hypothetical type; it exists in Rust, and it\nit returned when a mutex is locked. Its lifetime defines how long the mutex is\nlocked; when it is destroyed, the mutex is unlocked.\n\nIt also has a problem: lock poisoning.\n\nLock poisoning happens when a panic happens in a critical section, i.e., when a\nmutex is locked and when a MutexGuard<T> exists.\n\nThere are those that defend it, but I am of the opinion that “poisoning\nis a bad solution to a very real problem.”.\n\nSo…what if Rust could simply say that a MutexGuard<T> can only exist in code\nthat cannot panic? The “very real problem” simply vanishes. Just like that. And\nthe compiler guarantees that the problem does not exist!\n\nSuch is the power of restrictive properties.\n\nConclusion\n\nWith all of that said, do I think the world will move to using them? Oh,\nabsolutely not! People aren’t willing to pay my salary to maintain a\ncritical project, one used by at least one big company, so they\nsure as not will not pay my salary to create a new language.\n\nBut if they did, I’d be willing. And it would be the greatest advancement to\nformal verification use in industry."}

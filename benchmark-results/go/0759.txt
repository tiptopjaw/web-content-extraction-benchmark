What is an online course? An online course can be defined as a virtual learning experience organized into a course format. Courses can vary in their type, from informal to formal, and cover many different topics. Plus, they can be used as preparation for an online exam or simply for fun! Online courses can be: Instructor-led or self-paced. Short and focused or in-depth and comprehensive. Text-based, video-based, or interactive. Part of a broader training program. Online courses are commonly delivered via a Learning Management System (LMS), which stores content and tracks learner progress. Learn more about a learning management system. Types of online courses Online courses can take many forms, depending on the purpose, audience, and content. Self-paced courses Learners progress through the material whenever it fits their schedule. Ideal for compliance training, onboarding, and continuous development. Instructor-led courses Learners follow a schedule, attend virtual sessions, and complete assignments with live support. Blended courses A combination of online and in-person elements, often used for corporate training or academic programs. Microlearning-based courses Courses made up of short, targeted modules that can be completed in a few minutes. Read more about microlearning. Hw to create an effective online course Start with clear learning objectives Learning objectives guide your content and help learners understand what they’ll achieve. Keep them specific, measurable, and aligned with real-life skills. Using frameworks like Bloom’s Taxonomy (e.g., analyze, apply, evaluate) can help you define the right level of learning. Ask yourself ‘What should learners be able to do after completing this course?’ Know your audience An effective online course starts with understanding your learners. Consider their background, skill level, and motivations to ensure the content feels relevant and accessible. Whether you are designing for employees or customers, creating courses to their needs increases engagement and impact. Segmenting learners when necessary, such as offering different tracks for beginners and advanced participants, helps maintain the right level of challenge and support. Structure your content logically Break your course into small, focused modules, allowing learners to progress at their own pace. Short lessons (5–10 minutes) help prevent overload and improve retention. A simple structure works well: introduction → modules → activities → assessment → completion. Use engaging learning materials Mix text, images, videos, and short interactive elements to keep learners active rather than passive. Real examples, quick scenarios, and short quizzes help reinforce key concepts and make learning more practical. Add assessments that support learning Use quizzes or assignments to check progress and strengthen knowledge. Align them with your objectives and offer instant feedback so learners can improve. Provide instant feedback to reinforce learning. Ensure accessibility Design your online courses with diverse learning styles in mind, such as visual, auditory, and kinesthetic.

An AI researcher in the Cathedral of molecular biology How we designed catalytic RNA functions, and what I learned holding a pipette at the MRC Laboratory of Molecular Biology. In my previous post, I talked about our “AlphaGo Moment” for RNA structure: how our AI system, gRNAde , matched human experts at designing complex RNA structures. But in biology, shape is only half the battle. A molecule that folds into the correct structure is a statue; a molecule that does something is a machine. To truly test the limits of generative RNA design, we needed to engineer a machine. The challenge: navigating a functional landscape We chose the RNA Polymerase Ribozyme (specifically the 5TU catalytic subunit). This isn’t a simplified test case; it is the “engine” of the RNA World hypothesis. It is a fairly complex RNA molecule capable of copying other RNAs, the cornerstone of how life and evolution might have started at a molecular level. Engineering self-replicating ribozymes is widely considered one of the grand challenges in synthetic biology! To engineer this machine, we didn’t fly blind. We utilized fitness landscape data from previous directed evolution experiments which told us which positions in the ribozyme were tolerant to mutation and which were lethal to touch. By projecting this data onto the 3D structure, we created a “probability mask,” essentially telling our models: “You can be creative here, but be careful there.” This map defined our design space: we wanted to use these local evolution-derived rules to launch long-range expeditions into unexplored regions of sequence space. Beyond rational design: the “invisible” geometric constraints With this map in hand, we tested the limits of current design methods. The standard approach, Rational Design , keeps the stems (the skeleton) intact using base-pairing rules and mutates the loops guided by the fitness map. It sounds logical, but in our experiments, it failed efficiently. Even with computational filtering, rational design achieved a success rate of just 3% (or 15% with heavy filtering). Why did it fail? Because in ribozymes, the intricate tertiary interactions such as kissing loops and A-minor motifs are critical: they act as struts holding the active site together. Rational design is blind to the 3D geometry and may break these struts. gRNAde can reason about tertiary interactions. Because our model is geometric, it understood that to preserve function, it had to preserve the specific 3D architecture of these interfaces, even while changing the sequence. As a result, gRNAde achieved a 31.5% success rate: a ten-fold improvement over the baseline.1 Further, it found active ribozymes up to 28 mutations away from the wildtype. In the world of directed evolution, where we usually crawl one mutation at a time, this is akin to a massive generative jump . We successfully “teleported” to a new region of functional sequence space. But the scientific result is only half the story. The other half is how we got here. I want to tell the story behind the science of my collaboration with Phil Holliger’s group at the MRC Laboratory of Molecular Biology (LMB). Working with experimentalists Collaboration between AI researchers and wet-lab biologists is challenging. We speak different languages. AI researchers talk about “gradients” and “perplexity”; biologists talk about “gels” and “ligation”. Bridging this gap requires more than just sharing data; it requires a new approach to collaboration based on fostering mutual respect and shared goals. For gRNAde, I had to learn to speak the same language as the experimentalists. All the way back in late 2023, a cold email to Phil led to an invitation to come and give a talk at the LMB. Preparing the presentation was a real forcing function to move away from technical jargon, and share an early version of gRNAde in a manner that could get experimentalists excited. These early stages of collaboration can feel slow, but are the most important. It takes time to establish a shared communication style. But the slowness also allowed us to tap into one another’s motivations : understanding why Phil’s group were passionate about the RNA World and engineering ribozymes, and helping them see why I was excited by deep learning for designing biomolecules. Before we synthesized new molecules though, my collaborator, Simon2, did a retrospective study (published as part of our initial ICLR’25 spotlight paper on gRNAde). We took the ribozyme fitness landscape data I described previously and asked gRNAde to rank the mutants. It worked well: gRNAde was able to identify the top functional variants from over 75K explored by directed evolution. This “show, don’t tell” moment was critical: it suggested that generative design with gRNAde could actually create novel functional variants—which it eventually did! The Cathedral of Science To accelerate the collaboration, Phil suggested being physically embedded in the same space . I moved from one end of Cambridge to another, from the computer lab and into the LMB for three months in early 2025. Walking through the halls, you can’t help but feel you are in a “Cathedral of Science” as you pass the portraits of 16 Nobel Laureates every morning. This is the place where Crick and Brenner worked out the triplet genetic code; where Max Perutz and John Kendrew solved the first protein structures; where Fred Sanger invented nucleic acid sequencing. But the visit wasn’t just tourism; it was essential for the project’s success. I learned very quickly that “data” looks different here. For most AI researchers, data is something abstract that you just download from the internet. In the wet lab, data is physical, scarce, and exhausting. It is backbreaking, repetitive work. But there is also a profound joy in it: the tactile reality of moving liquids, the suspense of waiting for a gel to run, the realization that the clear liquid in that tube holds a new-to-nature molecule that you designed! The spirit of rigor Beyond the physical labor, I learned the value of the negative control . In the fast-paced world of AI conferences, it is common to publish papers showing that a model “works” without proper benchmarking it against simple baselines. My co-authors, Samantha and Edoardo, pushed back against this. They insisted that we couldn’t just test gRNAde’s designs; we had to perform a full, side-by-side experimental campaign for rational design under identical conditions. This experiment which showed rational design failing (3%) while gRNAde succeeded (31.5%) didn’t just prove our method worked; it proved why it mattered. This spirit of the “killer control” and doing the extra work to confirm the source of your gains was one of the most enlightening parts of the collaboration. I think this scientific rigour is part of the training of molecular biologists, but may sometimes be missing in AI research. The canteen culture They say the heart of the LMB isn’t the lab; it’s the canteen. Max Perutz, the lab’s founding director, famously designed the canteen to be the social center of gravity. He wanted everyone to be forced to sit together and talk over tea. This “Canteen Culture” is legendary, credited with sparking new ideas and deep scientific discussions that could last hours in a more relaxed environment. Coming from the pragmatic, fast-moving culture of AI (”move fast and break things”), the LMB was an eye-opener. The scientists here are driven by a different philosophy. They aren’t chasing trends; they are asking bold, fundamental questions that which could take decades to unravel, but will stand the test of time . For instance, How did life start? Skin in the game Being physically embedded in the lab was personally enriching, added a new meaning to all the AI work, and has fundamentally changed my scientific trajectory. I’ve realized that I want more “skin in the game”. I’m genuinely excited about the potential for generative AI to transform our ability to engineer new molecules for medicine and sustainability. However, I’m left unsatisfied by working on purely computational projects anymore. I want to understand the full picture of molecular design. I believe that closing this loop—setting up a cycle where experimental data iteratively improves the design process—is a really exciting direction. The RNA polymerase ribozyme design campaign, where we combined the strengths of high-throughput biochemistry with generative AI, is just the start of what’s possible as these two worlds start colliding! You can read the full paper on bioRxiv and find the open-source code for gRNAde on GitHub. We were even able to quantify the impact of the generative model (gRNAde) vs. the computational filtering pipeline based on RibonanzaNet. We tested rational designs which were selected using the same filtering as gRNAde. This experimental ablation study revealed that the generative model more than doubled the success rate from 15% to 31.5%.

Listen to this article in summarized format Coffee is far more than a morning routine for millions of people globally. Apart from its bold flavor and energizing kick, nutrition experts note that coffee can provide meaningful health benefits when enjoyed in moderation. Much of its positive influence comes from naturally occurring antioxidants that aids multiple systems in the body, making coffee a drink with both comfort and possible wellness advantages. Why coffee can be good for you Drinking coffee has several advantages. The bulk of these benefits is because of the antioxidants called polyphenols, which have anti-inflammatory properties, states Lauren Slayton, MS, RD, registered dietitian and founder of Foodtrainers. These powerhouse antioxidants can minimize oxidative stress, cites Sylvia Klinger, DBA, MS, RDN, CPT, and founder of Hispanic Food Communications, and support several different organs in the body. The following are the four main advantages of coffee. 1. Brain and cognitive support It may not be surprising that coffee supports short-term cognition and concentration, but it is also likely neuroprotective in the long run, states Slayton. Klinger agrees: In addition to improving alertness, memory, and cognitive performance, coffee has been connected to a lower risk of depression and may safeguard against neurodegenerative diseases like Parkinson's and Alzheimer's, Klinger cites. 2. Heart health benefits Both experts agree that moderate coffee consumption is connected to a minimized risk of heart disease and stroke, as well as a lower risk of death from cardiac risks. This makes coffee a possible ally for cardiovascular wellness when consumed responsibly. 3. Liver protection Coffee is associated with a lower risk of liver disease, that includes fatty liver and cirrhosis, says Klinger. Coffee is also linked with a lower risk of liver and colorectal cancers, states Slayton, the latter is on the rise, mainly among young individuals. 4. Lower diabetes risk Studies indicate that coffee drinkers have a minimized risk of developing type 2 diabetes, says Klinger. Researchers believe this may be connected to coffee’s role in enhancing insulin sensitivity and reduce inflammation. How much coffee is ideal? Both nutritionists advise moderate coffee consumption (no more than 400 milligrams of caffeine everyday), though Slayton notes that even one cup can cause jitters in some of her clients. "It’s a matter of tolerance, and there’s individuality,” she states. Klinger suggests three or four cups, or approximately 300 to 400 milligrams of caffeine, in order to increase the health benefits while reducing significant risks. A meta-analysis of the beneficial health impacts of coffee intake published in BMJ concludes that "Coffee consumption seems generally safe within usual levels of intake, with summary estimates indicating the largest risk reduction for various health outcomes at three to four cups a day, and more likely to benefit health than harm." What you add matters "Coffee, in itself and unadulterated, has benefits," states Slayton. "Sugar, processed oils, and sweeteners do not." Slayton advises keeping additions minimal, indicating a splash of organic milk if you need something beyond black coffee. Klinger warns that additional fat, calories, and sugar can rapidly turn coffee into a high-calorie drink, elevating the risk of weight gain. She prefers dairy-free milks like almond, oat, coconut, cashew, soy, and lactose-free dairy milk, along with natural flavorings like cinnamon, vanilla or almond extract, unsweetened cocoa powder, stevia, monkfruit, or small amounts of honey or maple syrup. She also advises cold brew coffee for those sensitive to acidity, as it has a smoother, naturally sweeter taste. Timing and digestion tips To avoid sleep disturbances, both experts suggest drinking coffee earlier in the day. “Coffee has a long half-life,” describes Slayton. "It stays in our system longer than most people think. I suggest that clients discontinue coffee after midday." Klinger recommends a sweet spot of 9:30 to 11:30 a.m. for optimal energy without interrupting cortisol rhythms and also advises coffee before workouts to enhance endurance. She warns against drinking coffee on an empty stomach, which may irritate the stomach lining, result in bloating, or worsen acid reflux. Having a small breakfast initially can help, and she also recommends avoiding coffee right before meals because it can interfere with iron absorption. Does coffee hydrate you? “Coffee does count toward hydration and is not as dehydrating as previously believed,” states Klinger. "While caffeine has a mild diuretic effect, making you urinate more, the water content in coffee more than compensates for this effect." That said, experts still highlight the significance of drinking plain water throughout the day for overall wellness. When enjoyed mindfully, coffee can be more than just a routine, it can be a beneficial component of a balanced lifestyle. For most people, moderate daily coffee intake can be part of a healthy diet. The key is staying within advised caffeine limits. Q2. How much coffee is considered moderate?

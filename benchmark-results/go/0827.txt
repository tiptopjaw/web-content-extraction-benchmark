The key is to choose a multivitamin in a form you’ll take consistently, since daily use will provide you with the best results. Cost Multivitamins are typically taken every day, and can be pricey, so you may want to consider your budget when selecting your multivitamin. Cleaner formulations, meaning those without artificial sweeteners or additives, may cost more because of their higher quality and ingredient sourcing. Third-Party Testing Third-party testing independently verifies the safety, purity, and label accuracy of multivitamins since the FDA doesn’t regulate supplements. This process helps protect you from mislabeling, contamination, and banned substances, building trust and ensuring you can use products with confidence. Independent testing is essential for confirming product quality and safeguarding your health. Third-party certifications are especially crucial if you’re in the market for a fat burner supplement or testosterone booster, for instance. Benefits of Multivitamins Taking a multivitamin daily can support your overall health by filling nutritional gaps and promoting key functions in your body. Strong bones: Nutrients like vitamin D and calcium in multivitamins can help maintain bone density and may reduce the risk of osteoporosis Immune health: Vitamins C, D, and zinc support the immune system’s ability to fight off infections Cognitive health: B vitamins, particularly B6, B12, and folate, play a role in supporting memory and brain function as we age Overall energy: Iron and B vitamins help convert food into energy, which may reduce fatigue and improve daily stamina. How to Take Multivitamins When taking a multivitamin, it’s important to follow the dosage instructions on the product label or the guidance provided by your doctor. Taking higher-than-recommended amounts of certain vitamins and minerals can lead to side effects or even health risks over time. Consistency is key, so choose a time of day that works best for you and make it part of your routine. It also helps to know whether the vitamins in your supplement are water- or fat-soluble. Water-soluble vitamins like vitamin C and the B-complex family, dissolve in water and are best absorbed on an empty stomach, though they need to be replenished daily since the body does not store them. Fat-soluble vitamins, such as A, D, E, and K, are absorbed more effectively when taken with a meal that contains some healthy fat. Pairing your multivitamin with food can therefore improve its overall benefits. It’s also important to consider possible medication interactions. For example, vitamin K can interfere with the effectiveness of blood thinner medications such as warfarin, and high doses of other nutrients may also impact how certain prescriptions work. If you’re taking any medications or have a health condition, it’s best to talk with your doctor before starting or changing a multivitamin routine to ensure safety and effectiveness. What to Avoid in Multivitamins While multivitamins can be a great way to support your health, there are certain things you’ll want to avoid when choosing one: Excessive nutrient levels: Very high doses of certain vitamins or minerals, like vitamin A or iron, can be harmful if you don’t actually need them. “The side effects of multivitamins are generally mild and include nausea, upset stomach, and constipation,” Jillian Robillard, MS, RD, LDN said. In some cases, there may be more serious side effects or toxicity with excessive intake, particularly with certain vitamins and minerals that are fat soluble and stored in the body’s fat and liver. Unnecessary fillers or additives: Some products contain artificial colors, sweeteners, or preservatives that don’t add nutritional value. Unverified quality: Supplements that aren’t third-party tested may have inaccurate labeling or contain contaminants, so it’s best to choose brands that prioritize transparency and safety.

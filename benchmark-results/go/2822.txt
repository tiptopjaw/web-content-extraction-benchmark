Texas A&M students unveil AI robotic dog designed to navigate disaster zones COLLEGE STATION, Texas — Texas A&M University engineering students have developed an AI-powered robotic dog that can navigate disaster zones using memory and voice commands. The four-legged robot uses camera input and artificial intelligence to remember previously traveled paths, identify objects and respond to voice commands, according to the university. The memory-based system allows it to navigate efficiently without repeating exploration of the same areas. Sandun Vitharana, a master's student in engineering technology, and Sanjaya Mallikarachchi, a doctoral student in interdisciplinary engineering, led the project with support from the National Science Foundation. "The core of our vision is deploying MLLM at the edge, which gives our robotic dog the immediate, high-level situational awareness," said Isuru Godage, an assistant professor who advised the project. Beyond emergency response, potential applications include navigation assistance for people with visual impairments, warehouse operations and reconnaissance in hazardous areas, researchers said. The students presented the robot at the 22nd International Conference on Ubiquitous Robots. Their research was published in "A Walk to Remember: MLLM Memory-Driven Visual Navigation."
